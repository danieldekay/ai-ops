### Empathy Mapping
**Category:** UX Research / Customer Discovery
**Tags:** user empathy, persona, discovery synthesis, collaborative, says-thinks-does-feels
**How it works:** The Empathy Map is a collaborative visualization tool, popularized by Dave Gray and widely used in Design Thinking practice, that captures what a specific user persona says, thinks, does, and feels within a defined context or journey stage. Originally a four-quadrant tool (Says, Thinks, Does, Feels), updated versions also include "Sees," "Hears," and capture Pains and Gains at the bottom. Teams populate the map collaboratively using research artifacts — interview quotes, observation notes, survey data — to build a rich, shared understanding of the user's perspective. Empathy Maps are typically created early in a design or discovery process to align teams on the human they are designing for before solution ideation begins.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Says — direct quotes and statements from the user in interviews or interactions
- Thinks — what the user believes but may not verbalize; underlying motivations and concerns
- Does — observable behaviors and actions the user takes
- Feels — emotional states; frustrations, anxieties, aspirations
- Sees — the environment, market, and information the user is exposed to
- Hears — influences from peers, media, and authority figures
- Pains — obstacles and frustrations (maps to Value Proposition Canvas)
- Gains — desired outcomes and success measures
**Guiding questions:**
- What are users actually saying in their own words, and what do those words reveal?
- What do we believe users think but would never directly express?
- What behaviors have we directly observed that differ from what users say?
- What emotional undercurrents are present in user research data?
- Where do user pains and gains most sharply contradict our existing assumptions?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Grounding of each quadrant in actual research data, not assumptions
- Distinctness and specificity of the persona context
- Team alignment after collaborative mapping exercise
- Discovery of insights not previously visible to the team
- Use of empathy map to directly inform problem framing and ideation
**Typical use cases:**
- Synthesizing qualitative user research into actionable insights
- Onboarding teams to user perspective before sprint ideation
- Persona development and validation
- Cross-functional alignment workshops at discovery kickoff
**Strengths and limitations:** Empathy Maps are fast, visual, and accessible to non-researchers, making them effective for building user empathy across diverse teams. They surface emotional dimensions often missed by purely task-focused research methods. Their limitation is that without rigorous research input they become repositories of team assumptions rather than user truths, creating false confidence in a persona that reflects designers more than users.
**Related frameworks:** User Persona, Customer Journey Map, Value Proposition Canvas, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Affinity Mapping, Service Blueprint

### Service Blueprint
**Category:** Service Design
**Tags:** service design, frontstage, backstage, support processes, line of visibility, touchpoints
**How it works:** Developed by Lynn Shostack and refined by service design practitioners, the Service Blueprint extends the Customer Journey Map by simultaneously documenting both the customer-facing (frontstage) experience and the organizational processes, systems, and actors (backstage) that support each customer interaction. The blueprint is organized across horizontal swim lanes separated by three key lines: the Line of Interaction (between customer and front-line staff), the Line of Visibility (separating visible from invisible actions), and the Line of Internal Interaction (separating front-line from support processes). This dual visibility makes the blueprint uniquely powerful for identifying where backstage failures surface as frontstage pain, and for designing service innovations that require cross-functional organizational change.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Customer Actions — what the customer does throughout the service journey
- Frontstage (Onstage) Actions — visible employee or automated actions the customer perceives
- Line of Visibility — the boundary between visible and invisible service components
- Backstage Actions — employee actions invisible to the customer but necessary for service delivery
- Support Processes — internal systems, technology, and processes enabling backstage actions
- Physical Evidence — tangible artifacts the customer encounters (receipts, apps, signage)
- Line of Internal Interaction — boundary between direct service staff and support functions
**Guiding questions:**
- Where do backstage failures most directly cause frontstage customer pain?
- Which support processes are the greatest sources of service delivery variability?
- What physical evidence do customers encounter, and how does it shape their perception?
- Where could automation of backstage actions improve consistency and reduce cost?
- Which backstage processes require cross-departmental coordination that is currently failing?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Accuracy of backstage process documentation (validated with operations teams)
- Visibility of all failure points and their frontstage consequences
- Cross-functional sign-off from all departments featured in the blueprint
- Identification of specific improvement opportunities with owners assigned
- Feasibility of proposed backstage changes within organizational constraints
**Typical use cases:**
- Designing or redesigning complex multi-channel services
- Identifying root causes of inconsistent service quality
- Onboarding new service providers or vendors into a service system
- Coordinating digital and physical channel integration
**Strengths and limitations:** Service Blueprints are uniquely effective at exposing the organizational complexity behind customer-facing experiences and driving system-level service innovation. They are essential for services involving multiple departments or external partners. Their main limitation is the significant effort required to document backstage processes accurately, and they can become overwhelming in complexity for very large service systems.
**Related frameworks:** Customer Journey Map, Systems Map, Iceberg Model, Empathy Map, Double Diamond, Contextual Inquiry

### Affinity Mapping
**Category:** UX Research / Ideation
**Tags:** synthesis, KJ method, sticky notes, clustering, qualitative research, pattern recognition
**How it works:** Affinity Mapping (also called the KJ Method after its creator Jiro Kawakita) is a collaborative synthesis technique that groups large volumes of qualitative data — research observations, interview insights, user feedback, brainstorm outputs — into emergent thematic clusters based on natural affinities rather than predetermined categories. Teams write individual insights on separate sticky notes, silently sort them into groups, label each group with a header capturing its theme, and then cluster headers into higher-order themes. The process externalizes collective knowledge, surfaces patterns invisible when data is examined individually, and builds shared understanding across a team. It is used in UX research synthesis, ideation workshops, retrospectives, and strategic planning sessions.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Data points — individual observations, quotes, or ideas on separate notes
- Silent sorting — individual grouping without discussion to reduce social influence
- Emergent clusters — groups formed by natural affinity, not predetermined categories
- Cluster headers — thematic labels capturing the essence of each group
- Higher-order themes — second-level grouping of cluster headers
- Dot voting — prioritization mechanism applied to clusters or headers
**Guiding questions:**
- What patterns are we seeing across multiple independent data points?
- Do any clusters surprise us by contradicting our existing assumptions?
- Which clusters contain the highest density of pain points or unmet needs?
- Are there isolated data points that don't fit any cluster, and what do they signal?
- How do identified themes map to our current product or strategy assumptions?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Volume and diversity of input data before mapping begins
- Independence of sorting (silent phase quality)
- Appropriateness and specificity of cluster header language
- Connection of thematic outputs to clear design or strategy implications
- Time from mapping to actionable decisions
**Typical use cases:**
- Synthesizing qualitative user interview data into design insights
- Organizing brainstorming outputs into thematic opportunity areas
- Sprint retrospective analysis
- Customer feedback and support ticket analysis ([CPO Club](https://cpoclub.com/product-development/design-frameworks/))
**Strengths and limitations:** Affinity Mapping democratizes synthesis by making the process participatory and visible, building shared team ownership of insights. It handles large volumes of qualitative data efficiently and often reveals unexpected patterns. Its limitation is that cluster quality depends heavily on the richness and representativeness of input data, and the silent sorting phase requires facilitation discipline to prevent premature convergence.
**Related frameworks:** Empathy Mapping, Customer Journey Mapping, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Contextual Inquiry, How-Might-We Statements, Thematic Analysis

### How-Might-We Statements
**Category:** Ideation / Design Thinking
**Tags:** problem framing, IDEO, ideation prompt, reframing, creativity, open-ended
**How it works:** How-Might-We (HMW) statements are ideation prompts developed at Procter & Gamble and widely adopted by IDEO and the Stanford d.school. They transform problem observations and pain points into open-ended, generative questions that inspire creative ideation without implying a specific solution. The "How" word implies solutions exist. "Might" removes the pressure of finding the single correct answer. "We" establishes collaborative ownership. HMW statements are derived directly from the Define phase of Design Thinking — from a Point of View (POV) statement or insight — and are typically generated in large numbers before being prioritized for ideation sessions. They are used as workshop prompts, brainstorm launchers, and framing tools in creative problem-solving.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Problem reframing — converting a pain point or insight into an opportunity question
- HMW syntax — "How might we [verb] [user need/outcome] in/while [context]?"
- Scope calibration — too narrow (one solution implied) vs. too broad (unfocused) HMWs
- Prioritization — selecting the most generative HMWs before ideation begins
- Ideation trigger — HMW as the input prompt to brainstorming or sketching sessions
- Laddering — moving up (more abstract) or down (more specific) the HMW spectrum
**Guiding questions:**
- Does this HMW suggest a solution, or does it leave room for many creative directions?
- Is the HMW grounded in a real and validated user need or observation?
- Is it scoped appropriately — not so broad as to be meaningless, not so narrow as to prescribe a solution?
- Which HMW, if answered well, would have the greatest impact on user experience?
- Can we generate 20+ HMW statements from this single insight?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Derivation from validated research rather than team assumptions
- Appropriate scope (generative but bounded)
- Number and diversity of ideas generated per HMW in ideation
- Connection between chosen HMWs and defined user challenges
- Ratio of eventually implemented solutions that trace back to HMW framing
**Typical use cases:**
- Launching ideation phases in design sprints and workshops
- Reframing customer complaints or NPS detractor feedback ([CPO Club](https://cpoclub.com/product-development/design-frameworks/))
- Generating diverse solution concepts for complex UX problems
- Strategic opportunity framing at product leadership offsites
**Strengths and limitations:** HMW statements are low-effort, high-yield facilitation tools that can transform stuck problem frames into generative creative territory. They are especially powerful in workshops with non-designers who find open-ended ideation uncomfortable without a structured prompt. Their limitation is that poorly constructed HMWs — either too broad or too prescriptive — can derail ideation sessions, and the technique requires strong facilitation to balance divergence with focus.
**Related frameworks:** Design Thinking, Point of View Statement, Brainstorming, Crazy 8s, Affinity Mapping, Design Sprint

### Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)
**Category:** Product Discovery / Continuous Discovery
**Tags:** Teresa Torres, continuous discovery, opportunity mapping, solutions, experiments
**How it works:** The Opportunity Solution Tree was developed by product discovery coach Teresa Torres as a visual framework for connecting product outcomes to discovery opportunities, potential solutions, and experiments. The tree starts with a desired outcome (the product goal) at the root. Teams then map the opportunity space — the customer needs, pain points, and desires that, if addressed, would achieve the outcome. Each opportunity branches into potential solutions, and each solution branches into experiments designed to test assumptions. The OST structure prevents teams from anchoring too quickly on a single solution and ensures that solution exploration is always tied to a specific opportunity derived from customer research. It makes the full discovery work visible and navigable.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Outcome — the desired product goal or OKR the team is pursuing
- Opportunity space — the universe of customer needs, pain points, and desires discovered through research
- Opportunities — specific customer needs that, if addressed, would contribute to the outcome
- Solutions — possible ways to address a given opportunity
- Experiments — tests designed to validate assumptions about solutions
- Assumption mapping — identifying the riskiest beliefs underlying each solution
**Guiding questions:**
- Is this opportunity truly derived from customer research, or is it a solution in disguise?
- Are we exploring multiple opportunities simultaneously before committing to one?
- For each solution, what is the riskiest assumption that could invalidate it?
- What is the smallest experiment that would test that assumption?
- Does every solution in the tree trace back to a validated customer opportunity?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Breadth of opportunity space explored before committing to solutions
- Grounding of opportunities in customer research (interviews, observations)
- Quality and speed of assumption-testing experiments
- Avoidance of "solution attachment" — pruning the tree based on evidence
- Connection between opportunity importance and outcome contribution
**Typical use cases:**
- Continuous discovery practice in mature product teams
- Replacing requirements documents with a living discovery artifact
- Managing exploration of complex product problem spaces
- Aligning discovery and delivery activities within sprint cycles
**Strengths and limitations:** The OST is uniquely effective at preventing premature solution commitment and at making the full scope of discovery work visible to teams and stakeholders. It integrates discovery and delivery rather than separating them. Its challenge is that populating the tree rigorously requires frequent customer contact (Torres recommends weekly interviews), which demands organizational discipline. Teams new to continuous discovery may find the tree overwhelming to build and maintain.
**Related frameworks:** Continuous Discovery, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Lean Startup, Assumption Mapping, Now–Next–Later Roadmap, Design Thinking

### Continuous Discovery
**Category:** Product Discovery / UX Research
**Tags:** Teresa Torres, weekly interviews, product trio, automated testing, discovery rhythm
**How it works:** Continuous Discovery is a product development practice codified by Teresa Torres that calls for product trios — a product manager, designer, and engineer — to conduct at least one customer interview per week every week, generating a continuous stream of qualitative insight that informs weekly product decisions. Rather than treating discovery as a phase that precedes delivery, continuous discovery makes research a permanent rhythm embedded into the team's weekly cadence. The Opportunity Solution Tree serves as the primary synthesis artifact. Automated recruiting pipelines remove the scheduling friction that prevents consistent interviewing. The practice shifts teams from episodic, project-based research to an ongoing learning system.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Product Trio — PM, designer, and engineer sharing discovery work together
- Weekly interview cadence — minimum one customer interview per week
- Automated recruiting — systematic pipeline for finding and scheduling participants
- Opportunity Solution Tree — living synthesis artifact connecting research to roadmap
- Assumption testing — rapid experiments to validate solution assumptions
- Story-based interviews — techniques for eliciting specific behavioral episodes rather than opinions
**Guiding questions:**
- Is the full product trio participating in customer interviews, not just the researcher?
- Do we have an automated recruiting pipeline that eliminates scheduling as an excuse?
- Are we interviewing the right customer segments to answer current strategic questions?
- How is weekly learning updating the Opportunity Solution Tree?
- Are we distinguishing between customer opinions and specific behavioral observations?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Consistency of weekly interview cadence (no skipped weeks)
- Diversity and representativeness of interview participants
- Quality of story-based interview facilitation
- Integration of insights into weekly roadmap and prioritization decisions
- Reduction in "we assume customers want this" language in product decisions
**Typical use cases:**
- Mature product teams establishing a durable discovery-delivery rhythm
- Organizations seeking to reduce product bets that miss the market
- Product trios building shared customer understanding without siloed research teams
**Strengths and limitations:** Continuous Discovery replaces episodic research with systemic organizational learning, dramatically reducing the gap between customer reality and product decisions. It creates shared customer empathy across the full product trio. Its greatest limitation is organizational inertia — scheduling weekly interviews consistently is harder than it sounds, and trio commitment to research can be crowded out by delivery pressures without strong leadership support.
**Related frameworks:** Opportunity Solution Tree, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Contextual Inquiry, Lean Startup, Design Sprint, User Interview Frameworks

### Contextual Inquiry
**Category:** UX Research
**Tags:** ethnographic research, field study, observation, Karen Holtzblatt, qualitative, naturalistic
**How it works:** Contextual Inquiry is a qualitative UX research method developed by Karen Holtzblatt and Hugh Beyer, described in their book *Contextual Design*. Researchers visit users in their natural work or life contexts, observing them perform real tasks while conducting a semi-structured interview simultaneously. The four guiding principles are Context (research occurs in the user's actual environment), Partnership (researcher and user collaborate as master and apprentice), Interpretation (researcher shares observations and inferences with the user for immediate correction), and Focus (the session is directed by a pre-defined set of research questions). Contextual Inquiry surfaces tacit, embodied knowledge that users cannot articulate in decontextualized interviews — the workarounds, shortcuts, and environmental factors that define actual usage.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Context — research conducted in user's natural environment
- Partnership — collaborative inquiry with users as the expert on their own practice
- Interpretation — researcher shares understanding for real-time user validation
- Focus — session guided by research themes defined before fieldwork
- Observation notes — detailed documentation of actions, artifacts, and environment
- Follow-up questions — probing the "why" behind observed behaviors
**Guiding questions:**
- What are users actually doing in their environment that we cannot capture in a lab?
- What workarounds and improvisations have users developed that reveal design gaps?
- What artifacts (spreadsheets, paper notes, Post-its) are users creating to compensate for product limitations?
- What environmental factors (interruptions, multiple screens, social dynamics) influence usage patterns?
- Where does our interpretation of a behavior match or diverge from the user's own explanation?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Fidelity of observation context to actual use environment
- Depth of implicit knowledge surfaced through the apprentice model
- Quality and accuracy of interpretation sessions
- Breadth of task types and user profiles observed
- Translation of field data into design-relevant insights
**Typical use cases:**
- Enterprise software design where usage context is complex and specialized
- Healthcare product research where workflow integration is critical
- Industrial or physical-digital hybrid product research
- Uncovering undiscovered user behaviors not captured by analytics
**Strengths and limitations:** Contextual Inquiry reveals hidden behaviors and environmental factors that no other research method can access, producing breakthrough design insights. The partnership model ensures that researcher interpretations are immediately validated, reducing bias. Its limitations are significant time and resource investment per session, geographic constraints for distributed user bases, and analysis complexity when synthesizing observational data.
**Related frameworks:** Ethnographic Research, Empathy Mapping, Customer Journey Mapping, Affinity Mapping, Think-Aloud Protocol, Diary Studies

### Think-Aloud Protocol
**Category:** UX Research
**Tags:** usability testing, verbal protocol, cognitive walkthrough, Jakob Nielsen, formative testing
**How it works:** The Think-Aloud Protocol is a usability evaluation method in which participants verbalize their thoughts, expectations, decisions, and reactions as they interact with a product or prototype. Developed in cognitive psychology and popularized in UX by Jacob Nielsen and colleagues, the method provides direct access to users' mental models, confusion points, and decision logic — information that task completion rates and click data cannot reveal. In concurrent think-aloud, participants verbalize during the task. In retrospective think-aloud, they narrate while reviewing a recording of their session. The researcher's role is to prompt verbalization without leading the participant, using phrases like "what are you thinking right now?" rather than evaluative questions.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Concurrent think-aloud — verbalizing during task performance
- Retrospective think-aloud — narrating over a session recording after task completion
- Task scenarios — realistic tasks participants are asked to complete
- Probing prompts — researcher cues to maintain verbalization without leading
- Observation notes — documentation of hesitations, errors, and emotional signals
- Session recording — audio, video, and screen capture for analysis
**Guiding questions:**
- Are participants narrating their thinking or narrating what they are doing?
- Where do verbalizations cluster around hesitation, confusion, or surprise?
- Do users' stated mental models match the system model embedded in the design?
- What expectations are being violated at key interaction points?
- Are the differences between concurrent and retrospective think-aloud revealing post-hoc rationalization?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Verbalization rate (are participants narrating consistently throughout?)
- Quality of probing prompts (not leading, not disruptive)
- Density of actionable usability insights per session
- Diversity of task scenarios relative to core use cases
- Translation of think-aloud findings into specific design changes
**Typical use cases:**
- Formative usability testing of prototypes during iterative design
- Identifying specific interaction points causing user confusion
- Evaluating information architecture and navigation structures
- Comparing competing design concepts on cognitive load
**Strengths and limitations:** Think-Aloud provides unmatched insight into user mental models and decision logic, explaining the "why" behind observed behaviors. It requires only 5 participants to reveal 85% of usability issues (per Nielsen's rule). Its limitation is that verbalizing while performing tasks is cognitively unnatural and can alter task performance. Retrospective think-aloud removes this concern but introduces memory-based rationalization. Both variants require skilled facilitation.
**Related frameworks:** Usability Testing, Cognitive Walkthrough, Heuristic Evaluation, Contextual Inquiry, A/B Testing, Diary Studies

### User Story Mapping
**Category:** Product Management / UX Research
**Tags:** Jeff Patton, backbone, activities, tasks, releases, backlog, narrative
**How it works:** User Story Mapping, developed by Jeff Patton, organizes product requirements into a two-dimensional map that preserves the narrative of user experience rather than flattening all work into a priority-ordered flat list. The horizontal axis represents user activities in chronological journey order — the backbone of the map — and the vertical axis represents increasing detail and specificity under each activity. The backbone (activities and tasks) tells the full story of the user experience; the vertical dimension (stories) represents work that can be sliced into releases. Teams use the map to define minimum viable releases by drawing horizontal "slices" that deliver end-to-end user value even with limited scope — rather than building full depth in one area before moving to the next.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Activities — high-level user goals representing major journey stages (backbone top level)
- Tasks — specific steps users take to complete each activity (backbone lower level)
- User Stories — detailed work items underlying each task
- Release slices — horizontal cuts across the map defining minimum viable experiences
- Backbone — the narrative spine of user activities and tasks
- Narrative flow — left-to-right temporal ordering of user experience
**Guiding questions:**
- Does this map tell the complete story of the user experience from start to finish?
- Where do our current release boundaries cut the user journey in ways that deliver incomplete experiences?
- Which combination of stories creates the smallest end-to-end valuable experience?
- Are there activities in the backbone that represent user work we could eliminate rather than support?
- How does the map reveal work that benefits some user segments but not others?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness and accuracy of the user narrative backbone
- Identification of minimum viable experience slices
- Explicit representation of multiple user segments or personas
- Alignment between map structure and sprint or release planning
- Contribution to shared team understanding of the product vision
**Typical use cases:**
- Release and MVP scope planning for new products
- Backlog refinement and organization for complex products
- Stakeholder alignment on product scope and phasing
- Discovery and definition of end-to-end user experiences
**Strengths and limitations:** User Story Mapping restores narrative context to requirements that flat backlogs destroy, helping teams understand the "whole product" rather than optimizing individual features in isolation. It naturally identifies minimum viable experiences rather than minimum viable feature sets. Its limitation is that maps become very large for complex products and require regular maintenance to remain useful as priorities evolve.
**Related frameworks:** Agile/Scrum, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Continuous Discovery, Customer Journey Map, Now–Next–Later Roadmap, Opportunity Solution Tree

### Persona Development
**Category:** UX Research / Product Management
**Tags:** user archetypes, segments, goals, behaviors, scenario, empathy, targeting
**How it works:** Personas are fictional but research-grounded composite profiles of target user archetypes, introduced to the UX community by Alan Cooper in his 1999 book *The Inmates Are Running the Asylum*. They synthesize patterns observed across multiple real users into a named, described character who embodies the goals, behaviors, frustrations, technical fluency, and contextual factors typical of a segment. Well-constructed personas are based on qualitative research (interviews, contextual inquiry) augmented by quantitative data (analytics, surveys). They serve as decision-making tools — teams ask "Would Maya do this?" rather than debating whose opinion is correct — and as communication artifacts that keep user needs visible throughout product development.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Demographics — age, role, context, and background relevant to the product domain
- Goals — what the persona is trying to accomplish (functional, emotional, social)
- Behaviors — how the persona currently approaches the domain; tools, workflows, habits
- Pain points — frustrations, barriers, and failures with current solutions
- Mental models — how the persona conceptualizes the domain and related systems
- Scenarios — narrative descriptions of the persona using or needing the product
- Technology fluency — comfort with relevant technology categories
**Guiding questions:**
- Is this persona based on research patterns or stakeholder assumptions?
- What are the core goals that distinguish this persona from others?
- In what specific scenario does this persona most urgently need our product?
- How would this persona react to Feature X — would it serve their goals or create new friction?
- Are our personas divergent enough from each other to drive meaningfully different design decisions?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Research grounding in actual user interviews and observations
- Distinctiveness across personas (not merely demographic variations)
- Specificity of goals and behaviors relative to the product domain
- Usage frequency by the team in actual design and roadmap decisions
- Scheduled validation and update cadence
**Typical use cases:**
- Guiding feature prioritization and design decisions in product teams
- Targeting and messaging development in marketing
- Onboarding new team members to the user landscape
- Evaluating designs or prototypes against specific user needs
**Strengths and limitations:** Personas give non-researchers access to user-centered thinking by creating memorable, human characters that represent otherwise abstract data. They are especially powerful for maintaining user focus in organizations dominated by internal stakeholder opinions. Their well-documented limitation is the risk of "persona theater" — personas created and filed away, never consulted in actual decisions — and the reductiveness of collapsing human diversity into a few archetypes.
**Related frameworks:** Empathy Mapping, Contextual Inquiry, Customer Journey Map, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Value Proposition Canvas, Continuous Discovery

### Heuristic Evaluation
**Category:** UX Research / Usability
**Tags:** Jakob Nielsen, expert review, usability heuristics, interface evaluation, cognitive walkthrough
**How it works:** Heuristic Evaluation is a discount usability inspection method developed by Jakob Nielsen and Rolf Molich in the early 1990s, in which expert evaluators examine a user interface against a set of recognized usability principles (heuristics) to identify violations. Nielsen's ten heuristics — covering visibility of system status, match between system and real world, user control and freedom, consistency and standards, error prevention, recognition over recall, flexibility, aesthetics, error recovery, and help/documentation — provide the evaluation criteria. Evaluators work independently, then consolidate findings. The method requires 3–5 evaluators to surface 75–90% of usability problems without involving actual users, making it fast and cost-effective for identifying issues early in design.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Evaluator expertise — UX specialists familiar with the heuristics and domain
- Ten usability heuristics — Nielsen's canonical principles as the evaluation standard
- Severity rating — scoring each issue by frequency, impact, and persistence (0–4 scale)
- Independent evaluation — evaluators work separately before aggregating findings
- Debrief session — consolidation and discussion of all identified issues
- Issue list — documented violations with heuristic reference and severity score
**Guiding questions:**
- Which heuristics are most relevant to this specific interface or interaction type?
- How severely will each identified violation affect users — how frequently will they encounter it and how catastrophic is the failure?
- Which violations, if addressed, would have the highest impact per unit of remediation effort?
- Are there patterns of violations (e.g., consistent consistency failures) indicating systemic design problems?
- Which issues require user testing to validate, versus those clearly requiring remediation?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Number of distinct usability issues found per evaluator and in aggregate
- Severity distribution of identified issues
- Clarity of issue descriptions enabling actionable remediation
- Correlation with problems later identified in user testing
- Speed and cost efficiency relative to user-based testing methods
**Typical use cases:**
- Rapid assessment of prototype or existing product usability before user testing
- Design review milestone evaluation in product development cycles
- Competitive product usability benchmarking
- Accessibility and inclusive design audit
**Strengths and limitations:** Heuristic Evaluation is the fastest, lowest-cost method for identifying a broad range of usability issues without recruiting participants, making it ideal early in design cycles. It generates actionable, categorized findings. Its limitation is expert-user bias — evaluators identify issues they know to look for, and may miss context-specific problems that only emerge in realistic use. It should complement, not replace, user-based research.
**Related frameworks:** Think-Aloud Protocol, Cognitive Walkthrough, Usability Testing, Expert Review, Design Principles, Accessibility Audit

### A/B Testing (Controlled Experimentation)
**Category:** Experimentation / Product Analytics
**Tags:** hypothesis testing, statistical significance, conversion optimization, split test, causality
**How it works:** A/B testing (also called split testing or controlled experimentation) is the product experimentation method of randomly assigning users to two or more variants of a product experience and measuring outcome differences to establish causal relationships between design or feature changes and user behavior. Unlike correlational analytics, A/B testing isolates the effect of a single variable by holding all others constant across randomly split groups. The scientific workflow involves forming a falsifiable hypothesis, calculating required sample sizes for statistical power, running the experiment for a minimum duration to account for novelty effects and time-based behavior patterns, measuring primary and guardrail metrics, and making a data-driven ship/no-ship decision.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Control variant (A) — the existing experience or null condition
- Treatment variant(s) (B, C…) — the changed experience being tested
- Randomization unit — the entity randomly assigned (user, session, device)
- Primary metric — the key outcome the experiment is designed to influence
- Guardrail metrics — metrics that must not degrade during the test
- Statistical significance — the probability threshold for rejecting the null hypothesis (typically p < 0.05)
- Statistical power — the probability of detecting a true effect when one exists (typically 80%)
- Minimum detectable effect — the smallest change worth detecting for business impact
**Guiding questions:**
- What specific hypothesis are we testing, and what causal mechanism connects the change to the metric?
- Is our sample size sufficient to detect the minimum business-meaningful effect?
- Have we identified guardrail metrics to prevent harmful side effects from winning variants?
- Are we accounting for novelty effects and seasonal variation in experiment duration?
- What does a statistically significant result actually mean for deployment decisions?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Hypothesis specificity and falsifiability before experiment launch
- Proper randomization and absence of selection bias
- Achievement of required statistical power before analysis
- Absence of peeking or early stopping that inflates false positive rates
- Quality of post-experiment learning documentation and institutional memory
**Typical use cases:**
- Onboarding flow optimization to improve activation rates
- Pricing and packaging variant testing
- Search ranking and recommendation algorithm evaluation
- Copywriting and CTA testing in marketing and product interfaces
**Strengths and limitations:** A/B testing provides the strongest available evidence for causal relationships between product changes and user behavior, enabling confident, data-driven product decisions. It scales effectively with product infrastructure investment. Its limitations include the need for substantial traffic volumes to achieve statistical power, the inability to test radical innovations (users can only evaluate what is shown to them), and the risk of optimizing local metrics at the expense of long-term user value.
**Related frameworks:** Hypothesis-Driven Development, Lean Startup, Growth Loop Model, AARRR, Bayesian Experimentation, Feature Flagging

### Ten Types of Innovation (Doblin)
**Category:** Innovation / Strategy
**Tags:** Doblin, Deloitte, Larry Keeley, innovation dimensions, product system, business model
**How it works:** Developed by Larry Keeley and colleagues at Doblin (now part of Deloitte), the Ten Types of Innovation framework asserts that most companies over-invest in product performance innovation while leaving nine other innovation dimensions largely unexploited. The ten types are organized across three categories: Configuration (Profit Model, Network, Structure, Process), Offering (Product Performance, Product System), and Experience (Service, Channel, Brand, Customer Engagement). The framework argues that the most durable and defensible innovations combine multiple types simultaneously — competitors can copy a single product feature, but replicating an integrated constellation of innovation types across configuration, offering, and experience is far harder.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Profit Model — innovative ways the company captures value (revenue model innovation)
- Network — leveraging other companies' processes, technologies, or brand channels
- Structure — distinctive organization of assets and talent
- Process — proprietary methods that enable superior performance or lower cost
- Product Performance — novel features, functions, or performance characteristics
- Product System — complementary products and services that extend the core offering
- Service — support and enhancements surrounding the offering
- Channel — how the offering is delivered to customers and users
- Brand — distinctive identity, promise, and values
- Customer Engagement — interactions that foster connections and community
**Guiding questions:**
- Which of the ten types are we currently investing in, and which are completely unexplored?
- Which combination of innovation types would be hardest for competitors to replicate?
- Are we defaulting to product performance innovation when experience or configuration innovations might deliver more differentiation?
- Which innovation types most directly address identified customer jobs and pains?
- How many innovation types is our leading competitor simultaneously deploying?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Coverage of innovation investment across multiple types (not just product performance)
- Integration and coherence across selected innovation types
- Defensibility of innovation type combinations against competitive imitation
- Alignment of selected innovation types with customer job priorities
- Balance between near-term performance and long-term differentiation
**Typical use cases:**
- Strategic innovation portfolio assessment and rebalancing
- Competitive analysis to identify unexploited innovation dimensions
- Innovation strategy design at the business unit or enterprise level
- Identifying non-obvious differentiation opportunities in mature markets
**Strengths and limitations:** The Ten Types framework dramatically expands the aperture of innovation thinking beyond product features, often revealing high-value unexplored dimensions. It provides a common language for discussing innovation strategy across functions. Its main limitation is that the ten types can feel abstract without concrete examples in the team's specific industry context, and the framework provides limited guidance on how to generate specific innovations within each type.
**Related frameworks:** Blue Ocean Strategy, Innovation Ambition Matrix, Business Model Canvas, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Value Proposition Canvas, Jobs Innovation Theory

### Innovation Ambition Matrix
**Category:** Innovation / Strategy
**Tags:** Nagji and Tuff, core-adjacent-transformational, portfolio, risk, Horizon model
**How it works:** The Innovation Ambition Matrix, developed by Bansi Nagji and Geoff Tuff at Deloitte and published in *Harvard Business Review* (2012), is a portfolio framework that categorizes innovation investments along two dimensions: how far the initiative is from existing products and offerings, and how far it is from existing customers and markets. The three resulting zones — Core (optimizing existing offers for existing customers), Adjacent (expanding into near-neighbor markets or product spaces), and Transformational (developing breakthroughs for new markets) — help leadership allocate innovation investment with explicit risk-return expectations. Research cited by Nagji and Tuff suggests a portfolio weighting of approximately 70/20/10 (Core/Adjacent/Transformational) produces optimal total returns, though this ratio varies by industry and strategic context.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Core innovations — optimizations of existing products and markets; incremental, low-risk
- Adjacent innovations — leveraging existing strengths in new markets or product categories
- Transformational innovations — breakthrough capabilities targeting new-to-world markets
- Offer axis — proximity of the innovation to current product portfolio
- Market axis — proximity to current customer base
- Portfolio balance — explicit allocation percentages across the three zones
- Return profiles — expected return timelines and risk levels per zone
**Guiding questions:**
- What percentage of our current innovation investment falls into each zone?
- Are we over-investing in Core at the expense of long-term competitiveness?
- Which Adjacent opportunities leverage our strongest existing capabilities?
- What is our organization's genuine risk tolerance for Transformational bets?
- How do our portfolio allocations compare to industry leaders in innovation return?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Explicit articulation of intended portfolio allocation by zone
- Balance between short-term Core returns and long-term Transformational bets
- Alignment of Transformational investments with emerging market signals
- Return tracking across all three zones over multiple planning cycles
- Leadership commitment to protecting Transformational investments during performance pressure
**Typical use cases:**
- Corporate innovation portfolio review and rebalancing
- R&D investment allocation decisions
- Strategic planning for business units in mature industries
- Evaluating M&A and partnership targets by innovation zone
**Strengths and limitations:** The Innovation Ambition Matrix introduces portfolio thinking to innovation, preventing the common failure mode of allocating 100% of resources to Core optimization. It creates a language for discussing innovation risk tolerance explicitly. Its limitation is that zone boundaries are subjective, and the 70/20/10 ratio should be treated as a starting heuristic rather than a universal prescription.
**Related frameworks:** McKinsey Three Horizons, Ten Types of Innovation, Blue Ocean Strategy, Stage-Gate, Business Model Canvas, Technology Readiness Levels
