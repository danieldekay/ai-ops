### Porter's Five Forces
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** competitive analysis, industry structure, market dynamics, barriers to entry
**How it works:** Porter's Five Forces, developed by Michael Porter at Harvard Business School in 1979, provides a lens for analyzing the competitive structure and attractiveness of an industry by examining five external forces. Rather than focusing solely on direct rivals, the framework widens the competitive aperture to include potential entrants, substitutes, suppliers, and buyers. Practitioners identify each force, assess its current intensity as high, medium, or low, and then determine how that intensity affects long-term industry profitability. The output directs managers toward strategies—cost leadership, differentiation, or focus—that build defensible positions against whichever forces are strongest. It is most powerful when applied to a clearly bounded industry rather than broad sectors. According to [StrategyU](https://strategyu.co/consulting-frameworks/), the framework helps teams "look outward" at competitive dynamics before committing to strategic moves.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Threat of new entrants (economies of scale, capital requirements, brand loyalty, regulation)
- Bargaining power of suppliers (concentration, switching costs, input differentiation)
- Bargaining power of buyers (volume, price sensitivity, switching costs)
- Threat of substitute products or services (relative price-performance, switching propensity)
- Rivalry among existing competitors (market growth, product differentiation, exit barriers)
**Guiding questions:**
- How easily can new players enter this market, and what barriers exist?
- How much leverage do our key suppliers have over pricing and terms?
- How price-sensitive are our customers and how easily can they switch?
- Are substitute offerings closing the performance gap?
- How intense is current competitive rivalry and is it escalating?
- Which single force poses the greatest long-run threat to profitability?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Clarity and specificity of industry boundary definition
- Evidence-based rating of each force (high/medium/low)
- Identification of the primary profit-eroding force
- Quality of strategic responses proposed for each significant force
- Dynamic perspective: how forces are likely to evolve
**Typical use cases:**
- Assessing attractiveness of a new market before entry
- Evaluating acquisition targets within an industry
- Guiding long-term investment and capacity decisions
- Preparing investor materials or board strategy presentations
**Strengths and limitations:** Porter's Five Forces excels at focusing attention on structural—rather than tactical—determinants of profitability and at comparing industries objectively. Its static snapshot nature is its chief limitation: rapidly evolving digital markets can shift the five forces faster than a conventional analysis cycle. It also treats the industry as the unit of analysis, which can obscure firm-specific advantages or platform dynamics where complementors—notably absent from the original model—reshape competitive logic. A sixth-force extension (complementors) is sometimes added. Best used alongside PESTEL for macro context and paired with internal capability assessments. Source: [Hyper Island](https://hyperisland.com/en/blog/hyper-insights/top-7-strategic-frameworks-every-leader-should-know).
**Related frameworks:** PESTEL Analysis, BCG Growth–Share Matrix, SWOT Analysis, Value Chain Analysis, Ansoff Matrix

### SWOT Analysis
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** situational assessment, internal/external analysis, planning, strengths, weaknesses
**How it works:** SWOT Analysis organizes an organization's strategic position into four quadrants: internal Strengths and Weaknesses, and external Opportunities and Threats. Originating in Stanford Research Institute work during the 1960s and popularized in subsequent decades, it is among the most widely used planning tools globally. The process typically begins with an internal audit—what capabilities, resources, or advantages does the firm genuinely own?—then shifts to an external environmental scan. The real analytical value emerges when teams cross-map quadrants: SO strategies leverage strengths to seize opportunities; ST strategies use strengths to neutralize threats; WO strategies address weaknesses that prevent opportunity capture; WT strategies minimize both vulnerabilities and external dangers. As noted by [Hyper Island](https://hyperisland.com/en/blog/hyper-insights/top-7-strategic-frameworks-every-leader-should-know), SWOT works best as a team conversation catalyst in strategic offsites to surface unvoiced knowledge before building a formal plan.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Strengths: internal assets, capabilities, brand equity, proprietary technology
- Weaknesses: internal gaps, resource constraints, skill deficits, legacy systems
- Opportunities: market gaps, regulatory tailwinds, unmet customer needs, technological shifts
- Threats: competitive moves, economic headwinds, regulatory risk, substitution
**Guiding questions:**
- Which internal capabilities are difficult for competitors to replicate?
- Where do internal gaps regularly limit performance?
- What external developments could be captured if resources were redirected?
- Which threats are most likely to materialize within the planning horizon?
- How can specific strengths be deployed against identified threats?
- Are there weaknesses that make the firm disproportionately vulnerable to a particular threat?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Honesty and specificity of internal assessment (avoiding generic statements)
- Breadth of external scanning sources
- Quality of cross-quadrant (SO/ST/WO/WT) strategic combinations
- Prioritization by likelihood and impact magnitude
**Typical use cases:**
- Annual strategic planning workshops
- Business unit review and resource allocation
- Pre-launch product or market assessment
- Leadership team alignment sessions
**Strengths and limitations:** SWOT's simplicity is its greatest asset: virtually any team can populate the four quadrants quickly, generating shared situational understanding. Its main weakness is a tendency toward completeness over rigor—long laundry lists replace prioritized insights. The framework is descriptive, not prescriptive; it surfaces what exists but does not automatically indicate what to do. Without systematic cross-quadrant analysis, teams may stop at the list stage and miss the strategic synthesis. Supplement with quantitative weighting, scenario analysis, or PESTEL to sharpen output. It is best used as a conversation starter, not a decision-making tool in isolation.
**Related frameworks:** PESTEL Analysis, Porter's Five Forces, Ansoff Matrix, McKinsey 7-S Framework, Balanced Scorecard

### PESTEL Analysis
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** macro-environment, external scanning, regulatory, political, economic, technology
**How it works:** PESTEL (sometimes PEST or PESTLE) is a structured macro-environmental scanning framework that catalogues external forces shaping an industry or market. Developed from early PEST frameworks in academic strategy literature, it systematically covers Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Legal dimensions. Practitioners gather evidence for each dimension—reviewing regulatory pipelines, economic forecasts, demographic studies, emerging technologies, climate transition policies, and legal precedents—and then assess the implications for the organization's strategy and risk profile. The goal is not to list every conceivable fact but to identify the forces most material to strategic decisions in the relevant time horizon. [Hyper Island](https://hyperisland.com/en/blog/hyper-insights/top-7-strategic-frameworks-every-leader-should-know) recommends pairing PESTEL with Porter's Five Forces when entering new geographies, using it for risk mapping and early-stage trend analysis. The six dimensions form a comprehensive scan of the macro-environment that informs both strategic direction and risk management priorities.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Political: government stability, trade policy, tax regime, regulation, geopolitics
- Economic: growth rates, inflation, interest rates, exchange rates, employment
- Social: demographics, cultural shifts, lifestyle trends, education levels
- Technological: R&D intensity, automation, digital adoption, disruptive innovation
- Environmental: climate regulation, resource scarcity, carbon markets, ESG expectations
- Legal: labor law, IP protection, data privacy regulation, antitrust rules
**Guiding questions:**
- Which political developments could disrupt current operating assumptions within three years?
- How are economic cycles expected to affect demand and cost structures?
- What demographic or behavioral shifts are altering the customer base?
- Which technologies are likely to compress current competitive advantages?
- What environmental regulations are likely to impose costs or open opportunities?
- Are there legal changes that require proactive compliance investment?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Relevance and materiality of identified factors to the specific business
- Evidence quality supporting each dimension
- Forward-looking horizon (not just current state)
- Integration of findings into strategic implications
- Differentiation between factors that are merely interesting and those that are decision-relevant
**Typical use cases:**
- New market or geography entry assessment
- Scenario planning for long-range strategy
- Investment due diligence
- Risk and resilience planning workshops
**Strengths and limitations:** PESTEL's breadth ensures that teams avoid blind spots from focusing only on competitive or internal factors. However, its comprehensiveness can become a weakness—exhaustive lists of marginally relevant factors can dilute focus. The framework is descriptive and retrospective if not deliberately oriented toward forward-looking signals. It also does not prioritize or quantify forces. Best used in conjunction with Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, or scenario planning to convert raw environmental data into strategic choices. The framework does not inherently prioritize which factors matter most, so practitioners must apply judgment to determine which dimensions pose the greatest strategic significance for their specific context and time horizon.
**Related frameworks:** Porter's Five Forces, SWOT Analysis, Scenario Planning, Country Risk Assessment, AFI Strategy Framework

### Ansoff Matrix
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** growth strategy, market development, product development, diversification
**How it works:** The Ansoff Matrix, published by Igor Ansoff in the Harvard Business Review in 1957, maps growth strategy options along two axes: whether the product is existing or new, and whether the target market is existing or new. This produces four strategic postures: Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets), Product Development (new products, existing markets), Market Development (existing products, new markets), and Diversification (new products, new markets). Risk escalates as firms move from penetration toward diversification. Practitioners use the matrix to align growth ambitions with risk appetite and resource availability. A firm with scarce capital should concentrate penetration efforts; one with strong R&D may pursue product development. According to [StrategyU](https://strategyu.co/consulting-frameworks/), evaluating each quadrant's potential returns and risks—and matching them to company resources—is the critical leveraging step.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Market Penetration: increase share in current markets with current products
- Product Development: create new products for existing customer base
- Market Development: take existing products into new geographies or segments
- Diversification: pursue new products in new markets (related or unrelated)
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the maximum achievable share in our current market, and what would capture it cost?
- Do our existing customers have unmet needs that new products could address?
- Are there adjacent geographies or customer segments accessible with minimal product change?
- Does the case for diversification justify the risk and organizational distraction?
- How does our risk tolerance and capital base constrain quadrant choice?
- Which growth path aligns most naturally with existing capabilities?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Quantification of market size and penetration headroom in each quadrant
- Honest risk assessment per strategic option
- Alignment between chosen strategy and resource base
- Competitive dynamics within the target quadrant
- Timeline realism for execution
**Typical use cases:**
- Corporate growth strategy planning
- Business unit portfolio allocation decisions
- New product or market investment evaluation
- Post-merger integration growth planning
**Strengths and limitations:** The Ansoff Matrix offers an immediately actionable strategic choice set and makes explicit the risk trade-offs between organic growth paths. Its limitation is the binary framing of "existing vs. new" for both products and markets, which masks gradations of novelty. It also does not address competitive reaction, channel complexity, or capability building timelines needed for execution. Combine with Porter's Five Forces (competitive context), McKinsey 7-S (capability alignment), and financial modeling to build a complete growth case. The matrix also does not account for execution capability or competitive response intensity, both of which significantly shape whether any growth strategy delivers its projected returns.
**Related frameworks:** BCG Growth–Share Matrix, Porter's Five Forces, Blue Ocean Strategy, Market Entry Framework, GE–McKinsey Matrix

### AFI Strategy Framework
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** strategic management, analysis, formulation, implementation, consulting
**How it works:** The AFI (Analyze, Formulate, Implement) Strategy Framework provides an end-to-end structure for strategic management that mirrors how professional strategists approach complete engagements. The Analyze phase involves diagnosing both the external environment (macro, industry, competitive) and internal resources (capabilities, financials, culture). The Formulate phase translates those insights into a coherent strategic direction—business-level positioning, corporate portfolio choices, and international strategy decisions. The Implement phase executes strategy through appropriate organizational design, governance structures, culture alignment, and performance management. The sequential yet iterative structure prevents the common failure of jumping straight from diagnosis to implementation without rigorous strategy formulation. [Hyper Island](https://hyperisland.com/en/blog/hyper-insights/top-7-strategic-frameworks-every-leader-should-know) recommends pairing the Analyze step with PESTEL or SWOT to ground formulation in data. Its strength lies in treating strategy as a continuous, iterative loop rather than a discrete annual planning exercise, ensuring that changing external conditions trigger timely reassessment and strategic adjustment across all three phases.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Analyze (external): macro trends, industry forces, competitive dynamics, market sizing
- Analyze (internal): resources, capabilities, value chain, culture, financial health
- Formulate: business-level strategy, corporate strategy, international strategy
- Implement: organizational structure, culture alignment, governance, performance systems
**Guiding questions:**
- Have we systematically assessed both external threats and internal capabilities before choosing strategy?
- Does the formulated strategy exploit identified strengths against real market opportunities?
- Is the organizational design capable of executing the chosen strategy?
- Where are the implementation bottlenecks most likely to emerge?
- How will we monitor strategy execution and when will we trigger reformulation?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness and rigor of both internal and external analyses
- Logical coherence between analysis findings and strategic choices
- Organizational readiness for implementation
- Clarity of accountability and milestones in the implement phase
- Feedback loops between implementation results and strategy recalibration
**Typical use cases:**
- End-to-end consulting strategy engagements
- Corporate strategic planning cycles
- Organizational transformation projects
- Long-range capital allocation decisions
**Strengths and limitations:** AFI's strength is its integrative completeness: it prevents teams from skipping steps and ensures strategy flows from evidence rather than intuition. The framework is comprehensive enough to guide an entire consulting engagement lifecycle. Its limitation is that the three-phase model can become overly linear in practice—real strategy is messier and more iterative. The framework also does not prescribe specific analytical tools within each phase, requiring practitioners to select appropriate sub-frameworks. Best suited for structured consulting contexts rather than rapid-cycle decision environments.
**Related frameworks:** PESTEL Analysis, McKinsey 7-S Framework, SWOT Analysis, Balanced Scorecard, Value Chain Analysis

### Blue Ocean Strategy
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** market creation, value innovation, competition, differentiation, uncontested markets
**How it works:** Blue Ocean Strategy, developed by W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne and published in their 2005 book of the same name, argues that lasting competitive advantage comes not from battling rivals in existing market space ("red oceans") but from creating entirely new market spaces ("blue oceans") where competition is irrelevant. The central analytical tool is the Strategy Canvas, which plots competing factors on the x-axis and offering levels on the y-axis to reveal the competitive profile of current players. Teams then apply the ERRC Grid—Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create—to reconstruct buyer value and unlock new demand simultaneously reducing costs. The framework distinguishes between value innovation (raising buyer value while lowering cost) and conventional innovation. Tipping Point Leadership addresses how to execute the strategy inside organizations resistant to change.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Strategy Canvas: visual mapping of competitive factors and offering levels
- ERRC Grid: Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create factors relative to industry standard
- Three tiers of non-customers: who is not yet buying and why
- Value innovation: simultaneously pursuing differentiation and low cost
- Tipping point leadership: overcoming organizational hurdles to execution
**Guiding questions:**
- Which factors our industry competes on should be eliminated because they no longer add buyer value?
- Which factors are over-designed relative to what buyers actually need?
- Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
- Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?
- Who are the non-customers closest to market adoption, and what barriers prevent them from switching?
- Can we achieve value innovation—higher buyer utility at lower cost—simultaneously?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Distinctiveness and non-imitation of the new value curve
- Size of addressable non-customer population unlocked
- Feasibility of cost structure supporting the value proposition
- Organizational capability and willingness to sustain the new offering
- Time horizon to market acceptance
**Typical use cases:**
- New product or business model innovation
- Strategic repositioning away from commoditized competition
- Startup strategy and differentiation
- Industry transformation and disruption planning
**Strengths and limitations:** Blue Ocean Strategy provides a rare proactive framework for creating competitive advantage rather than defending against existing rivals, making it highly valuable for innovation contexts. Its weakness is execution difficulty: truly uncontested market spaces are rare, and "blue oceans" rapidly attract competition once proven. The framework is also more straightforwardly applied by market-makers than by capital-constrained firms. The ERRC grid, while elegant, requires deep customer insight to populate accurately. Combine with design thinking and jobs-to-be-done analysis for richer insight into latent buyer needs.
**Related frameworks:** Ansoff Matrix, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Disruptive Innovation (Christensen), Value Proposition Canvas, Business Model Canvas

### Balanced Scorecard
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** performance management, strategy execution, KPIs, financial, learning, customer, process
**How it works:** The Balanced Scorecard (BSC) was developed by Robert Kaplan and David Norton and first published in the Harvard Business Review in 1992. It translates organizational strategy into a coherent set of performance measures across four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Business Processes, and Learning & Growth. The core premise is that financial measures alone are lagging indicators; firms that manage only financial results are "driving while looking in the rearview mirror." Each perspective contains objectives, measures, targets, and initiatives, all causally linked through a Strategy Map that shows how non-financial drivers ultimately generate financial outcomes. BSC functions as both a measurement system and a management system, aligning organizational units, budget allocation, and employee incentives to strategic priorities. The framework's power comes from causal linkage mapping—connecting leading indicators (capability, process) to lagging financial outcomes, making the logic of value creation explicit and testable.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Financial perspective: revenue growth, profitability, return on capital, cash flow
- Customer perspective: market share, customer satisfaction, retention, value proposition delivery
- Internal process perspective: operational excellence, innovation cycle time, quality metrics
- Learning and growth perspective: employee capability, information systems, organizational culture
- Strategy Map: causal linkage diagram connecting all four perspectives
**Guiding questions:**
- How must we appear to shareholders to succeed financially?
- What customer outcomes must we achieve to reach financial goals?
- Which internal processes must we excel at to deliver the customer value proposition?
- What capabilities, systems, and culture must we build to sustain the other three perspectives?
- Are our measures leading or lagging, and do we have a balance of both?
- Is there clear line-of-sight between individual team measures and corporate strategy?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Strategic alignment across all four perspectives
- Balance between leading and lagging indicators
- Causal coherence of the Strategy Map logic
- Number of measures (10–25 total is typical; excessive measures dilute focus)
- Integration with budgeting, incentive, and reporting processes
**Typical use cases:**
- Corporate strategic planning and execution management
- Business unit performance alignment
- Government and non-profit strategy implementation
- Executive dashboard design and board reporting
**Strengths and limitations:** The Balanced Scorecard's greatest strength is forcing explicit conversation about strategy execution and the non-financial drivers of long-term value, correcting the short-termism of purely financial management. It excels at creating organizational alignment when adopted consistently. Limitations include implementation complexity, the risk of measure proliferation, and the tendency to bureaucratize into a reporting exercise disconnected from strategic dialogue. Without strong leadership commitment, BSCs become checkbox exercises. Works best when reviewed in strategy review meetings rather than treated as a static dashboard.
**Related frameworks:** OKRs, OGSM, Strategy Map, McKinsey 7-S Framework, SWOT Analysis

### OGSM (Objectives, Goals, Strategies, Measures)
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** annual planning, goal setting, strategic alignment, KPIs, execution
**How it works:** OGSM translates high-level organizational vision into operational execution through four linked layers. Objectives are qualitative, aspirational statements of direction—what the organization aims to become. Goals are specific, quantitative outcomes that define success within the planning horizon. Strategies articulate the key initiatives and approaches that will deliver the goals. Measures are the leading and lagging KPIs that confirm whether strategies are producing the intended results. Developed originally by Procter & Gamble and widely used in FMCG and consumer-facing industries, OGSM condenses strategy to a single page, making it immediately communicable across organizational layers. [Hyper Island](https://hyperisland.com/en/blog/hyper-insights/top-7-strategic-frameworks-every-leader-should-know) recommends using OGSM as a live roadmap, updating it quarterly and keeping it visible rather than hidden in slide decks. Its one-page format forces concision, ensuring that every strategic choice is connected to a measurable goal and an actionable strategy, with explicit KPIs for accountability tracking throughout the planning period.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Objective: qualitative vision statement (e.g., "Become the most trusted brand in sustainable packaging")
- Goals: quantitative outcomes with time horizons (e.g., "Achieve 25% EU market share by Q4")
- Strategies: key initiatives enabling goal achievement (e.g., "Expand B2B channel via retail partnerships")
- Measures: KPIs and thresholds tracking strategy effectiveness (e.g., CAC, churn, contract value)
**Guiding questions:**
- What qualitative direction will inspire the organization over the planning horizon?
- Which numeric outcomes would unambiguously confirm success?
- Which two or three initiatives will account for the majority of goal achievement?
- How will we know within 30 days whether a strategy is on track?
- Are objectives, goals, strategies, and measures causally consistent with each other?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Internal consistency between all four OGSM layers
- Measurability of goals and measures
- Strategic focus: does the OGSM force genuine prioritization?
- Feasibility of goals given available resources
- Accessibility and communication across organizational levels
**Typical use cases:**
- Annual and quarterly business planning
- Business unit strategy alignment
- Cross-functional initiative coordination
- Strategic reset following major market disruptions
**Strengths and limitations:** OGSM's one-page discipline enforces radical prioritization, making it one of the most practical tools for operationalizing strategy. It bridges the common gap between strategic intent and daily operations. Limitations include a tendency to conflate strategy with tactics if the Strategies layer is not sufficiently high-level, and difficulty capturing emergent or adaptive strategy in fast-moving environments. OKRs offer a more iterative alternative; Balanced Scorecard adds multi-perspective depth. OGSM is most effective in stable industries with clear annual planning cycles. Organizations can also fall into the trap of setting too many OGSMs, diluting focus rather than concentrating effort on the critical few strategic priorities that will most differentiate performance.
**Related frameworks:** OKRs, Balanced Scorecard, Hoshin Kanri, SMART Goals, Strategy Map

### Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** customer insight, innovation, value proposition, product strategy, demand
**How it works:** Jobs-to-Be-Done theory, developed primarily by Clayton Christensen and further formalized by researchers including Tony Ulwick, reframes product strategy around the functional and emotional "job" a customer is trying to accomplish rather than around product attributes or demographic profiles. Customers "hire" products and services to get a job done; when a product does the job better than alternatives, it gains and retains customers. The framework distinguishes core functional jobs (the primary outcome sought), emotional jobs (how customers want to feel), and social jobs (how customers want to be perceived). Practitioners conduct JTBD interviews to uncover unarticulated needs, identify job-to-be-done statements, and map the job map—a process view of how customers currently accomplish the job. This underpins innovation and segmentation strategy grounded in behavioral demand rather than demographics.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Core functional job: the primary task the customer is trying to accomplish
- Emotional job: desired feelings or states during and after the job
- Social job: how the customer wishes to be perceived by others
- Job map: stages of the job (define, locate, prepare, confirm, execute, monitor, modify, conclude)
- Outcome statements: metrics customers use to evaluate success at each job stage
**Guiding questions:**
- What progress is the customer fundamentally trying to make?
- At which stages of the job do customers currently experience the most frustration?
- What non-consuming alternatives do customers use when no good product exists?
- Which outcome statements are important to customers but underserved by existing solutions?
- What emotional or social dimensions of the job are competitors ignoring?
- How does the job differ across customer segments we currently conflate?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Depth and authenticity of customer interviews
- Specificity of job-to-be-done statements (avoid product-centric framing)
- Coverage of functional, emotional, and social dimensions
- Identification of underserved outcomes (high importance, low satisfaction)
- Actionability of insights for product roadmap or strategy
**Typical use cases:**
- Product innovation strategy and roadmap prioritization
- Market segmentation based on demand rather than demographics
- Competitive repositioning to address unmet needs
- New product concept screening
**Strengths and limitations:** JTBD is uniquely powerful at uncovering latent demand that conventional market research misses, because it focuses on behavior and outcomes rather than opinions. It often reveals unexpected competitive sets (e.g., milkshakes competing with bananas for morning commuters). Limitations include execution demands: high-quality JTBD research requires trained interviewers and careful analysis. The framework can also be over-applied to categories where product attributes genuinely dominate. Pair with quantitative outcome-driven innovation research to prioritize investment across job stages. The most common limitation is interview quality: JTBD interviews require significant practitioner skill to surface underlying functional and emotional jobs rather than rationalizations of existing behavior, and poor interviews produce misleading product direction.
**Related frameworks:** Blue Ocean Strategy, Value Proposition Canvas, Design Thinking, Business Model Canvas, Customer Journey Mapping

### Scenario Planning
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** uncertainty, long-range planning, futures, risk, strategic flexibility
**How it works:** Scenario Planning is a structured methodology for developing multiple plausible futures and stress-testing strategy against each. Pioneered at Royal Dutch Shell in the 1960s and 1970s, it moves organizations beyond single-point forecasting by building three to five internally consistent narratives about how the environment could evolve. The process begins by identifying driving forces—social, technological, economic, environmental, political—then selecting two to three critical uncertainties with the highest impact and highest unpredictability. These uncertainties form the axes of a scenario matrix. Teams develop vivid, named narratives for each quadrant and then test current strategy against each scenario, identifying vulnerabilities and strategic options that are robust across multiple futures. Leading indicators are established to detect which scenario is unfolding in real time. Unlike forecasts, scenarios are explicitly not predictions; they are plausible alternative futures designed to stretch organizational thinking and build strategic resilience through pre-planned adaptive responses.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Driving forces identification (STEEP scan of key macro forces)
- Critical uncertainties selection (high impact, high unpredictability)
- Scenario axes and matrix construction
- Narrative development for each scenario
- Strategy stress-testing and option identification
- Early warning indicator system
**Guiding questions:**
- Which uncertainties, if they resolved differently, would most dramatically alter our strategy?
- Is our current strategy robust across all plausible scenarios, or does it only work in one?
- Which strategic options create value in multiple scenarios ("no-regret moves")?
- What are the leading indicators that would signal a particular scenario unfolding?
- How should we sequence investments given different scenario probabilities?
- Where does our current plan carry the greatest vulnerability?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Internal consistency of each narrative
- Genuine differentiation between scenarios (not optimistic/base/pessimistic)
- Breadth of environmental forces covered
- Quality of strategic insights generated per scenario
- Practicality of early-warning indicator system
**Typical use cases:**
- Long-range strategic planning (5–20 year horizons)
- Risk management and business continuity planning
- Policy development and infrastructure investment
- Industry-wide disruption preparation
**Strengths and limitations:** Scenario Planning builds organizational resilience by expanding leadership's ability to think in futures rather than linear trend extrapolation. Its greatest strength is preparing minds and strategies for surprises. Limitations include the resource intensity of full scenario engagements and the difficulty of maintaining scenario disciplines as operational pressures mount. Scenarios can also become narrative artifacts that are never operationally connected to real decisions. Integrate with decision trigger frameworks and strategic option value analysis to close the gap between narratives and action.
**Related frameworks:** PESTEL Analysis, Strategic Options Analysis, Real Options, Wargaming, Risk Matrix

### OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** goal setting, alignment, performance, quarterly planning, execution
**How it works:** OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) is a collaborative goal-setting framework developed at Intel by Andy Grove and later popularized through John Doerr's adoption at Google, as documented in his 2018 book "Measure What Matters." An Objective is an ambitious, qualitative description of what the team intends to achieve; Key Results are three to five specific, measurable outcomes that define what successful achievement of the objective looks like. OKRs cascade from company to team to individual level, creating vertical alignment. They operate on quarterly cycles, encouraging frequent check-ins and iteration. OKRs are intended to be stretch targets—achieving 70% of a Key Result is often considered strong performance—which differentiates them from performance management tools where targets must be fully met. Transparency is core: OKRs are typically visible across the organization.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Objective: inspiring, qualitative direction (what we want to achieve)
- Key Results: measurable, time-bound outcomes (how we know we achieved it)
- Cascading levels: company, department, team, individual OKRs
- Quarterly cadence with mid-period check-ins
- Grading: typically on a 0–1.0 or 0–100% scale; 0.7 is often the target
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the most important thing this team should accomplish this quarter?
- How would we objectively know, at quarter-end, whether we achieved the objective?
- Are key results outcomes (not activities or tasks)?
- Do team OKRs visibly support company-level OKRs?
- Are we setting stretch targets that require meaningful change to achieve?
- How will we review and adapt OKRs mid-cycle when conditions change?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Ambition: are objectives truly stretching beyond business-as-usual?
- Measurability: are key results quantifiable with defined targets?
- Alignment: do lower-level OKRs clearly support higher-level objectives?
- Focus: is the number of OKRs small enough to concentrate effort?
- Transparency: are OKRs visible and understandable across levels?
**Typical use cases:**
- Technology company strategy and execution management
- Rapid-growth startup organizational alignment
- Cross-functional initiative prioritization
- Quarterly planning and performance review
**Strengths and limitations:** OKRs create strong alignment and focus by making priorities explicit and visible, reducing the common organizational disease of effort dispersed across low-impact activities. The stretch-target philosophy promotes innovation. Weaknesses include implementation failure when OKRs are tied to compensation (converting them into conservative targets), when key results measure outputs rather than outcomes, or when the quarterly cadence is inadequate for industries with long lead times. Requires strong facilitation during the goal-setting process to avoid generic, unmeasurable key results. OKR adoption also frequently fails when organizations set too many objectives or when key results are chosen for measurability rather than strategic significance, undermining the framework's intended focus and accountability value.
**Related frameworks:** OGSM, Balanced Scorecard, Hoshin Kanri, SMART Goals, Strategy Map

### Hoshin Kanri (Policy Deployment)
**Category:** Strategy
**Tags:** strategic alignment, execution, Japanese management, X-matrix, catchball
**How it works:** Hoshin Kanri (literally "compass management" or "policy deployment") is a strategic planning and execution methodology originating in Japanese manufacturing, notably at Toyota. It aligns organizational effort at every level—from executive to front-line—toward a small number of annual and multi-year "breakthrough objectives." The X-Matrix is the central planning tool: it simultaneously displays long-term goals, annual priorities, improvement initiatives, and metrics, with visual correlation arrows showing how each element supports the others. The distinctive "catchball" process—iterative two-way dialogue between hierarchical levels during goal-setting—contrasts with conventional top-down deployment, ensuring goals are both ambitious and executable. Monthly and quarterly reviews close the plan-do-check-act (PDCA) loop. The critical mechanism is catchball: a multi-directional dialogue between leadership and operational teams that refines objectives and means until mutual commitment is achieved, avoiding both top-down mandate failures and bottom-up initiative fragmentation. According to lean management practice, catchball prevents the disconnect between strategic intent and operational execution that undermines most planning processes.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Breakthrough objectives: long-term (3–5 year) strategic priorities
- Annual priorities: specific focus areas for the current year
- Improvement initiatives: projects and actions enabling annual priorities
- Success metrics: measurable targets linked to each initiative
- X-Matrix: single-page visual integrating all four layers with correlation mapping
- Catchball: iterative dialogue for cascading and negotiating targets
**Guiding questions:**
- What are the two or three breakthroughs that would transform our competitive position over five years?
- Which annual priorities, if achieved, would most accelerate the breakthrough objectives?
- Where in the organization are there misalignments between stated priorities and daily activities?
- Does the catchball process genuinely allow lower levels to challenge and refine targets?
- Are our monthly review processes triggering real countermeasures, or just reporting?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Focus: are breakthrough objectives genuinely few and consequential?
- Vertical alignment across all organizational levels
- Bidirectionality of the catchball process
- Integrity of the PDCA review cycle
- Correlation strength between X-Matrix elements
**Typical use cases:**
- Lean transformation and operational excellence programs
- Manufacturing sector annual planning
- Quality management systems integration (ISO, TQM)
- Large organization alignment where top-down deployment has failed
**Strengths and limitations:** Hoshin Kanri excels at creating genuine organizational alignment and preventing the dispersion of effort across disconnected initiatives—a chronic problem in large organizations. The X-Matrix enforces explicit prioritization. Its main limitation is implementation complexity: the catchball process requires significant management time and a high-trust culture. Organizations without PDCA discipline or lean management maturity often implement Hoshin as a planning ritual without an effective review process, eliminating most of its value. In organizations accustomed to top-down planning without dialogue, the catchball process is often perceived as inefficient or politically threatening, and leadership commitment to genuine two-way iteration—rather than cascaded mandates dressed as catchball—is the most common failure point.
**Related frameworks:** OKRs, Balanced Scorecard, OGSM, Lean Management, PDCA Cycle

### MECE Principle
**Category:** Consulting & Problem-Solving
**Tags:** problem structuring, logic, issue trees, mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive
**How it works:** MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) is the foundational structuring principle underpinning consulting problem decomposition and communication. Developed by Barbara Minto during her tenure at McKinsey in the late 1960s, it governs how complex problems should be broken into component parts. "Mutually exclusive" means no overlap between categories—each element belongs to exactly one bucket. "Collectively exhaustive" means the categories together cover the entire problem space with no gaps. Applied to business problems, MECE prevents double-counting in analyses (e.g., revenue split by geography and by product line simultaneously) and eliminates the risk of missing an important causal driver. As documented on [Deckary](https://deckary.com/blog/problem-solving-frameworks), MECE underpins every problem-decomposition step in the McKinsey consulting process and forms the backbone of issue tree construction. Applying MECE rigorously prevents the two most common analytical failures in consulting and business analysis: double-counting causes or solutions, and missing entire categories of explanation that later prove critical.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Mutually exclusive: no overlap between categories
- Collectively exhaustive: no gap in coverage
- Decomposition levels: the principle applies at every level of the issue tree
- Test for overlap: ask whether any data point could plausibly belong to two categories
- Test for completeness: ask what category a hypothetical edge case would fall into
**Guiding questions:**
- Could any element of this breakdown belong to more than one category?
- Is there any part of the problem space that none of these categories covers?
- At what level of granularity should we stop decomposing?
- Are these categories truly parallel, or are some nested within others?
- Does this structure pass the "elevator test"—can it be explained in 30 seconds?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Zero overlap between categories (no element counted twice)
- Complete coverage of the relevant problem domain
- Parallel level of abstraction across sibling categories
- Internal consistency within each category
- Communicability to a non-specialist audience
**Typical use cases:**
- Structuring consulting issue trees and work plans
- Segmenting markets or customer bases for analysis
- Designing survey instruments and analytical frameworks
- Organizing PowerPoint slides or executive communication
**Strengths and limitations:** MECE is the most universally applicable structuring discipline in consulting because it applies regardless of problem type. Well-executed MECE structures accelerate analysis by ensuring effort is neither duplicated nor misdirected. Its limitation is that MECE compliance can become an end in itself, privileging logically tidy structures over those that best reflect business reality. Some real problems are genuinely complex and overlapping; forcing MECE can distort them. Moreover, MECE is a structural quality criterion, not a content criterion—a MECE framework can still yield wrong strategic answers.
**Related frameworks:** Issue Tree, Pyramid Principle, Hypothesis-Driven Approach, Logic Tree, Profitability Framework

### Issue Tree (Logic Tree)
**Category:** Consulting & Problem-Solving
**Tags:** problem decomposition, hypothesis, work planning, analysis structure
**How it works:** An Issue Tree (also called a Logic Tree) is a visual tool that decomposes a complex business question into progressively smaller, testable sub-questions until reaching specific hypotheses that can be validated with data. The process begins with a single governing question at the tree's root, which is broken into MECE branches at the first level; each branch is further decomposed until the leaves represent discrete analytical tasks assignable to individual team members. Two dominant tree types exist: diagnostic trees (starting from a symptom and branching toward root causes) and solution trees (starting from a desired outcome and branching toward enabling conditions). [Deckary](https://deckary.com/blog/problem-solving-frameworks) notes that issue trees are the primary structuring tool before any analysis begins in consulting engagements, transforming an overwhelming problem into a manageable work plan.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Root node: the governing business question
- First-level branches: MECE decomposition of the root question
- Sub-branches: progressive decomposition to testable hypotheses
- Leaf nodes: discrete, analyzable questions with clear data requirements
- Diagnostic vs. solution tree variants
**Guiding questions:**
- Is the root question specific and bounded enough to be answerable?
- Does the first level of branches pass MECE tests?
- At which node does enough data exist to reach a conclusion without further decomposition?
- Which branches are most likely to contain the answer, and should be prioritized?
- What data source or analysis would resolve each leaf node?
- Have we pruned branches that are unlikely to yield material insights?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- MECE compliance at every decomposition level
- Specificity of leaf-node hypotheses
- Appropriate depth (sufficient granularity for task assignment without over-decomposition)
- Prioritization of high-impact branches
- Clarity of data requirements at each leaf
**Typical use cases:**
- Consulting engagement work plan design
- Root cause analysis of operational or financial performance issues
- Strategic options mapping
- Analytical team task allocation
**Strengths and limitations:** Issue trees are exceptional at converting complex, ambiguous problems into concrete analytical roadmaps, reducing the risk of teams pursuing unfocused or redundant analyses. They also force early explicit thinking about problem structure, which often surfaces disagreements about scope. Limitations include the tendency to construct trees that look rigorous but miss the most important causal mechanism because it cuts across neat categorical boundaries. Teams can also over-invest in comprehensive tree-building when speed matters and a quick hypothesis-first approach would be more effective.
**Related frameworks:** MECE Principle, Hypothesis-Driven Approach, Root Cause Analysis, Pyramid Principle, McKinsey Problem-Solving Process
