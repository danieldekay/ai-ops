### Second-Order Thinking

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** consequences, systems, temporal reasoning, complexity, decision quality
**How it works:** Second-order thinking extends analysis beyond the immediate, visible outcome of a decision to its downstream and indirect effects. The first-order question is "what happens if I do this?" The second-order question is "and then what?" This cascade continues for as many rounds as is analytically useful. Howard Marks of Oaktree Capital popularized the framework in investing contexts, arguing that consensus thinking is always first-order and therefore priced in; alpha is only generated by correct second- and third-order reasoning. The mental move is to resist the pull of the obvious result and deliberately map the chain of consequences that unfolds once the environment, competitors, and affected parties respond to the first-order effect. Systems with feedback loops, adaptive agents, or strong network effects particularly reward this kind of extended reasoning.

**Dimensions / components:**
- First-order effect: immediate, direct consequence
- Second-order effect: response of the environment or agents to the first-order outcome
- Third-order effect: downstream consequences of those responses
- Time horizon specification (short, medium, long)
- Identification of affected stakeholders and their likely reactions
- Mapping feedback loops and potential amplification or dampening

**Guiding questions:**
- What is the immediate, obvious result of this action?
- How will people, markets, or systems respond to that result?
- What does that response in turn cause?
- Are there feedback loops that could amplify unintended effects?
- Over what time horizon should consequences be mapped?
- Which second-order effects are most asymmetric in magnitude?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Are multiple rounds of consequences explicitly mapped?
- Have affected stakeholders and their reactions been identified?
- Are feedback loops and non-linear dynamics considered?
- Is the chosen time horizon appropriate to the decision?
- Does analysis avoid stopping at the comfortable first-order answer?

**Typical use cases:**
- Policy design and regulatory impact assessment
- Investment thesis development
- Strategic product roadmap decisions with market-response implications
- Organizational change management

**Strengths and limitations:** Second-order thinking dramatically improves decision quality in complex adaptive systems where obvious solutions often generate perverse outcomes. It counters the "unintended consequences" failure mode that plagues single-step reasoning. The primary limitation is analytical cost and potential paralysis: chains of consequences can extend indefinitely, and at some point probabilistic uncertainty dominates. Practitioners must set deliberate stopping rules and accept irreducible uncertainty rather than attempting to map all outcomes. Overuse can also produce analysis paralysis. Applied consistently, it is arguably the single most valuable decision-making upgrade available to practitioners.

**Related frameworks:** First Principles Thinking, Systems Thinking, Inversion, Scenario Analysis, Pre-Mortem Analysis

### Inversion

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** failure-avoidance, mental models, problem reframing, risk, Munger
**How it works:** Inversion reframes goal-directed thinking by asking what would guarantee failure rather than what would ensure success. Associated strongly with Charlie Munger, who drew on the German mathematician Carl Jacobi's maxim "invert, always invert," the technique forces attention onto the downside landscape—the failure modes, catastrophic errors, and structural impediments that are often invisible when pursuing a goal optimistically. The practitioner lists all the ways a project, investment, or decision could fail catastrophically, then works backward to determine what must be avoided or designed out of the system. Inversion is complementary to forward planning rather than a replacement: combining both directions of reasoning produces more complete maps of the decision space. It is especially valuable for spotting assumptions so embedded in organizational culture that they are never questioned.

**Dimensions / components:**
- Defining the goal clearly in positive terms
- Inverting the question: "What would make this certainly fail?"
- Generating an exhaustive failure-mode list
- Ranking failure modes by probability and severity
- Identifying which failure modes are preventable by design
- Constructing the forward plan with those preventions built in
- Checking whether avoiding all failures is sufficient for success

**Guiding questions:**
- What would guarantee that this project fails completely?
- Which failure modes are most catastrophic and most probable?
- What habits, decisions, or structures should be explicitly avoided?
- If I worked for a competitor trying to defeat this plan, what would I do?
- Is avoiding failure sufficient, or are additional positive actions required?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Is the failure-mode list exhaustive and specific?
- Are failure modes ranked by impact and likelihood?
- Have preventative design choices been integrated into the plan?
- Does the inversion complement rather than replace positive planning?
- Are structural or cultural failure modes—not just tactical ones—included?

**Typical use cases:**
- Pre-mortem analysis before project launch
- Risk management in engineering system design
- Investment process stress-testing
- Strategic planning in adversarial markets

**Strengths and limitations:** Inversion reliably surfaces risks and failure modes that forward optimism obscures, making plans more robust before they encounter reality. Munger credits inversion with a large share of his own decision quality. The limitation is that avoiding all listed failures does not automatically produce success—some positive creative insight is still required. Additionally, the failure-mode list is only as good as the practitioner's imagination and domain knowledge; rare, novel failure modes may not surface. Combining inversion with diverse team perspectives mitigates this.

**Related frameworks:** Pre-Mortem Analysis, Second-Order Thinking, Devil's Advocate Technique, Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA), Red Team Analysis

### Cynefin Framework

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** complexity, sensemaking, context-appropriate response, Snowden, leadership
**How it works:** The Cynefin framework, developed by Dave Snowden and Cynthia Kurtz at IBM in 1999, is a sensemaking model that helps decision-makers identify the nature of the situation they face before choosing a response strategy. It defines five domains: Obvious (formerly Simple), where cause-and-effect relationships are clear and best practices apply; Complicated, where expertise is needed to analyze before acting; Complex, where cause-and-effect relationships are only visible in retrospect and safe-to-fail experiments are appropriate; Chaotic, where immediate action to establish order precedes sense-making; and Disorder, where the domain is not yet identified. The critical insight is that each domain demands a categorically different decision approach, and applying the wrong approach—especially treating complex systems as merely complicated—is a primary source of organizational failure. ([Cognitive Edge](https://cynefin.io/))

**Dimensions / components:**
- Obvious/Clear domain: known cause-effect, apply best practices
- Complicated domain: knowable cause-effect, requires expert analysis
- Complex domain: emergent cause-effect, probe → sense → respond
- Chaotic domain: no perceivable cause-effect, act → sense → respond
- Disorder domain: unknown which domain applies
- Distinguishing between domains before committing to a response mode

**Guiding questions:**
- Can we identify clear cause-and-effect relationships in this situation?
- Is expertise sufficient to analyze and then act, or is the system too dynamic?
- Are we in a situation where patterns only emerge after the fact?
- Is the situation so unstable that immediate action is required before analysis?
- Have we verified our domain diagnosis before choosing a response mode?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Has the domain been correctly identified before a response is chosen?
- Is the response strategy (sense-analyze-respond vs. probe-sense-respond) appropriate to the domain?
- Are safe-to-fail experiments used in complex domains rather than large-scale commitments?
- Is there a mechanism to detect domain transitions over time?
- Are decision-makers resisting the pull to treat complex problems as merely complicated?

**Typical use cases:**
- Organizational change initiatives in uncertain environments
- Crisis management and emergency response
- Software product development with emergent user requirements
- Strategy formulation in turbulent or novel markets

**Strengths and limitations:** Cynefin provides a disciplined vocabulary for a genuinely important distinction—the difference between complex and complicated systems—that most frameworks elide. It prevents the misapplication of best-practice thinking to emergent situations. Its main limitation is that domain assignment is itself a judgment call subject to bias, and organizations tend to default to treating everything as complicated. The framework is descriptive and diagnostic rather than prescriptive, so it requires complementary action tools once a domain is identified. Nonetheless, Cynefin remains the most operationally useful sense-making model for leaders navigating genuine complexity.

**Related frameworks:** Systems Thinking, Scenario Analysis, Agile Framework, OODA Loop, Observational Decision Cycle

### Type 1 / Type 2 Decision Framework

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** reversibility, speed, Bezos, decision velocity, organizational design
**How it works:** Jeff Bezos introduced this distinction in Amazon's 1997 shareholder letter, distinguishing between Type 1 decisions—irreversible, high-consequence, "one-way doors"—and Type 2 decisions—reversible, lower-consequence, "two-way doors." The framework argues that large organizations fail by applying the slow, deliberate process appropriate for Type 1 decisions to the much larger volume of Type 2 decisions, causing institutional slowness. For Type 1 decisions, extensive deliberation, broad consultation, and careful analysis are warranted. For Type 2 decisions, a single capable individual or small team should decide quickly with approximately 70% of the information they wish they had, then course-correct based on feedback. The critical managerial skill is accurately classifying decisions before applying the appropriate process, rather than defaulting to heavyweight process for all decisions. The framework reclaims organizational speed by liberating the 90% of decisions that are reversible from unnecessary deliberation overhead.

**Dimensions / components:**
- Type 1: irreversible, high-stakes, "one-way door"—warrants slow process
- Type 2: reversible, lower-stakes, "two-way door"—warrants fast process
- Decision classification: determining which type before choosing process
- Information threshold: acting at ~70% information confidence for Type 2
- Organizational design: empowering teams to make Type 2 decisions autonomously
- Course-correction: rapid feedback loop for Type 2 decisions that prove wrong

**Guiding questions:**
- If this decision turns out to be wrong, how easily can it be reversed?
- What are the consequences of reversing versus being stuck with the wrong choice?
- Am I applying heavyweight process to a decision that genuinely deserves speed?
- Who is the smallest group that has sufficient context to make this Type 2 decision?
- At what information threshold is speed more valuable than additional deliberation?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Is decision type accurately classified before process is assigned?
- Are Type 2 decisions being made at the appropriate organizational level?
- Is the course-correction loop actually active for Type 2 decisions?
- Is the organization avoiding over-indexing on Type 1 caution for Type 2 situations?
- Are genuinely irreversible decisions receiving the deliberation they warrant?

**Typical use cases:**
- Organizational decision-rights design
- Product feature launch sequencing
- Leadership empowerment and delegation frameworks
- Innovation portfolio management

**Strengths and limitations:** The framework is elegantly simple and solves a real organizational failure mode—institutional slowness caused by applying excessive process to routine decisions. Its greatest value is the mindset shift: recognizing that most decisions are Type 2 and that speed plus course-correction outperforms deliberation plus delay. The limitation is that classification itself can be difficult: practitioners routinely misclassify partially irreversible decisions, and social and reputational costs of reversal are often underestimated when labeling something a "two-way door." Consistently applying the distinction prevents the largest category of organizational decision-making error: over-engineering reversible choices.

**Related frameworks:** SPADE Framework, DARE Decision Framework, RACI Model, Cynefin Framework, Pre-Mortem Analysis

### SPADE Decision Framework

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** structured process, consensus, accountability, Rajaram, Square
**How it works:** The SPADE framework was developed by Gokul Rajaram and Jeff Kolovson at Square as a structured alternative to consensus-driven decision-making that sacrifices both speed and quality. SPADE stands for Setting, People, Alternatives, Decide, and Explain. In the Setting phase, the decision context, constraints, and success criteria are explicitly documented. In People, roles are assigned: who provides input, who is consulted, and who makes the final call. In Alternatives, at least three genuinely distinct options are generated and analyzed. In Decide, one alternative is selected based on the defined criteria. In Explain, the rationale is communicated to all stakeholders so that even those who disagreed can understand and support execution. The framework ensures that important decisions are made deliberately, with clear ownership, documented reasoning, and stakeholder alignment, without requiring slow organizational consensus.

**Dimensions / components:**
- Setting: framing the decision with context, constraints, and success criteria
- People: assigning decision rights, advisors, and informed parties
- Alternatives: generating and analyzing at least three viable options
- Decide: selecting among alternatives against defined criteria
- Explain: communicating the decision and its reasoning to stakeholders
- Documentation: creating a written record enabling post-hoc review

**Guiding questions:**
- What are the specific constraints and success criteria for this decision?
- Who needs to provide input versus who holds the final decision right?
- Have we generated genuinely distinct alternatives rather than variants of one idea?
- Which alternative best satisfies the defined success criteria?
- How will affected stakeholders be informed and aligned on the decision and its rationale?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Is the decision's context and success criteria explicitly documented?
- Are decision rights clearly assigned to a single owner?
- Are at least three meaningfully distinct alternatives analyzed?
- Is the explanation sufficient for stakeholders who disagreed to support execution?
- Is the decision record preserved for future learning and accountability?

**Typical use cases:**
- High-stakes product or technology decisions at scale
- Cross-functional strategic decisions requiring alignment
- Decisions with long implementation timelines requiring buy-in
- Organizational choices that will be scrutinized retrospectively

**Strengths and limitations:** SPADE elegantly solves the false choice between speed and deliberation by adding structure without requiring consensus. The Explain step in particular generates accountability and organizational learning that informal decision processes miss entirely. The limitation is overhead: the full SPADE process is too heavy for routine decisions and benefits most from Type 1 situations where written documentation and clear ownership add proportionate value. Teams must resist ritualizing it for decisions that are genuinely Type 2. When applied rigorously, SPADE dramatically improves decision quality and organizational confidence in outcomes.

**Related frameworks:** Type 1/Type 2 Decision Framework, DARE Decision Framework, RACI Model, Pre-Mortem Analysis, Weighted Decision Matrix

### Weighted Decision Matrix

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** multi-criteria, quantification, trade-off, structured analysis, objectivity
**How it works:** The weighted decision matrix, also called a weighted scoring model, is a structured tool for evaluating and comparing multiple alternatives against a set of criteria when each criterion differs in importance. The process involves listing all candidate options as columns and all relevant evaluation criteria as rows, assigning a weight to each criterion proportional to its importance (weights summing to 1 or 100), scoring each option against each criterion on a consistent scale (typically 1–5 or 1–10), multiplying each score by its criterion weight, and summing the weighted scores across criteria to produce a total for each option. The option with the highest total score is the analytically preferred choice. The matrix forces explicit articulation of what matters and how much, separating the "what is important" question from the "which option delivers it" question.

**Dimensions / components:**
- Options: the mutually exclusive alternatives under evaluation
- Criteria: dimensions of value against which each option is judged
- Weights: relative importance assigned to each criterion
- Scores: performance of each option on each criterion
- Weighted scores: criterion weight multiplied by option score
- Total scores: sum of weighted scores for each option
- Sensitivity analysis: testing how robust the winner is to weight changes

**Guiding questions:**
- What are the criteria that truly matter for this decision?
- How should criteria be weighted relative to each other?
- Are criteria genuinely independent, or do they overlap?
- How stable is the winning option to reasonable changes in weights?
- Are scores being assigned on evidence or on prior preference for an option?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Are criteria mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive of what matters?
- Are weights derived from explicit stakeholder priorities rather than implicit assumptions?
- Are scores assigned before rather than after weights are set, to reduce reverse-engineering?
- Does sensitivity analysis confirm the winner is robust?
- Is the process documented for accountability and learning?

**Typical use cases:**
- Vendor or supplier selection
- Technology stack evaluation
- Strategic option prioritization
- Hiring candidate comparison

**Strengths and limitations:** The weighted decision matrix converts implicit, contested trade-offs into explicit, debatable ones—a significant improvement in transparency and accountability. It is particularly valuable for surfacing disagreements about priorities (weights) rather than facts (scores). Its limitations are a false precision risk: precise numerical outputs can obscure the subjectivity in both criteria weights and scores. Practitioners tend to reverse-engineer scores to match their intuitive preference, making process discipline essential. Sensitivity analysis is non-optional. As a collaborative tool, it remains indispensable for multi-stakeholder decisions requiring a traceable rationale.

**Related frameworks:** RICE Scoring Model, MoSCoW Method, Pugh Concept Selection Matrix, Cost-Benefit Analysis, SPADE Framework

### 10/10/10 Framework

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** temporal perspective, emotional regulation, time horizons, Welch
**How it works:** The 10/10/10 framework, developed by author and journalist Suzy Welch, is a temporal perspective tool for decisions clouded by immediate emotional intensity. The practitioner asks three questions about any given choice: How will I feel about this decision in 10 minutes? How will I feel about it in 10 months? How will I feel about it in 10 years? Each time horizon activates a different emotional and cognitive register. The 10-minute perspective captures the immediate emotional pull—the embarrassment, excitement, or fear driving the impulse. The 10-month perspective accesses medium-term practical consequences that are often the most decision-relevant. The 10-year perspective evokes values and identity, asking which choice the future self will endorse. By forcing attention across all three horizons simultaneously, the framework counteracts both impulsive short-termism and excessive long-term abstraction.

**Dimensions / components:**
- 10-minute horizon: immediate emotional response and comfort
- 10-month horizon: medium-term practical consequences and regret
- 10-year horizon: long-term alignment with values and identity
- Emotional diagnosis: identifying which horizon is dominating current reasoning
- Discrepancy analysis: noticing when horizons conflict
- Decision alignment: choosing the option with best aggregate performance across horizons

**Guiding questions:**
- What is driving my immediate emotional reaction to this choice?
- How will the practical consequences of this decision unfold over the next several months?
- When I look back in a decade, which option will I be glad I chose?
- Are my 10-minute emotions consistent with my 10-year values?
- At which time horizon is my judgment least reliable and most in need of correction?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Have all three time horizons been genuinely engaged rather than treated as a formality?
- Is the decision consistent with long-term values even if uncomfortable in the short term?
- Has the immediate emotional driver been identified and its distorting influence assessed?
- Does the choice look good from the medium-term practical horizon that is often most consequential?

**Typical use cases:**
- Personal career or relationship decisions under emotional pressure
- Consumer purchasing decisions with significant long-term implications
- Leadership decisions with immediate political costs but long-term strategic benefit
- Negotiations where short-term concession conflicts with long-term positioning

**Strengths and limitations:** The 10/10/10 framework is accessible, fast, and powerful at breaking the grip of immediate emotional intensity on decisions. It operationalizes the concept of emotional regulation through deliberate cognitive perspective-taking. Its limitation is that it is inherently qualitative and offers no resolution mechanism when the three horizons genuinely conflict. It also does not address uncertainty—how confident can the practitioner be in their 10-year projection? Combining with scenario analysis improves robustness. Its simplicity makes it immediately accessible and widely applicable across personal and professional decisions.

**Related frameworks:** Temporal Discounting Model, Second-Order Thinking, Values Clarification, Pre-Mortem Analysis, Regret Minimization Framework

### Regret Minimization Framework

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** long-term, regret, Bezos, values alignment, life decisions
**How it works:** Jeff Bezos described this framework in explaining his decision to leave a successful career at D.E. Shaw to found Amazon. The process involves projecting oneself to age 80 and imagining which decision would cause more regret: taking the risk or not taking it. By anchoring judgment to the perspective of one's final years, the framework bypasses the cognitive distortions introduced by present circumstances—current comfort, peer expectations, immediate risk aversion—and accesses a values-level evaluation of what genuinely matters. The framework is particularly powerful for large, identity-defining decisions where reversibility is low but the stakes are primarily existential rather than financial. It shares structure with the 10/10/10 framework but extends the horizon maximally and focuses specifically on regret as the evaluation currency rather than consequences.

**Dimensions / components:**
- 80-year-old self perspective: imagining the view from one's final years
- Regret inventory: identifying which choice creates more future regret
- Present-bias correction: separating current discomfort from long-term values
- Irreversibility assessment: how much does getting this wrong close off future options?
- Values clarification: what does the regret projection reveal about core priorities?
- Action alignment: choosing the path that minimizes projected regret

**Guiding questions:**
- When I am 80, which choice will I regret not having taken?
- Am I allowing present comfort or fear to substitute for long-term value?
- Is the regret from inaction or action more fundamental to who I want to be?
- What would I tell a younger version of myself facing this exact choice?
- Am I optimizing for short-term risk avoidance or long-term fulfillment?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Has the practitioner genuinely adopted the long-term perspective rather than trivially applying it?
- Does the analysis surface values rather than just emotional preferences?
- Has present-bias been explicitly identified and discounted?
- Is the option with lower long-term regret also consistent with near-term obligations?

**Typical use cases:**
- Career transitions involving significant risk
- Entrepreneurial or investment commitments with high optionality cost
- Major lifestyle decisions (geographic moves, family choices)
- Decisions about pursuing unconventional paths against conventional expectations

**Strengths and limitations:** The regret minimization framework is remarkably effective at cutting through present-bias and clarifying what genuinely matters at a values level. Its simplicity—one framing device—makes it highly portable and memorable. The main limitation is that it addresses only one dimension of regret (existential/values-level) and does not account for the regret of financial ruin, relationship damage, or health consequences. It is also not useful for routine decisions where long-term regret is not a meaningful category. Its primary value is as a corrective to the short-term emotional weighting that dominates most reversible-seeming decisions.

**Related frameworks:** 10/10/10 Framework, Values Clarification, Temporal Discounting Model, Second-Order Thinking, Opportunity Cost Analysis

### DARE Decision Framework

**Category:** Decision-Making
**Tags:** role clarity, accountability, decision rights, cross-functional, governance
**How it works:** DARE stands for Deciders, Advisors, Recommenders, and Execution stakeholders. Unlike RACI (which assigns Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed roles), DARE focuses specifically on the decision-making process rather than task execution. Deciders are the individuals who hold the final decision right—ideally one person, never more than three. Advisors are domain experts whose input is required before the decision is made but who do not hold a veto or vote. Recommenders research options and propose the course of action, doing the analysis that enables the Decider to choose. Execution stakeholders are those who will implement the decision and whose operational constraints should inform the recommendation. The framework prevents the diffusion of accountability that occurs when decision rights are unclear, while ensuring that the Decider has access to adequate expertise and implementation perspective.

**Dimensions / components:**
- Deciders: final decision authority, ideally singular
- Advisors: domain experts providing required input without veto power
- Recommenders: analysts or researchers generating options and proposals
- Execution stakeholders: implementers whose constraints inform recommendations
- Role assignment: explicit designation before deliberation begins
- Escalation path: what happens when a Decider is unavailable or conflicted

**Guiding questions:**
- Who holds the final decision right for this choice?
- Whose expertise is required input but not a vote?
- Who is responsible for generating and analyzing the options?
- Whose implementation constraints should shape the recommendation?
- Are decision roles assigned before deliberation begins, or are they emerging ad hoc?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Is there a single identified Decider with genuine authority?
- Are Advisor, Recommender, and Execution stakeholder roles clearly distinguished?
- Has the Recommender generated genuinely distinct alternatives?
- Do Execution stakeholder constraints feature in the recommendation?
- Is role clarity established before, not during, deliberation?

**Typical use cases:**
- Cross-functional product or strategy decisions at fast-scaling organizations
- Committee-prone decisions where consensus is diluting accountability
- Decisions where expertise and decision authority legitimately reside in different people
- Governance design for recurring decision categories

**Strengths and limitations:** DARE solves the accountability-diffusion problem that is endemic to consensus-driven organizations without concentrating all knowledge in the Decider. By separating the roles, it allows expertise to inform a decision without vetoing it—a critical design choice. Its limitation is that role assignments can themselves become contested, particularly when organizational status and formal authority are misaligned. The framework also works best when Deciders genuinely hold authority; in organizations where power is informal, DARE assignments may be performative. The framework's real value is in forcing explicit conversation about decision rights before conflict makes those conversations political.

**Related frameworks:** RACI Model, SPADE Framework, Type 1/Type 2 Decision Framework, Weighted Decision Matrix, RAPID Decision Framework

### Scenario Analysis

**Category:** Decision-Making / Forecasting
**Tags:** uncertainty, planning, strategy, Shell, futures thinking
**How it works:** Scenario analysis is a structured method for exploring a range of plausible future states rather than committing to a single point forecast. Developed formally by Herman Kahn at RAND Corporation and refined by Pierre Wack's team at Shell in the 1970s—where scenarios of oil price volatility enabled Shell to navigate the 1973 OPEC embargo more successfully than competitors—the technique identifies two to four critical uncertainties, combines them into distinct narrative futures, and tests how a strategy or decision performs across those futures. Scenarios are not predictions but plausible, internally consistent stories about how the future could unfold along different axes of uncertainty. The goal is strategic robustness: designing choices that perform adequately across multiple scenarios, or identifying option value—moves that look attractive only if certain scenarios unfold.

**Dimensions / components:**
- Identifying driving forces shaping the decision environment
- Selecting two critical, high-uncertainty axes for the scenario matrix
- Constructing two to four distinct, plausible, named scenarios
- Testing current strategy performance against each scenario
- Identifying robust options that perform acceptably across all scenarios
- Identifying option-value moves contingent on specific scenarios

**Guiding questions:**
- What are the two or three most consequential uncertainties facing this decision?
- What distinct futures could plausibly result from different resolutions of those uncertainties?
- How does our current strategy perform if each scenario unfolds?
- Which options are robust across all scenarios, and which are bets on specific ones?
- What early indicators would signal which scenario is materializing?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Are scenarios based on genuine uncertainties rather than projections of current trends?
- Are scenarios internally consistent and mutually distinct?
- Has strategy been tested against each scenario rather than just the most likely one?
- Have robust versus contingent options been explicitly categorized?
- Are scenario indicators defined for early-warning monitoring?

**Typical use cases:**
- Long-range corporate strategy in highly uncertain markets
- Policy design under political and macroeconomic uncertainty
- Infrastructure investment with 20+ year time horizons
- Risk management in financial institutions

**Strengths and limitations:** Scenario analysis uniquely addresses the problem of deep uncertainty—situations where probabilities themselves cannot be reliably assigned. It forces acknowledgment of multiple futures and produces genuinely robust strategies rather than optimized point-solutions. Shell's documented outperformance during the 1973 oil crisis remains the canonical demonstration of its value. The limitation is significant resource intensity and the risk of anchoring on the scenarios constructed rather than remaining open to outcomes outside the scenario set (the "unknown unknowns" problem). Scenario quality depends heavily on the imagination and domain knowledge of participants.

**Related frameworks:** Cynefin Framework, Second-Order Thinking, Monte Carlo Simulation, Pre-Mortem Analysis, Delphi Method

### Socratic Method

**Category:** Critical Thinking
**Tags:** inquiry, dialogue, Socrates, questioning, assumption-challenging
**How it works:** The Socratic Method is a form of cooperative argumentative dialogue that uses systematic questioning to expose contradictions in an interlocutor's reasoning, stimulate critical thinking, and reveal underlying assumptions. Originating in the practice of Socrates as documented by Plato, the method proceeds by the questioner feigning ignorance—the "Socratic irony"—and asking a series of increasingly precise questions designed to expose the logical entailments and inconsistencies of the other person's stated beliefs. The modern educational and organizational application involves using open-ended probing questions rather than assertions to drive analysis, generate hypotheses, and surface assumptions. Unlike debate, the Socratic method is not adversarial—it aims at shared understanding. It is particularly powerful for examining beliefs held unreflectively and for forcing practitioners to operationalize vague claims.

**Dimensions / components:**
- Clarifying questions: "What do you mean by that?"
- Probing assumptions: "What are you assuming here?"
- Probing evidence: "What evidence supports this?"
- Probing implications: "If that is true, what follows?"
- Questioning the question: "Why is this the right question to ask?"
- Exploring alternative viewpoints: "What would someone who disagrees say?"

**Guiding questions:**
- What exactly do I mean when I use this term or claim?
- What assumption am I making that I have not examined?
- What evidence would cause me to change this position?
- What are the logical implications of this position?
- What alternative explanations have I not considered?
- What would a thoughtful critic say against my position?

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Are questions genuinely open-ended rather than leading?
- Do questions progress logically from surface claim toward underlying assumptions?
- Is the dialogue producing genuine revision of belief rather than defensive entrenchment?
- Are implications of stated positions being explicitly traced?
- Is the method applied reciprocally rather than unilaterally?

**Typical use cases:**
- Legal depositions and cross-examination
- Philosophy and ethics education
- Organizational consulting and coaching
- Policy deliberation and structured argument

**Strengths and limitations:** The Socratic Method is uniquely powerful for exposing hidden assumptions and the logical entailments of stated positions, particularly in domains where beliefs are held confidently but unreflectively. It produces genuine conviction revision because the reasoner leads themselves to the contradiction rather than being told about it. Its limitation is that it requires significant time and skill; in practice, it can feel adversarial and provoke defensiveness if not executed with genuine collaborative intent. It is also ineffective when applied to empirical questions requiring data rather than logical analysis.

**Related frameworks:** Devil's Advocate Technique, Steel-Manning, Red Team Analysis, Argumentation Mapping, Dialectical Thinking
