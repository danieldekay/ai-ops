### Lean Startup
**Category:** Innovation / Experimentation
**Tags:** MVP, build-measure-learn, validated learning, pivot, Eric Ries, startup
**How it works:** Developed by Eric Ries and described in his 2011 book, Lean Startup applies lean manufacturing principles to new-venture creation and product development. The central engine is the Build–Measure–Learn feedback loop: teams identify their riskiest assumptions, design the smallest possible experiment (the Minimum Viable Product) to test those assumptions, measure real-world outcomes against predicted outcomes, and then decide whether to persevere, pivot, or abandon the hypothesis. The framework treats every product launch as a scientific experiment rather than an execution of a predetermined plan, and it prizes velocity of learning above velocity of feature delivery. Key metrics shift from vanity metrics (page views, downloads) to validated learning metrics that confirm or deny causal hypotheses about customer behavior.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Problem-Solution Fit — confirming that the identified problem is real and worth solving
- Minimum Viable Product (MVP) — the smallest artifact that generates validated learning
- Build–Measure–Learn loop — the core iteration engine
- Innovation Accounting — metrics that track learning progress, not just output
- Pivot vs. Persevere — structured decision point based on evidence from experiments
- Validated Learning — the unit of progress in a Lean Startup
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the riskiest assumption underlying our current strategy?
- What is the cheapest, fastest way to test that assumption?
- What metric would conclusively confirm or deny our hypothesis?
- Are our current metrics vanity metrics or actionable learning metrics?
- Based on the evidence, should we pivot, persevere, or stop?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Speed of Build–Measure–Learn cycles relative to competitors
- Quality and actionability of hypotheses being tested
- Degree to which metrics are tied to causal business assumptions
- Clarity and rigor of pivot decisions
- Rate of validated learning over time
**Typical use cases:**
- Early-stage startup product development under high uncertainty
- New product lines inside established companies (intrapreneurship)
- Testing new business models before committing significant capital
- Evaluating feature investments in existing products
**Strengths and limitations:** Lean Startup dramatically reduces wasted investment by validating assumptions before full-scale building, and its pivot discipline prevents teams from persisting with failing strategies. It is highly adaptable across industries. Limitations include the risk of building an MVP so minimal it fails to convey genuine value, and a tendency to over-index on quantitative metrics while under-investing in qualitative understanding of *why* metrics behave as they do. It also requires leadership tolerance for uncertainty that many organizations lack.
**Related frameworks:** Design Thinking, Customer Development, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Business Model Canvas, Agile/Scrum, Continuous Discovery

### Double Diamond
**Category:** Design Thinking / Service Design
**Tags:** divergence, convergence, problem space, solution space, UK Design Council
**How it works:** Developed by the [UK Design Council in 2005](https://www.designcouncil.org.uk/our-resources/the-double-diamond/), the Double Diamond maps the design process as two sequential diamond shapes representing divergent and convergent thinking. The first diamond covers the Problem Space: diverging to discover insights through research (Discover phase) and then converging to define the right problem (Define phase). The second diamond covers the Solution Space: diverging again to develop a wide range of ideas (Develop phase) before converging on a refined solution (Deliver phase). The explicit visual separation of problem discovery from solution development is its defining contribution — it prevents teams from jumping to solutions before fully understanding the problem. An updated 2019 version added four guiding principles: be people-centered, communicate visually, collaborate and co-create, and iterate.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Discover — broad research into user needs, context, and stakeholder landscape
- Define — synthesis and framing of the core design challenge
- Develop — ideation, concept development, and prototyping of multiple solutions
- Deliver — testing, refinement, and launch of the chosen solution
- Divergence phases — deliberate expansion of possibilities
- Convergence phases — disciplined reduction to the most promising option
**Guiding questions:**
- Are we solving the right problem before investing in solutions?
- What research methods will best reveal hidden user needs in the Discover phase?
- How do we know our problem statement accurately represents the challenge?
- What range of solution concepts have we genuinely explored before committing?
- How will we test and validate before full-scale delivery?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Quality and breadth of discovery research
- Sharpness and user-centeredness of the problem definition
- Volume and diversity of concepts explored in Develop
- Evidence-based criteria used to select the delivered solution
- Appropriateness of testing rigor before final delivery
**Typical use cases:**
- Service redesign projects in public sector or enterprise settings
- New product concept development requiring deep problem research
- Design education and workshop facilitation
- Complex cross-functional design challenges
**Strengths and limitations:** The Double Diamond is accessible and widely recognized, making it an effective communication tool for aligning stakeholders around design process expectations. Its explicit separation of problem and solution space prevents premature convergence, a common failure mode. However, the linear diamond metaphor can imply a sequential process that real projects rarely follow — in practice, teams cycle between phases. It also lacks specific guidance on research methods, ideation tools, or delivery formats.
**Related frameworks:** Design Thinking, Service Blueprint, HCD, Opportunity Solution Tree, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Agile/Scrum

### Business Model Canvas
**Category:** Product Strategy / Innovation
**Tags:** Osterwalder, value proposition, revenue streams, business model, Strategyzer
**How it works:** Created by Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur and popularized in *Business Model Generation* (2010), the Business Model Canvas is a one-page visual framework for describing, designing, and challenging business models. It organizes the nine building blocks of any business model — covering the customer side (segments, channels, relationships, revenue) and the infrastructure side (key activities, resources, partnerships, cost structure) — around a central Value Proposition. The canvas enables teams to see, at a glance, how all components of a business model fit together and to stress-test whether the logic is internally consistent. It is equally useful for analyzing existing models and designing new ones, and pairs naturally with the Value Proposition Canvas for deeper customer-job alignment.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Customer Segments — the groups of people or organizations a company serves
- Value Propositions — the bundle of products and services that create value for each segment
- Channels — how the company delivers value to customers
- Customer Relationships — types of relationships established with each segment
- Revenue Streams — how the company generates cash from each segment
- Key Resources — most important assets required to deliver the model
- Key Activities — most important things the company must do
- Key Partnerships — network of suppliers and partners
- Cost Structure — all costs incurred to operate the model
**Guiding questions:**
- Who are our most important customer segments, and do our value propositions match their jobs?
- Are our channels reaching customers effectively and efficiently?
- Which key activities are genuinely core to our value proposition versus outsourceable?
- Are our revenue streams aligned with the value customers receive?
- Where are the hidden cost drivers that undermine our model's viability?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Internal consistency among all nine building blocks
- Clarity and differentiation of the value proposition for each segment
- Feasibility and defensibility of key partnerships and resources
- Alignment between revenue model and customer value delivered
- Financial viability of the cost structure relative to revenue streams
**Typical use cases:**
- New venture design and pitching
- Strategic planning and business model innovation within established firms
- Competitive analysis by mapping competitors' canvases
- Intrapreneurial project justification
- M&A target evaluation
**Strengths and limitations:** The Business Model Canvas democratizes strategic thinking by making it visual, collaborative, and rapid to complete. It surfaced the importance of business model design as a distinct discipline from product development. Its limitations lie in its static snapshot nature — it captures a model at one moment and does not represent dynamics, competitive responses, or the sequence of activities needed to transition from the current to a future model. It also lacks financial depth.
**Related frameworks:** Value Proposition Canvas, Lean Canvas, Blue Ocean Strategy Canvas, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Strategy Map, Business Model Navigator

### Value Proposition Canvas
**Category:** Customer Discovery / Product Strategy
**Tags:** Osterwalder, customer jobs, gains, pains, fit, product-market fit
**How it works:** An extension of the Business Model Canvas developed by Osterwalder, Pigneur, Bernarda, and Smith, the Value Proposition Canvas zooms into the relationship between a single customer segment and the value proposition designed to serve it. The tool comprises two sides: the Customer Profile (customer jobs, pains, and gains) and the Value Map (products and services, pain relievers, and gain creators). Achieving "fit" — where the Value Map closely addresses the most important jobs, pains, and gains in the Customer Profile — is the explicit goal. The canvas is used iteratively: teams populate it with research-backed hypotheses, test those hypotheses with customers, and update the canvas as they learn. It operationalizes Jobs-to-Be-Done thinking in a structured, collaborative artifact.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Customer Jobs — what customers are trying to accomplish (functional, emotional, social)
- Customer Pains — negative outcomes, risks, and obstacles customers experience
- Customer Gains — outcomes and benefits customers desire
- Products & Services — the offering through which value is delivered
- Pain Relievers — how the offering alleviates specific customer pains
- Gain Creators — how the offering produces specific customer gains
- Fit — the degree of alignment between Profile and Value Map
**Guiding questions:**
- What are the top three most important jobs this customer segment is trying to accomplish?
- What pains cause the most frustration, and how severe are they?
- What gains matter most to this segment beyond the obvious?
- How specifically does our offering relieve each identified pain?
- Which gain creators are genuinely valued versus nice-to-have?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Depth and validity of customer research informing the Profile
- Specificity and testability of pain relievers and gain creators
- Degree of fit between Value Map and Customer Profile
- Evidence that ranked pains and gains reflect actual customer priorities
- Iterative refinement based on testing outcomes
**Typical use cases:**
- Early-stage product-market fit validation
- Repositioning an existing product for a new segment
- Marketing message development grounded in customer language
- Product feature prioritization workshops
**Strengths and limitations:** The Value Proposition Canvas creates a shared language between product, marketing, and customer success teams, and it forces explicit mapping between customer needs and product capabilities. It is highly effective as a workshop artifact. Its main limitation is that it is only as good as the underlying customer research — populated with assumptions rather than validated data, it can provide false confidence in a value proposition that has not been tested.
**Related frameworks:** Business Model Canvas, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Empathy Map, Lean Canvas, Customer Journey Map, Kano Model

### RICE Prioritization
**Category:** Roadmap Prioritization
**Tags:** Intercom, scoring model, reach, impact, confidence, effort, backlog
**How it works:** Developed by Intercom's product team and detailed in their product management blog, RICE is a quantitative scoring framework that ranks product initiatives by dividing the product of three benefit factors (Reach, Impact, Confidence) by Effort. Reach estimates how many users will be affected within a defined time period. Impact estimates the magnitude of effect on the chosen metric, typically scored on an ordered scale (0.25 = minimal, 3 = massive). Confidence reflects how certain the team is in the reach and impact estimates, expressed as a percentage. Effort is the total work required, expressed in person-months. The resulting RICE score enables teams to compare qualitatively different initiatives on a single numerical scale, reducing the influence of subjective advocacy in backlog prioritization conversations.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Reach — number of users or events affected per time period
- Impact — estimated magnitude of effect on target metric (ordered scale)
- Confidence — percentage certainty in reach and impact estimates
- Effort — total person-months of work required
- RICE Score — (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort
- Time horizon — the period over which Reach is calculated (typically quarterly)
**Guiding questions:**
- How many users will this change realistically affect within the scoring period?
- What is the expected magnitude of improvement to our core metric?
- How confident are we in these estimates, and what evidence supports that confidence?
- What is the realistic total effort including design, engineering, and QA?
- Does the RICE score align with strategic priorities, or does it need a qualitative override?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Consistency of scoring definitions across the team and time periods
- Use of data to inform Reach and Impact rather than pure assumption
- Calibration of Confidence levels against historical forecast accuracy
- Alignment of highest-scoring items with declared product strategy
- Explicit handling of dependencies between high-scoring initiatives
**Typical use cases:**
- Quarterly backlog prioritization in product teams
- Communicating prioritization rationale to engineering and design partners
- Comparing internally generated ideas against customer-requested features
- Portfolio-level investment decisions across multiple product areas
**Strengths and limitations:** RICE produces an auditable, repeatable scoring process that reduces HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion) effects and creates a common language for prioritization debates. It scales well across large backlogs. Its main limitation is that the underlying inputs remain estimates, and teams can game scores by inflating confidence or reach. RICE also does not capture strategic alignment or dependencies, so high scores alone should not drive decisions without qualitative review.
**Related frameworks:** Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF), MoSCoW, Impact–Effort Matrix, Kano Model, OKRs, ICE Scoring

### Impact–Effort Matrix
**Category:** Roadmap Prioritization / Ideation
**Tags:** 2x2 matrix, quick wins, prioritization, ideation, effort-value, six sigma
**How it works:** The Impact–Effort Matrix (also called the Value–Effort or Benefit–Effort Matrix) is a 2×2 prioritization tool that plots initiatives along two axes — the potential impact or value delivered to users or the business on one axis, and the implementation effort (time, cost, resources) on the other. The resulting four quadrants generate four strategic categories: High Impact / Low Effort items are "Quick Wins" to prioritize immediately; High Impact / High Effort items are "Major Bets" requiring planning and investment; Low Impact / Low Effort items are "Fill-Ins" for spare capacity; and Low Impact / High Effort items are "Money Pits" to avoid. The matrix is a workshop-friendly, visual tool that drives rapid alignment on relative priorities across diverse stakeholders.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Impact axis — estimated value delivered to users or business (customer satisfaction, revenue, retention)
- Effort axis — estimated implementation cost (engineering time, resources, complexity)
- Quick Wins quadrant — high impact, low effort; prioritize first
- Major Bets quadrant — high impact, high effort; plan and resource carefully
- Fill-Ins quadrant — low impact, low effort; tackle during slack capacity
- Money Pits quadrant — low impact, high effort; deprioritize or eliminate
**Guiding questions:**
- What evidence informs our assessment of impact for each item?
- Are we systematically biased toward underestimating effort?
- Have we exhausted Quick Wins before committing to Major Bets?
- Which Money Pits are consuming capacity that could be redirected?
- How do we handle items where impact and effort are genuinely uncertain?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Objectivity and data-backing of impact and effort estimates
- Group calibration to minimize individual anchor bias
- Frequency of revisiting the matrix as estimates improve
- Balance of Quick Wins versus Major Bets in active roadmap
- Stakeholder agreement on placement of contested items
**Typical use cases:**
- Workshop prioritization of feature ideas or improvement opportunities
- UX roadmapping and design sprint scoping (per [NN/Group](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/prioritization-methods/))
- Sprint retrospectives to identify process improvements
- Innovation portfolio review sessions
**Strengths and limitations:** The matrix is highly accessible, requires no scoring formulas, and generates rapid visual consensus. It works well in facilitated workshop settings with cross-functional teams. Its primary weakness is that both axes are subjective — different team members may place the same item in radically different quadrants without explicit estimation criteria. It is also binary (high/low) whereas real priorities exist on continuous scales.
**Related frameworks:** RICE, MoSCoW, Kano Model, Opportunity Solution Tree, Weighted Scoring Model, Now–Next–Later Roadmap

### Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF)
**Category:** Roadmap Prioritization / Agile
**Tags:** SAFe, cost of delay, agile prioritization, Lean, Reinertsen, sequencing
**How it works:** Weighted Shortest Job First is a prioritization model from the Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe) rooted in Don Reinertsen's work on flow and queuing theory. WSJF calculates priority by dividing the Cost of Delay — a composite of User/Business Value, Time Criticality, and Risk Reduction/Opportunity Enablement — by the Job Size (duration or story points). The core insight is that deferring high-value, time-sensitive work is not costless: delay has an economic cost that should inform sequencing decisions. By dividing value by size, WSJF maximizes throughput of delivered value over time — shorter jobs delivering equivalent delay cost should be done first. It is especially powerful for organizations running multiple parallel programs.
**Dimensions / components:**
- User/Business Value — relative value delivered to customers or business
- Time Criticality — how quickly value decays or deadline risk increases if deferred
- Risk Reduction/Opportunity Enablement — risk mitigated or future opportunity unlocked
- Cost of Delay — sum of the three value components (scored relatively)
- Job Size — estimated duration or effort
- WSJF Score — Cost of Delay / Job Size
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the economic cost of delaying this initiative by one sprint or one quarter?
- Is this item time-sensitive due to market windows, compliance deadlines, or competitor moves?
- Does completing this work reduce significant risk or enable a future high-value capability?
- How does job size affect our ability to deliver high cost-of-delay items quickly?
- Are we optimizing for portfolio-level throughput rather than individual team efficiency?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Relative scoring consistency across the portfolio
- Explicit accounting for time criticality in sequencing decisions
- Team calibration workshops to align on Cost of Delay estimates
- Correlation between WSJF priority and business outcomes delivered
- Integration with capacity planning to make WSJF actionable
**Typical use cases:**
- PI (Program Increment) planning in SAFe agile release trains
- Enterprise portfolio prioritization across multiple product lines
- Capital allocation decisions for engineering investments
- Balancing technical debt reduction against feature delivery
**Strengths and limitations:** WSJF brings economic rigor to prioritization by quantifying the cost of inaction — a dimension most frameworks ignore — and it scales well to large program portfolios. Its main challenge is that Cost of Delay estimation requires organizational maturity and can feel abstract to teams without lean/flow training. It is most effective when teams have calibrated relative scoring through repeated use.
**Related frameworks:** RICE, Lean Portfolio Management, Now–Next–Later Roadmap, OKRs, Opportunity Backlog, Kanban

### Now–Next–Later Roadmap
**Category:** Roadmap Prioritization / Product Strategy
**Tags:** roadmap, outcome-based, agile roadmap, Janna Bastow, prioritization, timeline-free
**How it works:** The Now–Next–Later roadmap, popularized by Janna Bastow of ProdPad, organizes the product backlog into three time horizons rather than fixed dates: Now (work in progress or about to start), Next (committed near-term work), and Later (longer-term possibilities). Unlike Gantt-style roadmaps, it makes no false promises about delivery dates for items beyond the immediate horizon, acknowledging that product work involves ongoing learning and change. Priority decreases with distance: Now items are fully defined, Next items are well understood but may shift, and Later items are directional bets. The format communicates strategic intent without over-committing to specifics, aligns stakeholders on direction, and creates space for discovery and change as teams learn from delivery.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Now horizon — work actively in progress; fully scoped and committed
- Next horizon — upcoming committed work; well-understood but subject to refinement
- Later horizon — strategic directions and bets; intentionally less detailed
- Outcome framing — each item framed around the outcome sought, not the feature to build
- Confidence gradient — explicit acknowledgment that certainty decreases with distance
**Guiding questions:**
- What are we actively building or shipping right now, and is it delivering expected outcomes?
- What is definitively committed as the next body of work, and what evidence supports those choices?
- What longer-term bets are we exploring, and what would we need to learn to commit to them?
- Are our Later items framed as outcomes and opportunities rather than predetermined features?
- How do OKRs or strategic themes map to items across the three horizons?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Clarity and completeness of Now items relative to delivery readiness
- Outcome-orientation of item descriptions across all horizons
- Stakeholder understanding that Later items are not commitments
- Cadence of roadmap reviews and updates as learning occurs
- Alignment with product strategy and OKRs
**Typical use cases:**
- Communicating product strategy to stakeholders and executives
- Quarterly planning and roadmap review sessions
- Replacing date-driven Gantt roadmaps in agile organizations
- Onboarding new team members to product direction
**Strengths and limitations:** The Now–Next–Later format is honest about uncertainty in a way date-driven roadmaps are not, reducing the planning theater that accompanies false precision. It is visually simple and accessible to non-technical stakeholders. Its limitation is that it can frustrate stakeholders who need commitments for go-to-market or resource planning, and without disciplined outcome framing it can devolve into a feature-list roadmap under a different label.
**Related frameworks:** OKRs, Opportunity Solution Tree, WSJF, RICE, Story Mapping, Continuous Discovery

### Agile/Scrum
**Category:** Product Management / Experimentation
**Tags:** sprints, iteration, ceremonies, backlog, cross-functional, agile manifesto
**How it works:** Agile is a family of iterative software development approaches formalized by the [Agile Manifesto](https://agilemanifesto.org/) in 2001. Scrum, the most widely adopted Agile framework (codified by Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber), organizes work into fixed-length sprints (typically 1–4 weeks) with defined roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Development Team) and ceremonies (Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective). The Product Owner maintains and prioritizes a backlog; the team commits to a sprint goal and delivers a potentially shippable increment at each sprint's end. This model replaces long waterfall planning cycles with frequent delivery, enabling rapid response to user feedback and changing requirements.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Sprint — fixed iteration cycle (1–4 weeks) with a defined goal and scope
- Product Backlog — ordered list of all desired work maintained by the Product Owner
- Sprint Backlog — subset of backlog committed to in a given sprint
- Sprint Planning — ceremony to select and plan work for the upcoming sprint
- Daily Scrum — 15-minute daily sync on progress, plans, and impediments
- Sprint Review — demonstration of increment to stakeholders
- Sprint Retrospective — team reflection on process improvements
- Definition of Done — shared criteria for what "complete" means
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the sprint goal, and does every item in the sprint serve that goal?
- Are we delivering working software at the end of every sprint?
- What impediments are preventing the team from reaching full velocity?
- What did we learn from the Sprint Review that should change our backlog priorities?
- What process improvements emerged from the retrospective, and how are we tracking them?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Consistency of sprint goal clarity and team commitment
- Ratio of planned to completed sprint backlog items
- Quality and outcome-focus of Sprint Review feedback
- Number of actionable retrospective improvements implemented
- Velocity trend and predictability over time
**Typical use cases:**
- Software product development in cross-functional teams
- New feature development with evolving requirements
- Internal tool and platform development
- Hardware-software integrated product development
**Strengths and limitations:** Scrum's short cycles and frequent ceremonies create tight feedback loops, reduce integration risk, and make team health and blockers visible early. It is widely understood and supported by extensive tooling. Its limitations include ceremony overhead that can consume significant team time, the risk of "zombie scrum" where ceremonies continue without genuine inspection and adaptation, and difficulty scaling to large programs without additional frameworks like SAFe or LeSS.
**Related frameworks:** Kanban, Design Sprint, Lean Startup, SAFe, Shape Up, Continuous Discovery

### Design Sprint (Google Ventures)
**Category:** Design Thinking / Experimentation
**Tags:** GV, Jake Knapp, five days, prototype, test, decision-making, rapid validation
**How it works:** The Design Sprint is a five-day structured process developed at [Google Ventures by Jake Knapp](https://www.gv.com/sprint/), with contributions from John Zeratsky and Braden Kowitz. It compresses months of design and validation work into a single week by combining elements of Design Thinking, prototyping, and user testing. Monday is dedicated to mapping the problem and choosing a target. Tuesday involves sketching competing solution concepts. Wednesday brings a decision on which concept to prototype. Thursday is a rapid prototyping day to build a high-fidelity but fake prototype. Friday involves testing the prototype with five real target users and gathering qualitative learning. The sprint answers critical business questions before committing engineering resources, and is particularly powerful for high-stakes, high-uncertainty challenges.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Map (Monday) — understanding the problem space, long-term goal, and sprint target
- Sketch (Tuesday) — individual solution sketching inspired by existing ideas
- Decide (Wednesday) — structured decision process to choose the best concept
- Prototype (Thursday) — building a realistic-looking prototype quickly
- Test (Friday) — structured user interviews with five participants
- Sprint Master — facilitator responsible for process adherence
- Decider — executive sponsor with authority to make key decisions
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the most important question we need to answer this week?
- Who is the target user for Friday's test, and how will we recruit five of them?
- What is the long-term goal this sprint is serving?
- Does Thursday's prototype accurately simulate the user experience we are testing?
- What did we learn from Friday's tests, and does it change our strategy?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Clarity and specificity of the sprint question being answered
- Quality and diversity of solution sketches generated on Tuesday
- Fidelity of the prototype relative to the key interaction being tested
- Relevance and representativeness of Friday's user testing participants
- Actionability and strategic impact of learning generated
**Typical use cases:**
- Validating a new product concept before development begins
- Exploring solutions to a critical UX problem causing user churn
- Aligning cross-functional stakeholders through shared rapid prototyping
- Evaluating competing strategic directions for a product or service
**Strengths and limitations:** The Design Sprint is highly effective at forcing decision-making and generating validated learning within a one-week timeframe, breaking organizational inertia and creating shared understanding. It democratizes design by involving non-designers. Limitations include the logistical difficulty of assembling a full team for five consecutive days and the risk that the sprint question was too narrow or too broad. A prototype test with five users provides directional signal, not statistical significance.
**Related frameworks:** Design Thinking, Lean Startup, Double Diamond, Rapid Prototyping, User Testing, How-Might-We Statements

### Shape Up
**Category:** Product Management / Roadmap
**Tags:** Basecamp, Ryan Singer, betting table, six weeks, appetite, no sprints
**How it works:** Shape Up is Basecamp's product development methodology, documented by Ryan Singer. It rejects sprint-based agile in favor of six-week work cycles punctuated by two-week cooldowns. Work is "shaped" before it enters a cycle — meaning it has defined problem framing, rough solution concepts, and explicit risk reduction — but it is not broken into micro-tasks. The "Betting Table" is where senior stakeholders choose which shaped pitches to fund each cycle. Teams operate with full autonomy during cycles, with no daily standups and no sprint reviews. The "appetite" — how much time the team is willing to spend on a problem — is fixed; the scope is variable. Circuit breakers prevent projects from running indefinitely over cycle boundaries.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Shaping — upstream work to define the problem, rough solution, and risks before betting
- Appetite — the maximum time investment deemed appropriate for a given problem
- Pitch — a document presenting a shaped idea to the Betting Table
- Betting Table — the forum where leadership selects pitches for the coming cycle
- Six-Week Cycle — the core work unit; teams build and ship within the cycle
- Cooldown — two-week buffer for maintenance, exploration, and repairs between cycles
- Circuit Breaker — automatic cancellation of any work that does not ship within one cycle
**Guiding questions:**
- Is this problem worth spending six weeks on, or does it have a smaller appetite?
- Have we identified the rabbit holes (risks) before placing this bet?
- Is the pitch clear enough that a team can operate autonomously for six weeks?
- Does the shaped solution leave implementation details to the team?
- What are we explicitly choosing NOT to bet on this cycle?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Quality and completeness of shaping before betting
- Proportion of cycle bets that ship within the cycle
- Absence of runaway projects exceeding their appetite
- Autonomy and morale of teams operating within cycles
- Clarity of pitch documents relative to team comprehension
**Typical use cases:**
- Product development in small-to-medium-sized product companies
- Teams seeking an alternative to sprint ceremonies and estimation overhead
- Organizations where product leaders want more upstream influence on what gets built
**Strengths and limitations:** Shape Up eliminates much of the overhead of sprint ceremonies and restores upstream product thinking to the shaping phase. The appetite concept reframes conversations from "how long will it take?" to "how much is this worth?" Its limitations include reduced visibility for external stakeholders, difficulty integrating with cross-team dependencies, and challenges for teams accustomed to sprint accountability structures.
**Related frameworks:** Agile/Scrum, Kanban, Continuous Discovery, OKRs, Lean Startup, Now–Next–Later Roadmap

### North Star Metric Framework
**Category:** Product Strategy / Growth
**Tags:** single metric, growth model, Sean Ellis, product-led growth, input metrics, alignment
**How it works:** The North Star Metric (NSM) framework asks product teams to identify one metric that best captures the core value delivered to customers — a metric that, if consistently grown, reliably predicts long-term sustainable business success. The term was popularized by growth practitioners including Sean Ellis and Amplitude. The NSM sits at the top of a metric tree: teams then identify 3–6 "input metrics" (leading indicators that drive the NSM) that teams can directly influence through product and growth investments. This hierarchy aligns the entire organization around a shared definition of value creation and prevents local optimization of metrics that do not ladder up to genuine user value. The NSM should reflect both customer value and business sustainability.
**Dimensions / components:**
- North Star Metric — one metric reflecting core value delivered to customers
- Input Metrics — 3–6 leading indicators that directly drive the NSM
- Metric Tree — visual hierarchy showing NSM and its decomposition into inputs
- Lagging vs. Leading distinction — NSM often lags; input metrics are leading signals
- Product-Market Fit signal — a well-chosen NSM reveals PMF if growing organically
**Guiding questions:**
- Does this metric capture genuine value delivered to customers, not just business extraction?
- Is the NSM a lagging or leading indicator, and what drives it?
- What 3–6 input metrics most directly move the NSM?
- Are all teams across the organization clear on how their work contributes to the NSM?
- Is the NSM growing — and is that growth driven by improved user value or channel spending?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Reflection of genuine customer value (not just revenue or engagement)
- Sensitivity to product changes (does it move when the product improves?)
- Alignment with long-term business sustainability
- Clarity of ownership of each input metric across teams
- Resistance to gaming and metric inflation
**Typical use cases:**
- Establishing shared product strategy in growth-stage companies
- Aligning cross-functional teams on a common success definition
- Growth model design for product-led-growth companies
- Communicating product impact to investors and boards
**Strengths and limitations:** A well-chosen North Star Metric dramatically reduces misalignment and local optimization, and forces the organization to articulate what value it creates for customers. It can be transformative in companies previously focused only on financial metrics. Limitations include the difficulty of choosing the right metric — a poor NSM choice can mislead the entire organization — and the false simplicity of reducing complex multi-sided businesses to a single number.
**Related frameworks:** OKRs, AARRR Pirate Metrics, Growth Loop Model, Balanced Scorecard, Input–Output Metrics, Lean Analytics

### AARRR Pirate Metrics
**Category:** Growth / Experimentation
**Tags:** Dave McClure, acquisition, activation, retention, referral, revenue, funnel, growth hacking
**How it works:** AARRR — dubbed "Pirate Metrics" by [Silicon Valley investor Dave McClure](https://www.demandcurve.com/blog/growth-hacking) — is a product and marketing funnel framework that organizes growth levers into five sequential stages: Acquisition (how users find the product), Activation (first meaningful experience with the product), Retention (users returning repeatedly), Referral (users recommending to others), and Revenue (monetization of user value). Teams use the framework to identify which funnel stage is the biggest drag on growth and to focus optimization efforts accordingly. Each stage has associated metrics and experiments. AARRR is particularly useful for consumer and SaaS products and helps prevent over-investment in acquisition while ignoring downstream retention failures.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Acquisition — channels, CAC, organic vs. paid, traffic sources
- Activation — first "aha moment"; onboarding completion; initial value delivery
- Retention — DAU/MAU ratios, churn rate, engagement cohorts, habit formation
- Referral — NPS, viral coefficient, referral program performance, word-of-mouth
- Revenue — ARPU, LTV, conversion rate, expansion revenue, payback period
- Funnel conversion rates — the percentage flowing from each stage to the next
**Guiding questions:**
- Which stage of the funnel has the largest drop-off, and is that expected or fixable?
- Have we validated that activated users genuinely experienced the core value proposition?
- What is our retention curve shape, and does it flatten indicating retained users exist?
- Is referral occurring organically, and what triggers it?
- Is revenue growth driven by new acquisition or expansion from existing customers?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Data completeness and accuracy across all five stages
- Stage-by-stage conversion rate benchmarks vs. industry
- Identification of the bottleneck stage limiting total growth
- Quality of experiments designed to improve each stage
- LTV:CAC ratio as an overall model health indicator
**Typical use cases:**
- Growth model design for consumer apps and SaaS products
- Identifying and prioritizing growth experiments
- Communicating business model health to investors
- Onboarding analytics and activation optimization
**Strengths and limitations:** AARRR provides a complete, end-to-end view of the customer lifecycle in a single memorable model, enabling teams to diagnose systemic growth problems rather than optimizing locally. It is widely understood across growth, marketing, and product disciplines. Its main weakness is the sequential funnel metaphor, which implies linearity that modern user journeys often do not follow. It can also over-index on measurable digital signals and underweight qualitative experience factors.
**Related frameworks:** North Star Metric, Growth Loop Model, Customer Journey Map, OKRs, Lean Analytics, Cohort Analysis

### Growth Loop Model
**Category:** Growth / Product Strategy
**Tags:** viral loops, compounding growth, product-led growth, acquisition loop, engagement loop
**How it works:** The Growth Loop Model, developed and popularized by Reforge (Brian Balfour and Casey Winters), argues that the AARRR funnel model inadequately captures how the best growth systems actually work. Funnels are linear and extractive; loops are compounding and self-reinforcing. A Growth Loop is a closed system where one cohort of users, through their actions within the product, generates the inputs that acquire or activate the next cohort. Examples include viral loops (users invite contacts), content loops (users create content that attracts new users via search), and network effect loops (more users increase value for existing users). Teams design products to strengthen and multiply their core growth loops rather than optimizing individual funnel stages in isolation.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Input — the trigger that starts the loop (new user, content piece, purchase)
- Action — the behavior the product encourages that generates output
- Output — the artifact or signal produced by user action (invite, review, content)
- Reinvestment — the mechanism by which output generates new inputs
- Loop types — viral, content/SEO, paid, engagement, product, and user-generated content loops
- Loop strength — speed, conversion, and volume through each loop iteration
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the primary loop that drives user acquisition in our product today?
- Does user action within the product generate outputs that attract or activate new users?
- How fast does each loop cycle, and what are the key friction points reducing cycle speed?
- Are our growth loops compounding over time, or are we relying on linear paid acquisition?
- What new loops could we design into the product architecture?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Self-sufficiency of loops relative to paid channel dependence
- Cycle speed and compounding rate of primary loops
- Conversion rates at each loop stage
- Resilience of loops to external platform changes (algorithm updates, policy changes)
- Portfolio of multiple loops reducing single-loop dependency
**Typical use cases:**
- Product-led growth strategy design
- Consumer marketplace and social product architecture
- Content platform and community product strategy
- Reducing CAC by designing inherently viral product mechanics
**Strengths and limitations:** Growth Loops reframe product design from feature delivery to system design, producing insights that funnel optimization misses entirely. Companies that identify and strengthen their core loops achieve compounding rather than linear growth. The framework requires comfort with systems thinking and can be challenging for teams accustomed to feature-centric roadmaps. It also requires sufficient data to measure loop metrics accurately.
**Related frameworks:** AARRR Pirate Metrics, North Star Metric, Network Effects Framework, Viral Coefficient Model, Product-Led Growth, Flywheel Model
