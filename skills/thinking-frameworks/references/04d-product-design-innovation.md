### Iceberg Model of Systems Thinking
**Category:** Systems Design / Strategy
**Tags:** systems thinking, mental models, structures, patterns, events, root cause, iceberg
**How it works:** The Iceberg Model is a systems thinking tool drawn from the work of Jay Forrester and the system dynamics tradition, popularized in organizational contexts by Peter Senge's *The Fifth Discipline* and used prominently by [IDEO](https://www.ideou.com/blogs/inspiration/an-overview-of-our-best-design-thinking-strategy-frameworks) in their systems design practice. The model uses the iceberg metaphor to represent the layered nature of systemic problems: at the visible surface are Events (specific observable incidents); below the surface lie Patterns (trends over time); deeper still are Structures (the rules, relationships, and feedback loops that generate the patterns); and at the bottom are Mental Models (the beliefs, assumptions, and values that create the structures). Reacting only to events is the least effective intervention; changing mental models produces the deepest and most durable system change.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Events (visible) — specific observable incidents that trigger reactive responses
- Patterns/Trends — recurring behaviors over time that reveal a non-random signal
- Structures — the rules, relationships, feedback loops, and incentives generating the patterns
- Mental Models — the underlying beliefs, assumptions, and worldviews creating the structures
- Leverage points — systemic intervention opportunities identified at each level
- Reactive vs. generative responses — event-level vs. structural vs. mental-model interventions
**Guiding questions:**
- What patterns recur in this system over time that individual events obscure?
- What structures — policies, incentives, feedback loops — are generating these patterns?
- What mental models in the organization make these structures seem natural or inevitable?
- At what level of the iceberg could an intervention create the most durable system change?
- Whose mental models would need to shift for structural change to become politically possible?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Depth of analysis below event-level reactions
- Identification of reinforcing and balancing feedback loops in the structural layer
- Surfacing of implicit organizational mental models driving behavior
- Actionability of identified structural interventions
- Long-term durability of changes made at structural versus event level
**Typical use cases:**
- Organizational change management and transformation
- Root cause analysis for recurring product quality or process failures
- Strategic planning in complex adaptive systems (healthcare, education, government)
- Product ecosystem and platform strategy
**Strengths and limitations:** The Iceberg Model is a powerful counter to reactive management, shifting attention from symptom treatment to root cause intervention. It produces durable solutions by targeting structures and mental models rather than events. Its limitation is that deep systems analysis requires significant time and data, and changing mental models — the deepest layer — is an organizational challenge that often exceeds the scope of product teams working within normal planning horizons.
**Related frameworks:** Systems Map, Causal Loop Diagram, Double Diamond, Five Whys, Design Thinking, Service Blueprint

### Systems Map
**Category:** Service Design / Strategy
**Tags:** stakeholder map, systems thinking, IDEO, relationships, ecosystem, visualization
**How it works:** A Systems Map is a visual artifact that lays out all stakeholders, actors, and entities within a system and draws the relationships and flows between them — information, money, goods, influence, and decisions. Used extensively by IDEO as part of their [systems design practice](https://www.ideou.com/blogs/inspiration/an-overview-of-our-best-design-thinking-strategy-frameworks), Systems Maps help teams see the whole ecosystem they are designing within rather than optimizing a single touchpoint. Creation involves listing every relevant stakeholder, drawing arrows representing the nature and direction of key relationships, and reflecting on specific areas of the map to identify concentration of power, gaps, misaligned incentives, and opportunities for systemic change. They reveal interdependencies invisible to any single participant in the system.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Actors/Stakeholders — all entities with a role in the system (users, providers, regulators, partners)
- Relationship arrows — directed connections representing flows between stakeholders
- Flow types — information, money, value, goods, decisions, influence
- Concentration of power — nodes with disproportionate influence over system dynamics
- Gaps — missing relationships or absent actors that weaken the system
- Feedback loops — cycles within the map that either reinforce or balance system behavior
- Boundary definition — the explicit scope of what is inside vs. outside the system being mapped
**Guiding questions:**
- Have we included all actors who influence the system, including indirect or invisible ones?
- Where is value flowing, and where is it being captured versus destroyed?
- Which actors have disproportionate power, and how does that affect other actors' behavior?
- Where are the critical bottlenecks or gaps in the system that create friction?
- What changes in one part of the system would have the most significant ripple effects?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness of stakeholder identification
- Accuracy of relationship types and directions
- Identification of systemic leverage points for change
- Cross-validation of the map with stakeholders from different positions in the system
- Actionability of insights derived from map analysis
**Typical use cases:**
- Healthcare or government service system redesign
- Platform business strategy and ecosystem design
- Multi-stakeholder policy and social innovation programs
- Complex enterprise product strategy with multiple user types
**Strengths and limitations:** Systems Maps reveal the full complexity of the context products operate within, enabling designs that account for systemic dynamics rather than optimizing individual interactions. They are essential for multi-stakeholder service and platform design. Their limitation is that creating accurate, validated systems maps requires significant research effort and stakeholder access, and maps can become visually overwhelming without careful scope definition and simplification.
**Related frameworks:** Iceberg Model, Service Blueprint, Stakeholder Analysis, Customer Journey Map, Double Diamond, Causal Loop Diagram

### Strategy Process Map (IDEO)
**Category:** Product Strategy / Design Thinking
**Tags:** IDEO, strategy design, choices under uncertainty, strategic possibilities, scenario-driven
**How it works:** The Strategy Process Map is a design thinking–inspired strategic framework used by [IDEO](https://www.ideou.com/blogs/inspiration/an-overview-of-our-best-design-thinking-strategy-frameworks) to guide the creation of strategy by treating it as a creative design problem rather than an analytical exercise. Strategy is defined as the act of making choices under uncertainty and competition, and the process applies design thinking methods to strategic questions. The framework moves through identifying the strategic problem, framing a strategic question, generating a wide range of strategic possibilities, exploring what would have to be true for each possibility to succeed, identifying barriers and constraints, conducting empirical tests on the most promising possibilities, and finally choosing a strategic direction based on evidence.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Problem Identification — naming the strategic challenge the organization faces
- Strategic Question — reframing the problem as an open, generative strategic question
- Possibilities Generation — divergent exploration of multiple strategic directions
- "What Would Have to Be True" analysis — identifying conditions required for each possibility to succeed
- Barrier Identification — understanding what currently prevents each possibility from being viable
- Strategic Testing — conducting experiments or analysis to test critical assumptions
- Strategic Choice — evidence-informed selection of a strategic direction
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the precise strategic challenge we are facing — and have we avoided solving it before defining it?
- What are all the possible ways we could address this challenge, not just the obvious ones?
- For our most promising possibilities, what would have to be true about the market, customers, and our capabilities?
- What tests could quickly reveal which conditions are or are not actually true?
- Based on what we have learned, which strategic direction gives us the most confidence?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Sharpness and generativity of the strategic question framing
- Breadth of possibilities generated before evaluation begins
- Rigor of "What Would Have to Be True" analysis for each possibility
- Quality and speed of strategic tests conducted
- Clarity and confidence of the final strategic choice relative to evidence gathered
**Typical use cases:**
- Organizational strategy design at executive or leadership team level
- Product portfolio strategy when facing a major strategic inflection point
- Market entry and category strategy development
- Annual strategic planning processes seeking alternatives to traditional frameworks
**Strengths and limitations:** The Strategy Process Map brings the creativity and rigor of design thinking to strategic planning, preventing the common failure of selecting a strategic direction based on advocacy rather than evidence. The "What Would Have to Be True" reframe is particularly powerful for bypassing defensive debate. Its limitation is that it requires participants comfortable with ambiguity and the design thinking mindset — in highly analytical organizations, the approach may feel insufficiently quantitative without supplementary financial analysis.
**Related frameworks:** Design Thinking, Blue Ocean Strategy, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Scenario Planning, Innovation Ambition Matrix, OKRs

### Business Model Navigator
**Category:** Innovation / Strategy
**Tags:** Gassmann, Frankenberger, Csik, St. Gallen, 55 patterns, business model, recombination
**How it works:** The Business Model Navigator was developed by Oliver Gassmann, Karolin Frankenberger, and Michaela Csik at the University of St. Gallen and published in *The Business Model Navigator* (2014). Based on analysis of over 250 of the world's most successful business model innovations, the framework identifies 55 recurring business model patterns that account for 90% of successful innovations. Rather than inventing business models from scratch, teams apply the Navigator by mapping their current business model to the "magic triangle" (Who are the customers? What is the value proposition? How is value created? How is revenue generated?), then systematically exploring how established patterns — such as Freemium, Long Tail, Pay-per-Use, Razor and Blade, or Subscription — could be applied or adapted to their context.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Magic Triangle — the four dimensions of any business model: Who, What, How, Value
- 55 Business Model Patterns — catalog of proven, recurring models with examples
- Initiation — analysis of current business model and external environment
- Ideation — exploration of relevant patterns and analogical recombination
- Integration — shaping a new business model with unique value proposition
- Implementation — piloting and iterating the new model with market feedback
- Pattern matching — identifying which of the 55 patterns apply to the team's context
**Guiding questions:**
- How does our current business model map to the magic triangle?
- Which of the 55 patterns are most applicable to our industry and context?
- How could we combine multiple patterns in a novel way that competitors have not tried?
- What business model innovations in adjacent industries could be transferred to ours?
- What aspects of our current business model are ripe for reinvention?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Breadth of patterns considered before selecting candidates for exploration
- Novelty of pattern combinations relative to current industry models
- Feasibility of implementing the new model given existing resources and capabilities
- Customer value created by the new business model versus the current one
- Defensibility of the new model against rapid competitive imitation
**Typical use cases:**
- Business model innovation in established companies facing disruption
- New venture design where business model differentiation is a strategic priority
- Corporate innovation workshops seeking structured alternatives to brainstorming
- Strategic planning sessions exploring non-obvious competitive repositioning
**Strengths and limitations:** The Business Model Navigator provides a structured, research-backed approach to business model innovation that overcomes the blank-page problem by offering a catalog of proven patterns as inspiration. It is particularly effective for teams in mature industries who assume their business model is fixed. Its limitation is that pattern analogies can constrain rather than liberate thinking, and the 55-pattern catalog requires deep familiarity to use effectively.
**Related frameworks:** Business Model Canvas, Blue Ocean Strategy, Value Proposition Canvas, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Ten Types of Innovation, Lean Canvas

### Strategic Foresight and Scenario Planning
**Category:** Strategy / Innovation
**Tags:** futures thinking, Shell, scenarios, uncertainty, long-term planning, PESTLE, horizon scanning
**How it works:** Strategic Foresight is a structured practice for systematically exploring possible futures to inform present-day strategy and product decisions. Developed and institutionalized by Shell's scenario planning unit in the 1970s (under Pierre Wack and later Kees van der Heijeck) and extended by practitioners including the Institute for the Future, the approach involves scanning the external environment for driving forces and critical uncertainties, developing narrative scenarios representing distinctly different plausible futures, testing strategic options against each scenario, and identifying robust strategies that perform acceptably across multiple futures. [JD Meier](https://jdmeier.com/10-best-innovation-frameworks/) identifies Strategic Foresight as one of the ten best innovation frameworks for its ability to surface long-term risks and opportunities.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Environmental scanning — systematic monitoring of trends across PESTLE domains (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental)
- Driving forces — macro forces shaping the external environment
- Critical uncertainties — high-impact, low-predictability variables that determine future directions
- Scenario narratives — coherent, plausible stories about distinctly different futures
- Option stress-testing — evaluating current strategies against each scenario
- Robust strategies — options that deliver acceptable outcomes across multiple scenarios
- Early warning signals — leading indicators that a particular scenario is becoming reality
**Guiding questions:**
- What critical uncertainties, if they resolved differently, would create fundamentally different competitive environments?
- For each scenario, which of our current strategic bets would succeed and which would fail?
- What investments are robust across all plausible scenarios?
- What early signals would indicate that a specific scenario is emerging?
- What strategic options do we currently lack that we should build regardless of which scenario occurs?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Plausibility and distinctiveness of scenarios
- Comprehensiveness of environmental scanning inputs
- Quality of strategic option evaluation against each scenario
- Identification of robust strategies independent of scenario outcome
- Early warning signal monitoring system design
**Typical use cases:**
- 5–10 year corporate strategy development in uncertain industries
- Technology roadmap development for platform products
- Innovation portfolio design in sectors facing regulatory or technological disruption
- Investor and board strategy communication about uncertainty management
**Strengths and limitations:** Strategic Foresight prevents strategy myopia by forcing engagement with multiple plausible futures rather than a single predicted future. It was instrumental in Shell's navigation of the 1970s oil crisis. Its main limitation is the difficulty of maintaining scenario planning rigor over time — scenarios age quickly as environments evolve, and the practice requires sustained organizational commitment and skilled facilitation.
**Related frameworks:** McKinsey Three Horizons, Innovation Ambition Matrix, PESTLE Analysis, Blue Ocean Strategy, Strategic Process Map, OKRs

### Four Types of Strategy Stakeholders
**Category:** Product Strategy / Change Management
**Tags:** IDEO, stakeholder management, collaboration, strategy activation, change, influence
**How it works:** The Four Types of Strategy Stakeholders framework, developed by [IDEO](https://www.ideou.com/blogs/inspiration/an-overview-of-our-best-design-thinking-strategy-frameworks) as part of their strategy design practice, provides a taxonomy of the roles different people play in successfully designing and activating a strategy. The framework identifies Core Team (the people doing the work of creating the strategy), Extended Team (those critical to bringing the strategy to life in implementation), Subject Matter Experts (people whose specialized knowledge is needed to build, test, or validate the strategy), and Sponsors (those who must approve the strategy and whose active support is essential for organizational legitimacy). Identifying and intentionally engaging all four types prevents the common failure mode of strategy designed in isolation that lacks the expertise, buy-in, or sponsorship needed for activation.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Core Team — the primary team responsible for creating the strategy content
- Extended Team — implementation partners critical to strategy delivery
- Subject Matter Experts — specialized knowledge holders called in for specific inputs
- Sponsors — decision-makers and resource holders whose approval enables execution
- Engagement design — different involvement models for each stakeholder type
- Activation planning — strategies for converting stakeholder groups from passive recipients to active contributors
**Guiding questions:**
- Who needs to be in the core team to ensure the strategy reflects the full range of organizational reality?
- Who in the extended team will bear the burden of implementation but currently lacks context on the strategy's rationale?
- Which subject matter expertise are we missing that could change our strategic direction?
- Who are the sponsors who must actively champion this strategy for it to receive resources and legitimacy?
- How do we engage each stakeholder type in a way appropriate to their role and available time?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness of stakeholder identification across all four types
- Appropriateness of engagement intensity for each type
- Active (vs. passive) involvement of sponsors in strategy validation
- Subject matter expert involvement at critical decision junctures
- Post-strategy activation engagement with extended team
**Typical use cases:**
- Organization-wide strategy design and activation processes
- Product strategy development requiring cross-functional alignment
- Change management programs requiring broad organizational commitment
- Large-scale service redesign initiatives with multiple implementing departments
**Strengths and limitations:** This framework prevents the execution gap by designing stakeholder engagement into the strategy creation process rather than treating communication as an afterthought. It is particularly effective in large organizations where strategies frequently fail due to insufficient buy-in or missing expert input. Its limitation is that it provides a taxonomy without detailed guidance on how to engage each type — facilitators must draw on additional stakeholder engagement methods for implementation specifics.
**Related frameworks:** Strategy Process Map, Systems Map, Service Blueprint, OKRs, Balanced Scorecard, Change Management Frameworks

### Prioritization Scorecard (Weighted Scoring Model)
**Category:** Roadmap Prioritization
**Tags:** multi-criteria decision analysis, weighted criteria, portfolio management, product management
**How it works:** The Weighted Scoring Model is a multi-criteria prioritization technique that enables teams to rank features, initiatives, or projects by scoring them against multiple strategic criteria, each weighted by its relative importance to current organizational goals. Unlike RICE (which uses a fixed formula) or MoSCoW (which uses qualitative categories), the Weighted Scoring Model is fully customizable: teams select the criteria most relevant to their strategy (e.g., strategic alignment, revenue potential, customer satisfaction impact, technical risk, market differentiation), assign weights to each, score each item against each criterion, and calculate a weighted total score. Items are then ranked by total score. The model makes weighting decisions explicit and auditable.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Criteria selection — identifying the 4–8 factors most relevant to current strategic priorities
- Weight assignment — allocating relative importance to each criterion (weights sum to 100%)
- Item scoring — rating each candidate initiative against each criterion (typically 1–5 or 1–10)
- Weighted score calculation — (score × weight) summed across all criteria
- Rank ordering — items ranked by total weighted score
- Sensitivity analysis — testing how rankings change if weights are adjusted
**Guiding questions:**
- Which criteria reflect our actual current strategic priorities rather than historical habits?
- Are the assigned weights truly reflecting the relative importance of each criterion?
- Does the ranked output align with our intuitive strategic sense — and if not, which is more reliable?
- How sensitive are our rankings to modest changes in criterion weights?
- Are any criteria so highly correlated that they are double-counting the same underlying value?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Relevance and strategic alignment of selected criteria
- Team consensus on weight assignments before scoring begins
- Independence of criteria (minimizing redundancy)
- Calibration of scoring scales across participants
- Frequency of criterion and weight review as strategy evolves
**Typical use cases:**
- Quarterly product roadmap prioritization with multiple strategic objectives
- Portfolio investment allocation across business units
- Vendor and technology selection decisions
- Feature prioritization when multiple stakeholder groups have distinct criteria
**Strengths and limitations:** The Weighted Scoring Model is highly flexible and explicitly surfaces the trade-offs embedded in prioritization decisions, making the weighting choices available for debate and refinement. It accommodates diverse stakeholder criteria in a single framework. Its main weakness is the false precision its numerical outputs can imply — underlying scores remain subjective, and the model provides analytical structure rather than objective truth. Teams can unconsciously anchor weights to justify pre-determined preferences.
**Related frameworks:** RICE, WSJF, MoSCoW, Kano Model, Impact–Effort Matrix, OKRs

### Lean Portfolio Management
**Category:** Product Strategy / Roadmap Prioritization
**Tags:** SAFe, portfolio kanban, value streams, investment allocation, Epic, decentralized decisions
**How it works:** Lean Portfolio Management (LPM) is a SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) practice that applies Lean and Agile principles to enterprise portfolio governance, connecting strategy to execution through a continuous flow of Epics (the largest units of product work). LPM organizes work around Value Streams — the sequences of activities delivering value to customers — rather than functional departments. Three core competencies define LPM: Strategy and Investment Funding (aligning portfolio investments to strategy and funding value streams rather than projects), Agile Portfolio Operations (coordinating value streams and supporting operational excellence), and Lean Governance (maintaining guardrails and spending controls). The Portfolio Kanban visualizes and manages the flow of Epics from ideation through implementation.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Value Streams — sequences of steps that deliver continuous value to customers
- Portfolio Kanban — a visualization tool for managing Epic flow from funnel through implementation
- Epic — the largest unit of portfolio work; contains a lightweight business case
- Investment Themes — strategic investments guiding portfolio budget allocation
- Participatory Budgeting — decentralized, team-informed allocation of investment within themes
- Lean Governance — portfolio guardrails, metrics, and corrective action processes
- Program Increment (PI) Planning — quarterly planning events connecting portfolio strategy to team execution
**Guiding questions:**
- Are our investment allocations aligned with our declared strategic priorities — or has historical funding inertia taken over?
- Is the Portfolio Kanban making Epic flow and bottlenecks visible to leadership?
- Are we funding stable value streams rather than temporary projects, enabling continuous delivery?
- Which Epics in the funnel should never start based on current capacity and strategy?
- Is participatory budgeting empowering teams to make local investment decisions within strategic guardrails?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Alignment of portfolio investment proportions to strategic priorities
- Flow efficiency through the Portfolio Kanban
- Quality and completeness of Epic lightweight business cases
- Decentralization quality — are teams making better local decisions within strategic constraints?
- Speed from Epic identification to portfolio decision
**Typical use cases:**
- Enterprise organizations running multiple agile product teams
- SAFe adoption programs in large technology and financial organizations
- Portfolio governance reform replacing project-based with product-based funding models
- Investment allocation reform in organizations with historical silo-based budgeting
**Strengths and limitations:** Lean Portfolio Management addresses the critical gap between agile team-level practices and enterprise-level strategic governance, enabling organizations to be strategically coherent without micromanaging teams. It replaces project-based funding (which incentivizes local hoarding) with value-stream funding (which enables continuous delivery). Its limitation is that LPM requires significant organizational maturity and commitment to SAFe practices — partial implementations often fail to deliver the benefits.
**Related frameworks:** WSJF, OKRs, Agile/Scrum, SAFe, Now–Next–Later Roadmap, McKinsey Three Horizons

### Pirate Funnel Optimization (AARRR Growth Experiments)
**Category:** Growth / Experimentation
**Tags:** growth hacking, funnel optimization, Sean Ellis, experiment velocity, conversion rate optimization
**How it works:** Growth Experiments within the AARRR framework constitute a systematic discipline for identifying and testing growth levers across the full customer lifecycle through rapid, hypothesis-driven experimentation. The process involves identifying the highest-leverage funnel stage (where the largest drop-off or highest impact opportunity exists), formulating specific growth hypotheses for that stage, designing the minimum experiment to test the hypothesis, running the experiment with appropriate measurement, and learning quickly from results to inform the next experiment. Teams often operate a "growth backlog" of ranked experiment hypotheses and hold weekly "growth meetings" to review results and triage the next experiments. The emphasis is on experiment velocity — learning as fast as possible rather than running individual large campaigns.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Funnel stage identification — locating the highest-leverage optimization opportunity
- Growth hypothesis — a specific prediction: "If we [change], [metric] will improve by [amount]"
- Experiment design — the minimum intervention required to test the hypothesis
- Measurement setup — instrumentation for capturing experiment outcomes
- Statistical validation — appropriate rigor for the experiment's decision stakes
- Growth backlog — ranked queue of pending growth experiments
- Growth meeting cadence — weekly review of results and experiment triage
**Guiding questions:**
- Which funnel stage has the highest absolute drop-off and the most unexplored improvement opportunities?
- Is our growth hypothesis specific enough to be falsifiable with the data we can collect?
- Are we running experiments fast enough — multiple per week — to generate meaningful learning velocity?
- Are we documenting experiment results to build institutional memory and prevent re-running failed tests?
- Are our experiments revealing product insights, not just optimization surface wins?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Experiment velocity (experiments per week per growth area)
- Hypothesis specificity and falsifiability
- Statistical rigor appropriate to experiment stakes
- Documentation quality of results and learnings
- Rate of positive experiments leading to permanent product improvements
**Typical use cases:**
- Growth teams in consumer technology and SaaS companies
- Product-led growth programs optimizing onboarding and activation
- E-commerce conversion rate optimization programs
- Mobile app install and activation funnel optimization
**Strengths and limitations:** Systematic growth experimentation can produce compounding improvement across the full customer lifecycle while building organizational learning about customer behavior. Teams with high experiment velocity consistently outperform those relying on large, infrequent campaigns. Its limitation is the risk of local optimization — improving individual funnel metrics without improving overall user value or product quality — and the cultural requirement for leadership comfort with a high rate of experiment failure.
**Related frameworks:** AARRR, A/B Testing, North Star Metric, Growth Loop Model, Lean Startup, Product-Market Fit

### Service Design Thinking
**Category:** Service Design
**Tags:** Marc Stickdorn, Adam Lawrence, co-creation, iterative, multi-method, holistic, interdisciplinary
**How it works:** Service Design Thinking, synthesized by Marc Stickdorn, Jakob Schneider, and Adam Lawrence in *This Is Service Design Thinking* (2010) and expanded in *This Is Service Design Doing* (2018), is a human-centered, co-creative, and iterative approach to designing and improving services. It operates across five core principles: services should be designed from the user's perspective (user-centered), involve all stakeholders in design (co-creative), map the complete service experience over time (sequencing), consider both tangible and intangible service components (evidencing), and account for the full service system rather than individual touchpoints (holistic). Service Design Thinking integrates tools from UX, design thinking, systems thinking, ethnography, and business strategy into a unified multi-method practice.
**Dimensions / components:**
- User-centered — designed from the perspective of all users, including front-line staff
- Co-creative — involving diverse stakeholders (users, employees, partners) in the design process
- Sequencing — mapping the service experience as a temporal journey, not a set of touchpoints
- Evidencing — making intangible service qualities tangible through physical evidence and artifacts
- Holistic — considering the entire service ecosystem, not individual interaction points
- Iterative prototyping — rapidly testing service concepts through simulations and role-plays
**Guiding questions:**
- Are we designing with our users or for them — and have we included front-line service workers in the design process?
- Are we mapping the service as a sequence of experiences over time rather than a set of features?
- What tangible artifacts make the service quality visible and memorable to users?
- How does the backstage service system enable or limit the frontstage user experience?
- Are we prototyping service experiences holistically — including context, timing, and staff interaction — not just interfaces?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Depth of co-creation with users and service workers
- Completeness of temporal sequencing in journey documentation
- Quality and fidelity of service experience prototypes
- Holistic coverage of all service system elements in design scope
- Evidence of iterative testing and refinement before full implementation
**Typical use cases:**
- Healthcare patient experience redesign
- Government and public sector service transformation
- Hospitality, travel, and retail service innovation
- Bank, insurance, and financial service redesign
**Strengths and limitations:** Service Design Thinking is uniquely effective at bridging the gap between user experience design and organizational process design, producing service innovations that account for both customer and employee experience. Its holistic scope prevents the common failure of improving one touchpoint while degrading adjacent ones. Its limitation is the significant research and facilitation investment required to execute the co-creative, multi-method approach at scale.
**Related frameworks:** Service Blueprint, Customer Journey Map, Double Diamond, Design Thinking, Systems Map, Empathy Mapping

### Hypothesis-Driven Development
**Category:** Experimentation / Product Management
**Tags:** scientific method, product bets, falsifiable hypothesis, experiment, learning, validated
**How it works:** Hypothesis-Driven Development (HDD) is a product development practice that requires every product decision — feature addition, design change, pricing experiment, or messaging update — to be framed as a falsifiable hypothesis before implementation begins. Derived from the scientific method and popularized within the Lean UX and Lean Startup communities, HDD replaces "we are building feature X" with "we believe building feature X will cause users to do Y, resulting in outcome Z, and we will measure this with metric M." By making the expected causal chain explicit before building, teams clarify their reasoning, design appropriate measurements upfront, and know what evidence would confirm or deny the bet. HDD integrates naturally with A/B testing, feature flagging, and sprint-based delivery.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Hypothesis statement — "We believe [action] for [users] will achieve [outcome], measured by [metric]"
- Assumption identification — the beliefs underlying the hypothesis
- Minimum viable implementation — the smallest version that tests the hypothesis
- Measurement design — instrumentation and metrics defined before building begins
- Evaluation criteria — the specific evidence that would confirm or deny the hypothesis
- Post-experiment review — structured learning documentation regardless of outcome
**Guiding questions:**
- Can we articulate exactly what causal mechanism connects our proposed change to the expected outcome?
- What metric will change if our hypothesis is correct — and can we measure it reliably?
- What result would constitute disconfirmation of our hypothesis, and are we prepared to accept it?
- Are we designing measurements before building, not after?
- What did we learn from this experiment, regardless of whether the outcome was positive?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Specificity and falsifiability of hypothesis statements
- Pre-definition of success and failure criteria
- Instrumentation completeness before experiment launch
- Proportion of team's work covered by explicit hypotheses
- Quality of post-experiment learning documentation
**Typical use cases:**
- Feature development in data-rich product environments
- Product roadmap justification for engineering investment
- A/B test design and pre-registration
- Learning culture development in product organizations
**Strengths and limitations:** Hypothesis-Driven Development creates organizational learning discipline that prevents teams from building features whose value is assumed rather than validated, and it accumulates an institutional knowledge base of tested product bets. It also creates clarity for engineering and design teams about the purpose and success criteria of their work. Its challenge is cultural: hypothesis framing feels unnatural to teams accustomed to feature specs, and requires leadership that values learning from failures rather than punishing them.
**Related frameworks:** Lean Startup, A/B Testing, Lean UX, Assumption Mapping, Continuous Discovery, Build-Measure-Learn

### Diary Studies
**Category:** UX Research
**Tags:** longitudinal research, self-reporting, experience over time, qualitative, behavioral, remote
**How it works:** Diary Studies are a longitudinal qualitative research method in which participants self-report their experiences, behaviors, thoughts, and emotions related to a topic or product over an extended period — typically one to four weeks — using structured diary instruments (paper, app, or SMS-based). Unlike single-session research methods, diary studies capture behavior in natural context over time, revealing temporal patterns, habit formation, incidental use, and experience change that lab studies miss. They are particularly valuable for understanding infrequent events, multi-day workflows, or the evolution of attitudes and behaviors as users get comfortable with a new product. Entries are collected through prompted notifications and may include text, photos, audio, or video.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Structured prompts — standardized questions or frameworks guiding diary entries
- Entry frequency — prompted or self-triggered (hourly, daily, event-contingent)
- Duration — typically 1–4 weeks depending on the behavior frequency being studied
- Media types — text, photos, audio, video; choice depends on research goals
- Kickoff interview — baseline context-setting session with participants
- Closing interview — debrief interview interpreting diary entries with each participant
- Analysis — thematic coding of diary entries combined with interview debrief data
**Guiding questions:**
- What behavioral patterns are we trying to understand that require observation over multiple days or weeks?
- How frequently does the target behavior occur — and is that frequency compatible with a meaningful diary study duration?
- What diary entry prompts will elicit rich, specific, behavioral reports rather than vague opinions?
- How will we balance entry burden with data richness to maximize completion rates?
- What patterns in diary data will most surprise us relative to single-session research findings?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Participant completion rates throughout the diary period
- Richness and specificity of diary entry content
- Emergence of temporal patterns not visible in single-session research
- Quality of closing interview debrief in interpreting diary data
- Translation of diary insights into specific design or strategy decisions
**Typical use cases:**
- Understanding how users incorporate a new product into daily routines
- Tracking the experience of multi-day or multi-week workflows (e.g., financial planning, health management)
- Researching infrequent but high-stakes behaviors (e.g., product purchase decisions, emergency responses)
- Evaluating long-term experience change after a major product update
**Strengths and limitations:** Diary Studies capture the authentic texture of lived experience over time, revealing temporal dynamics and contextual factors that all single-session methods miss. They are the only practical way to study infrequent events or experience evolution. Their limitations include participant burden (attrition over time), self-report bias (participants may journal what they think researchers want to hear), and significant analysis effort. They also require careful prompt design to elicit useful entries.
**Related frameworks:** Contextual Inquiry, Experience Sampling Method, Think-Aloud Protocol, Empathy Mapping, Customer Journey Map, Ethnographic Research

### 5 Elements of Strategy Action Plans (IDEO)
**Category:** Product Strategy / Change Management
**Tags:** IDEO, strategy activation, resources, information flows, decision spaces, measures, people
**How it works:** The Five Elements of Strategy Action Plans is a framework developed by [IDEO](https://www.ideou.com/blogs/inspiration/an-overview-of-our-best-design-thinking-strategy-frameworks) to guide organizations in translating a chosen strategic direction into a concrete activation plan. Strategy without activation is decoration; this framework identifies the five organizational levers that must be intentionally designed for strategy to succeed. The elements are: Resources (allocating budget, time, and tools to strategy-critical activities), People (assembling teams with the right diversity of skills and knowledge), Information Flows (designing how strategy-relevant information reaches the right people at the right time), Decision Spaces (clarifying where teams have and do not have the authority to make decisions), and Measures (defining what success looks like at multiple time horizons).
**Dimensions / components:**
- Resources — financial guardrails, budget allocation, and tool provisioning
- People — team composition, diversity of expertise, and role clarity
- Information Flows — communication infrastructure ensuring the right information reaches the right people
- Decision Spaces — explicit boundaries of team authority and escalation pathways
- Measures — short, medium, and long-term success metrics aligned to strategic objectives
- Interdependency mapping — understanding how each element affects the others
**Guiding questions:**
- Have we allocated specific resources — budget and time — to each strategic priority, not just declared them?
- Does the team have the full range of skills and perspectives needed to execute this strategy?
- What information flows need to be designed or redesigned to support strategy execution?
- Where do teams currently have ambiguous authority, and how does that ambiguity slow execution?
- What does success look like at 90 days, 1 year, and 3 years — and are those measures in place?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Specificity of resource allocations to strategic activities
- Clarity of role and decision authority for all team members
- Completeness and appropriateness of information flow design
- Measurability and time-specificity of success measures
- Coherence and mutual reinforcement of all five elements
**Typical use cases:**
- Strategy activation planning following a strategic choice
- Organizational design for new product lines or business units
- Cross-functional program governance design
- Leadership team strategy execution workshops
**Strengths and limitations:** This framework prevents the most common strategy execution failure — a well-designed strategy that dies during activation because the organizational conditions for success were not intentionally designed. It is particularly powerful for leaders who excel at strategic thinking but underestimate the operational design required for execution. Its limitation is that each element requires its own deep implementation work, and the framework at this level provides the map rather than the territory.
**Related frameworks:** Strategy Process Map, Four Types of Strategy Stakeholders, OKRs, Balanced Scorecard, Change Management Frameworks, Systems Map

### Jobs-to-Be-Done — Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)
**Category:** Customer Discovery / Innovation
**Tags:** Tony Ulwick, unmet outcomes, opportunity algorithm, underserved, overserved, segmentation
**How it works:** Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) is the rigorous research methodology developed by Tony Ulwick at Strategyn that operationalizes the Jobs-to-Be-Done concept into a quantitative innovation process. Where JTBD identifies the job, ODI provides the systematic method for discovering which desired outcomes (the metrics customers use to measure job execution success) are underserved or overserved by existing solutions. Teams conduct large-sample surveys asking customers to rate each outcome's importance and satisfaction with current solutions. The "opportunity algorithm" (Importance + max[Importance − Satisfaction, 0]) scores each outcome, creating a ranked opportunity landscape. High-importance, low-satisfaction outcomes represent innovation opportunities; high-importance, high-satisfaction outcomes represent incumbent advantages to protect.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Job Map — the universal sequence of steps involved in performing the job
- Desired Outcomes — the 50–150 metrics customers use to measure job success
- Importance rating — how critical each outcome is to performing the job well
- Satisfaction rating — how well current solutions deliver each outcome
- Opportunity Score — Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0); scores above 10 = high opportunity
- Overserved outcomes — high satisfaction, low importance; cost-reduction targets
- Market segmentation by outcome — grouping customers by shared unmet outcome profiles
**Guiding questions:**
- For each step in the job map, what are all the outcomes customers use to judge success?
- Which outcomes score highest on the opportunity algorithm — and are we investing there?
- Are there entire market segments defined by a distinct set of underserved outcomes?
- Which outcomes are overserved, suggesting feature reduction or price reduction opportunities?
- How does our product's satisfaction score compare to competitors' on the highest-opportunity outcomes?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Sample size representativeness for statistical validity of opportunity scores
- Completeness of the desired outcome inventory across all job map steps
- Correlation between high-opportunity outcomes and current product roadmap priorities
- Market segment distinctiveness based on outcome importance clustering
- Longitudinal tracking of opportunity scores as product improves
**Typical use cases:**
- New product strategy in markets where customer needs are poorly understood
- Competitive repositioning based on uncovered unmet outcome clusters
- M&A target evaluation based on which outcomes an acquisition would help address
- Product line rationalization based on overserved outcome identification
**Strengths and limitations:** ODI provides the most rigorous and quantitative operationalization of JTBD, replacing guesswork with statistically grounded opportunity prioritization. It has a strong track record in B2B and medical device innovation. Its limitation is significant research investment — large surveys and complex analysis — that may be impractical for early-stage teams or rapid iterative contexts.
**Related frameworks:** Jobs-to-Be-Done, Kano Model, Value Proposition Canvas, Continuous Discovery, Customer Development, Prioritization Scorecard
