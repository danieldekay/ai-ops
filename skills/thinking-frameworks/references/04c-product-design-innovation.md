### McKinsey Three Horizons
**Category:** Innovation / Strategy
**Tags:** growth, portfolio, core business, emerging, options, time horizons, strategy
**How it works:** The Three Horizons of Growth framework was developed by Mehrdad Baghai, Stephen Coley, and David White and published in *The Alchemy of Growth* (1999). It organizes business activities into three time-based horizons: Horizon 1 covers the core business that generates current profits and must be defended and optimized; Horizon 2 includes emerging opportunities that could become significant revenue drivers within 2–5 years; and Horizon 3 represents seeds for the future — embryonic ideas, partnerships, or technologies that may transform the business over 5–10 years. The framework argues that companies must simultaneously manage all three horizons rather than sequentially, since Horizon 1 funds the exploration of Horizons 2 and 3, while Horizons 2 and 3 ensure continuity when the core matures or is disrupted.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Horizon 1 — core business; near-term revenue, optimization, and defense
- Horizon 2 — emerging opportunities; scaling and building tomorrow's core
- Horizon 3 — options and experiments; long-term bets and exploratory ventures
- Portfolio balance — simultaneous investment across all three horizons
- Horizon migration — the path by which H3 ideas become H2 businesses and eventually H1 core
- Time horizons — approximate timescales: H1 (now), H2 (2–5 years), H3 (5–10+ years)
**Guiding questions:**
- Is our H1 business generating sufficient cash to fund H2 and H3 investment?
- Which H2 opportunities are closest to generating meaningful revenue, and what do they need to cross the threshold?
- What H3 experiments are we running that hedge against H1 disruption?
- Are we making the mistake of applying H1 metrics and management practices to H3 ventures?
- How many years until our H1 business faces existential competitive or technological threat?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Explicit investment allocation across all three horizons
- Distinct management practices tailored to each horizon's maturity
- Rate of H2 opportunities graduating to H1 core over time
- Quality and diversity of H3 experiments as strategic hedges
- Organizational protection of H2 and H3 ventures from H1 resource pressure
**Typical use cases:**
- Corporate strategy and portfolio planning
- R&D investment prioritization
- Business unit strategy in mature industries facing disruption
- Investor communication about long-term growth pipeline
**Strengths and limitations:** Three Horizons provides an intuitive, boardroom-accessible framework for communicating the importance of simultaneous investment across time horizons and resisting the short-termism of pure H1 optimization. It has become a foundational strategy communication tool. A limitation noted by later scholars is that in fast-moving technology markets, the time horizons have compressed dramatically, and the sequential innovation assumption (H3 → H2 → H1) is increasingly violated by disruptive entrants that start at H3 and skip to H1.
**Related frameworks:** Innovation Ambition Matrix, Blue Ocean Strategy, Business Model Canvas, Stage-Gate, OKRs, Lean Portfolio Management

### Stage-Gate Process
**Category:** Innovation / Product Development
**Tags:** Robert Cooper, Scott Edgett, gates, NPD, structured review, portfolio management, CPG
**How it works:** The Stage-Gate process, co-developed by Dr. Robert G. Cooper and Scott J. Edgett in the 1980s and formalized by Cooper in his 1990 book *Winning at New Products*, is a structured project management framework for new product development. Projects pass through a series of predefined Stages (where work is done) separated by Gates (where management reviews progress and makes go/kill/hold decisions before authorizing advancement). The five standard stages are Discovery, Scoping, Business Case, Development, and Testing/Launch. At each Gate, projects are evaluated against explicit criteria by a "gate keeper" group of senior decision-makers, ensuring that only projects meeting strategic, commercial, and technical requirements advance. Stage-Gate is widely used in CPG, pharmaceutical, aerospace, and manufacturing industries.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Stage 1 (Scoping) — quick assessment of market opportunity and technical feasibility
- Stage 2 (Business Case) — detailed market and technical investigation; business case creation
- Stage 3 (Development) — product and process development; early customer testing
- Stage 4 (Testing and Validation) — comprehensive market, technical, and financial validation
- Stage 5 (Launch) — full commercialization and market launch
- Gates — decision points with defined deliverables and go/kill criteria
- Gate Keepers — senior leaders authorized to make advancement decisions
**Guiding questions:**
- Does the project meet strategic alignment criteria at each Gate?
- What is the commercial attractiveness relative to risk at this Gate?
- What evidence of customer demand has been gathered since the last Gate?
- Which projects should be killed or held based on Gate assessment?
- Is the development stage plan sufficiently detailed to execute with confidence?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness and quality of deliverables at each Gate
- Commercial attractiveness scores across multiple financial metrics
- Strength of technical and market validation evidence
- Strategic fit with portfolio priorities
- Speed through stages relative to planned timelines
**Typical use cases:**
- Physical product development in regulated industries (pharma, medical devices)
- Consumer packaged goods (CPG) innovation pipelines ([Zappi](https://www.zappi.io/web/blog/product-innovation-frameworks/))
- Aerospace and defense product programs
- Large-scale software platform initiatives requiring governance
**Strengths and limitations:** Stage-Gate provides rigorous governance and resource protection, ensuring that high-risk investments are validated before major spending commitments. It is excellent for managing large, complex product programs with regulatory requirements. Its key criticism is inflexibility — in fast-moving digital markets, sequential Stage-Gate cycles are too slow and the binary go/kill framing discourages experimentation and learning. Many organizations have adopted "Agile Stage-Gate" hybrids.
**Related frameworks:** Innovation Ambition Matrix, McKinsey Three Horizons, Lean Startup, Agile/Scrum, Business Case Framework, Portfolio Management

### Lean Canvas
**Category:** Customer Discovery / Innovation
**Tags:** Ash Maurya, business model, startup, problem-solution fit, MVP, Running Lean
**How it works:** The Lean Canvas, developed by Ash Maurya as a startup-optimized adaptation of Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas, replaces four BMC blocks (Key Activities, Key Resources, Key Partnerships, Customer Relationships) with four startup-critical blocks (Problem, Solution, Unfair Advantage, Key Metrics). The result is a one-page artifact that forces founders and early-stage product teams to articulate the problem being solved, the customer segments experiencing it, the proposed solution, the unique value proposition, acquisition channels, revenue and cost assumptions, success metrics, and the defensible advantage that makes this team uniquely suited to win. Lean Canvas is designed to be completed rapidly and updated frequently as learning invalidates assumptions.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Problem — top 1–3 problems the product solves; existing alternatives customers use today
- Customer Segments — target users and early adopter characteristics
- Unique Value Proposition — single, clear, compelling message that differentiates
- Solution — top 3 features addressing the defined problems
- Channels — paths to reach customers (online, offline, owned, paid, viral)
- Revenue Streams and Cost Structure — business model economics
- Key Metrics — measurable indicators of product and business health
- Unfair Advantage — something that cannot be easily copied or bought
**Guiding questions:**
- What specific problem are we solving, and who has it badly enough to change behavior?
- Who are the early adopters most willing to tolerate an imperfect first version?
- What is the one clear message that explains what we do and why it matters?
- What is our genuinely defensible advantage — not things any well-funded competitor could replicate?
- Which assumption on this canvas, if wrong, would kill the business?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Specificity and validation of the problem statement
- Distinctiveness of the Unique Value Proposition relative to existing alternatives
- Clarity and realism of the Unfair Advantage
- Identification of the riskiest assumption for prioritized testing
- Frequency of canvas updates driven by validated learning
**Typical use cases:**
- Early-stage startup validation and investor communication
- New product line inception within established companies
- Accelerator program and venture studio sprint processes
- Customer discovery workshops to align founding teams
**Strengths and limitations:** Lean Canvas is faster to complete than a full business plan and more startup-relevant than the original BMC, focusing on problem-solution dynamics rather than operational infrastructure. It is excellent for initial hypothesis articulation. Its limitations include the risk of creating a polished artifact that masks unvalidated assumptions, and the Unfair Advantage block is often left vague by founders who have not yet established defensibility.
**Related frameworks:** Business Model Canvas, Value Proposition Canvas, Customer Development, Lean Startup, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Opportunity Solution Tree

### Customer Development
**Category:** Customer Discovery / Lean Startup
**Tags:** Steve Blank, four steps, customer discovery, customer validation, company building
**How it works:** Customer Development is the methodology Steve Blank developed from his experience founding and studying startups, formalized in *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* (2005) and condensed in *The Startup Owner's Manual* (2012). It posits that most startup failures stem from building a product before validating customer and market assumptions. Customer Development consists of four phases: Customer Discovery (testing whether the identified problem exists and whether customers care), Customer Validation (testing whether a repeatable, scalable sales process exists for the solution), Customer Creation (driving end-user demand at scale), and Company Building (transitioning from startup to execution mode). The first two phases can loop multiple times before a startup earns the right to execute.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Customer Discovery — hypothesis formation and testing through direct customer conversations
- Customer Validation — proving the product can be sold and a repeatable sales model exists
- Customer Creation — demand creation and market entry execution
- Company Building — organizational scaling for execution
- Pivot — structured decision to change a fundamental business hypothesis based on discovery
- Minimum Feature Set — smallest product that enables Customer Validation
**Guiding questions:**
- What are the 8–10 most critical hypotheses underlying our business model?
- How many real customer conversations have we had before coding a single feature?
- Have we found a repeatable, scalable path to customer acquisition?
- Are we past Customer Validation before investing in demand creation?
- What did we discover that fundamentally changes our target customer or problem assumption?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Number of customer conversations per week during Discovery phase
- Quality of hypotheses formulated before customer outreach
- Rigor of criteria for declaring Discovery or Validation complete
- Speed of pivot decisions relative to contradicting evidence
- Distinction between pivot and natural iteration
**Typical use cases:**
- Early-stage startup product strategy
- New market entry by established companies
- Internal venture studio processes
- Accelerator programs and incubator curricula
**Strengths and limitations:** Customer Development is the foundational discipline that directly preceded the Lean Startup movement and remains essential for rigorously discovering and validating markets before execution. Its emphasis on getting out of the building to talk to customers directly counters the common startup failure mode of building in isolation. Its limitation is that the framework requires founders to be comfortable with ambiguity and rejection, and later phases (Customer Creation, Company Building) provide less specific tactical guidance.
**Related frameworks:** Lean Startup, Lean Canvas, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Continuous Discovery, Value Proposition Canvas, Design Thinking

### Problem-Solution Fit
**Category:** Customer Discovery / Product Strategy
**Tags:** fit, validation, early stage, MVP, desirability, problem worth solving
**How it works:** Problem-Solution Fit is the first major product-market milestone a new product must achieve: confirmation that a real, painful, and widespread problem exists AND that the proposed solution addresses it in a way customers find compelling and are willing to use. It precedes Product-Market Fit, which additionally requires evidence of scalable, repeatable growth. Teams achieve Problem-Solution Fit through customer interviews validating the problem's existence and urgency, qualitative prototype tests demonstrating that the solution concept resonates, and early behavioral indicators (waitlist signups, pre-orders, pilot usage) showing intent beyond verbal agreement. Without Problem-Solution Fit, investing in growth and scale produces efficient delivery of the wrong value.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Problem validation — confirming the problem exists, is frequent, and is important enough to solve
- Target early adopters — identifying the segment most acutely affected and most willing to try new solutions
- Solution concept testing — qualitative evaluation of solution resonance with the problem
- Behavioral intent signals — pre-orders, signups, pilot commitments indicating genuine interest
- Problem-Solution Hypothesis Canvas — documentation of current assumptions
- Qualitative vs. quantitative evidence — both are necessary for confidence
**Guiding questions:**
- How many people with this problem have we actually spoken to — not assumed, but interviewed?
- Is the problem we're solving actively hurting people now, or is it a latent aspiration?
- When we describe our solution concept, do customers lean forward or lean back?
- What behavioral evidence (not opinion) do we have that customers will use this solution?
- Have we found the early adopters who have the problem the worst and have tried the most alternatives?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Volume and quality of problem validation interviews
- Consistency of problem urgency across multiple independent informants
- Quality of solution concept testing artifacts used
- Behavioral (not just verbal) evidence of solution pull
- Clarity on who the specific early adopter segment is
**Typical use cases:**
- Validating a new startup concept before MVP development
- Evaluating a new product initiative in an established company
- Investor pitch preparation grounded in evidence
- Product team alignment on what problem to solve before roadmap creation
**Strengths and limitations:** Problem-Solution Fit is a simple but powerful forcing function that prevents teams from investing in building solutions for problems that are not real or not valued. It establishes a rigorous evidentiary baseline for product decisions. Its limitation is the difficulty of defining definitive criteria for "fit achieved" — it is a milestone with fuzzy edges, and teams can both declare fit prematurely or never feel confident enough to proceed to building.
**Related frameworks:** Customer Development, Lean Startup, Value Proposition Canvas, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Lean Canvas, Product-Market Fit

### Product-Market Fit
**Category:** Product Strategy / Growth
**Tags:** Marc Andreessen, Sean Ellis, retention, PMF, growth readiness, product-led
**How it works:** Product-Market Fit (PMF) is the degree to which a product satisfies a strong market demand. Coined by Marc Andreessen in 2007, PMF describes the state in which a product resonates so deeply with a target market that the market pulls the product forward — users return organically, recommend enthusiastically, and resist churning. Sean Ellis proposed a practical PMF measurement: surveying users with "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" and achieving at least 40% responding "very disappointed" as a leading PMF indicator. Andy Rachleff added the dimension of product positioning — PMF requires both the right value proposition AND the right customer segment. Before PMF, growth spending is wasteful; after PMF, growth compounds.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Retention curve — a flat retention curve (stabilizing above zero) is the primary quantitative signal
- Sean Ellis "very disappointed" survey — 40%+ threshold as qualitative PMF indicator
- NPS — Net Promoter Score as a supplementary signal
- Organic growth — word-of-mouth and referral as behavioral PMF evidence
- Time-to-PMF — the duration of discovery and iteration required
- Segment and value proposition specificity — PMF is always fit within a specific segment
**Guiding questions:**
- Does our retention curve flatten, indicating a stable cohort of genuinely retained users?
- What percentage of users would be "very disappointed" if our product disappeared?
- Are users referring others without incentive — the strongest PMF signal?
- Are we currently trying to grow before achieving PMF — a common and costly mistake?
- Have we identified the exact segment and use case where PMF exists, even if it is narrow?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Retention curve shape (flattening vs. asymptotic decline to zero)
- Sean Ellis survey threshold achievement (40%+ very disappointed)
- NPS trajectory and benchmark comparison
- Organic referral rate and viral coefficient
- Sales cycle length and conversion rate as PMF indicators in B2B
**Typical use cases:**
- Startup investment readiness evaluation
- Decision to shift from discovery to growth investment
- Product positioning refinement to identify the segment of strongest fit
- Post-launch evaluation of a new product or market entry
**Strengths and limitations:** Product-Market Fit provides a conceptually clear milestone that separates discovery from growth phases and prevents premature scaling, one of the most common startup failure modes. The Sean Ellis survey provides an actionable quantitative proxy. PMF's limitation is ambiguity — it is easier to recognize retrospectively than to declare with confidence in real time, and in B2B contexts the 40% threshold may not be appropriate across all sales motions.
**Related frameworks:** Problem-Solution Fit, North Star Metric, AARRR, Lean Startup, Customer Development, Continuous Discovery

### Creative Problem Solving (CPS)
**Category:** Creativity / Innovation
**Tags:** Osborn-Parnes, Alex Osborn, Sidney Parnes, divergent thinking, convergent thinking, ideation
**How it works:** Creative Problem Solving is a structured methodology for generating creative solutions to problems, originally developed by Alex Osborn (originator of brainstorming) and Sidney Parnes in the 1950s at Buffalo State College and updated multiple times since. Modern CPS (the Osborn-Parnes model updated by the Creative Education Foundation) comprises six stages alternating between divergent and convergent thinking: Objective Finding, Fact Finding, Problem Finding, Idea Finding, Solution Finding, and Acceptance Finding. Each stage opens up possibilities before narrowing to the most promising directions. The framework is deliberately meta-cognitive — participants become aware of which mode of thinking (diverging or converging) they are in and respect the rules of each mode.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Objective Finding — clarifying the desired goal or outcome
- Fact Finding — gathering data and information about the situation
- Problem Finding — reframing and defining the core problem or challenge
- Idea Finding — generating a wide range of potential solutions without judgment
- Solution Finding — evaluating and strengthening ideas into actionable solutions
- Acceptance Finding — developing an implementation plan and gaining stakeholder buy-in
- Divergent phase rules — defer judgment, generate quantity, build on others' ideas
- Convergent phase rules — be affirmative, keep novelty alive, check goals
**Guiding questions:**
- Are we clearly in divergent or convergent mode at each stage — and are we respecting that boundary?
- Have we reframed the problem multiple times before settling on a definition?
- How many ideas did we generate before beginning to evaluate?
- Which ideas, if combined or built upon, become more powerful than any single idea alone?
- What implementation obstacles did Acceptance Finding reveal, and how do we address them?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Quantity and diversity of ideas generated in Idea Finding
- Quality of problem reframes produced in Problem Finding
- Criteria used in Solution Finding to evaluate and strengthen ideas
- Actionability and stakeholder alignment of the Acceptance Finding output
- Facilitator skill in managing transitions between divergent and convergent modes
**Typical use cases:**
- Innovation workshops and organizational problem-solving sessions
- Product discovery ideation phases
- Leadership strategy retreats
- Education and organizational development programs
**Strengths and limitations:** CPS is one of the most thoroughly researched and documented creative process frameworks, with over six decades of evidence supporting its effectiveness. Its explicit alternation between divergent and convergent phases prevents premature judgment from killing creative ideas. Its limitations include the time investment required to execute all six stages fully and the need for skilled facilitation to manage divergent-convergent transitions effectively.
**Related frameworks:** Design Thinking, Six Thinking Hats, Brainstorming, SCAMPER, How-Might-We Statements, Lateral Thinking

### SCAMPER
**Category:** Creativity / Ideation
**Tags:** Bob Eberle, Alex Osborn, attribute listing, analogical thinking, product improvement, creative technique
**How it works:** SCAMPER is a structured ideation checklist developed by Bob Eberle, building on a list of idea-generating verbs originally compiled by Alex Osborn. The acronym stands for seven cognitive operations applied systematically to an existing product, service, or process to generate improvement ideas and innovations: Substitute (replace components, materials, or rules), Combine (merge with another product or service), Adapt (adjust for a different purpose or context), Modify/Magnify/Minify (change scale, form, or attributes), Put to other uses (apply in unexpected ways), Eliminate (remove elements), and Reverse/Rearrange (flip or reorder components). SCAMPER is used as a structured prompt system that prevents ideation from stalling by providing systematic cognitive triggers.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Substitute — what elements can be replaced with something different?
- Combine — what can be merged or integrated with another product or idea?
- Adapt — how can this be adapted from another context or product?
- Modify / Magnify / Minify — what can be enlarged, reduced, or otherwise changed?
- Put to other uses — what other applications does this solution have?
- Eliminate — what features, steps, or components can be removed without losing core value?
- Reverse / Rearrange — what happens if we flip or reorder the components?
**Guiding questions:**
- If we substituted the most expensive component, what would we use instead?
- What would happen if we combined our product with a completely different category of tool?
- How do products in a completely different industry handle this exact type of problem?
- What would our product look like if we eliminated half its current features?
- What entirely different user group might find value in what we've built?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Number of novel ideas generated per SCAMPER operation
- Diversity of SCAMPER operations actively applied (not just Substitute and Eliminate)
- Quality of the most promising ideas surfaced across all seven operations
- Integration of SCAMPER outputs into subsequent ideation and evaluation phases
- Team comfort with the technique after multiple facilitated sessions
**Typical use cases:**
- Product improvement ideation sessions
- Feature brainstorming for existing product evolution
- Workshop ideation phases within Design Thinking or Design Sprint processes
- Creativity training and team ideation capability building
**Strengths and limitations:** SCAMPER is fast, lightweight, and produces a reliable volume of ideas from relatively constrained creative inputs. It is particularly effective for product improvement tasks where there is an existing artifact to operate on. Its limitation is that it is less suited to genuinely blank-slate innovation where there is no existing product to apply the operations to, and it tends to produce incremental rather than transformational ideas.
**Related frameworks:** Brainstorming, How-Might-We Statements, Creative Problem Solving, TRIZ, Analogical Thinking, Lateral Thinking

### Open Innovation
**Category:** Innovation / Strategy
**Tags:** Henry Chesbrough, external ideas, crowdsourcing, ecosystem, co-creation, R&D
**How it works:** Open Innovation, coined by Henry Chesbrough in his 2003 book of the same name, challenges the "closed innovation" model in which companies generate, develop, and commercialize all innovations internally. Chesbrough argues that in a world of abundant knowledge, companies that leverage external ideas, technologies, and paths to market alongside internal resources will outpace those that rely on internal R&D alone. Open Innovation flows inward (external ideas used in internal development) and outward (internal ideas commercialized through external partners). Mechanisms include licensing, spin-offs, joint ventures, crowdsourcing platforms, corporate accelerators, hackathons, and open-source contributions. The Four C Model (Connect, Capture, Create, Commercialize) provides a process framework for implementation.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Inbound Open Innovation — licensing external IP, acquiring startups, tapping customer communities for ideas
- Outbound Open Innovation — licensing unused IP, spinning out ventures, publishing non-core research
- Connect — building external networks with customers, startups, universities, and suppliers
- Capture — tapping collective intelligence through open calls, challenges, and hackathons
- Create — co-developing and selecting innovations with external partners
- Commercialize — prototyping, scaling, and distributing innovations through partner channels
- Innovation ecosystem — the network of external partners that feed the innovation funnel
**Guiding questions:**
- What valuable knowledge and ideas exist outside our organization that we are not accessing?
- What internal IP are we sitting on that could be commercialized through licensing or spin-out?
- Which external partners — startups, universities, customers — have the deepest insight into our key innovation challenges?
- How do we structure IP ownership and incentive alignment for co-created innovations?
- What open innovation mechanisms (hackathons, accelerators, licensing) are most appropriate to our industry?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Number and quality of external idea sources integrated into the innovation pipeline
- ROI from inbound licensing and partnership versus internal R&D investment
- Speed of technology access through external sources versus internal development
- Quality of ecosystem partner relationships and reciprocity
- Revenue generated from outbound licensing and spin-out activities
**Typical use cases:**
- Corporate R&D strategy for large enterprises in technology-intensive industries
- Startup ecosystem engagement programs (corporate accelerators)
- Pharmaceutical licensing and co-development partnerships
- Consumer co-creation and community innovation platforms
**Strengths and limitations:** Open Innovation dramatically expands the idea generation surface available to organizations and can accelerate time-to-innovation by leveraging external investment and expertise. It is especially valuable in industries where no single firm can master all relevant technologies. Its challenges include IP protection complexity, cultural resistance in organizations with "not invented here" tendencies, and the management overhead of ecosystem relationships.
**Related frameworks:** Business Model Canvas, Innovation Ambition Matrix, Ten Types of Innovation, Blue Ocean Strategy, Customer Co-Creation, Jobs-to-Be-Done

### 10X Thinking (Moonshot Thinking)
**Category:** Innovation / Creativity
**Tags:** Google X, Astro Teller, moonshot, radical goals, constraint-breaking, breakthrough thinking
**How it works:** 10X Thinking, associated with Google's X moonshot factory and popularized by figures including Astro Teller and Larry Page, challenges teams to pursue solutions that are ten times better than current approaches rather than ten percent improvements. The premise is that 10X goals require fundamentally different approaches — they cannot be achieved by incremental optimization of existing solutions, forcing teams to challenge every underlying assumption about how a problem should be approached. [JD Meier](https://jdmeier.com/10-best-innovation-frameworks/) identifies 10X Thinking as applicable across strategic planning, innovation, product development, and personal development. The moonshot factory model institutionalizes 10X by explicitly funding high-risk, high-impact projects that most organizations would abandon.
**Dimensions / components:**
- 10X goal articulation — defining the target as 10x improvement on the current benchmark
- Assumption dismantling — identifying and challenging constraints assumed to be fixed
- Constraint reframing — converting apparent limitations into design parameters
- Rapid learning cycles — failing fast on 10X ideas to preserve organizational learning
- Moonshot factory structure — organizational model for housing 10X projects safely
- Breakthrough vs. incremental distinction — recognizing when a goal requires a new approach
**Guiding questions:**
- If we had to achieve 10x improvement rather than 10%, which assumptions about our approach would have to be false?
- What resources or technologies, if they existed, would make 10x achievable?
- Which constraints do we treat as laws of physics that are actually just industry conventions?
- How do we structure a 10X project to fail fast and learn without destroying the core business?
- What does the best possible version of this look like, regardless of current feasibility?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Degree of assumption challenging versus incremental optimization
- Ambition of the stated goal relative to current industry benchmarks
- Speed and efficiency of learning cycles when 10X approaches fail
- Number of fundamentally different approaches explored
- Long-term breakthrough potential relative to shorter-term resource investment
**Typical use cases:**
- Strategic innovation in technology, healthcare, and energy companies
- Corporate moonshot programs and skunkworks projects
- Platform product strategy aimed at category creation
- Research and development investment framing
**Strengths and limitations:** 10X Thinking breaks the gravitational pull of incrementalism and forces genuinely new approaches that often produce breakthroughs even when the 10X goal itself is not achieved. It is powerful for culture and aspiration-setting. Its limitation is that without disciplined organizational support and protected funding, 10X projects are typically killed by short-term performance pressure. It also requires high tolerance for failure and long time horizons that most corporate environments do not sustain.
**Related frameworks:** Blue Ocean Strategy, Innovation Ambition Matrix, McKinsey Three Horizons, Design Thinking, First Principles Thinking, Open Innovation

### Workflow-to-Opportunities Framework
**Category:** UX Research / Product Discovery
**Tags:** workflow analysis, friction mapping, jobs-to-be-done, pain points, feature ideation, CPO Club
**How it works:** The Workflow-to-Opportunities Framework, described by [CPO Club](https://cpoclub.com/product-development/design-frameworks/), is a UX-grounded product discovery technique that systematically analyzes pre-defined customer workflow diagrams to surface friction points, pain points, and unmet needs, then converts those observations into structured opportunities for new product features or improvements. Teams distribute workflow diagrams to workshop participants, who spend 10–15 minutes independently annotating the workflow with observations across four categories: friction points, pain points, major needs, and points of satisfaction. Annotations are then synthesized using affinity mapping and dot voting to prioritize the most impactful opportunities for subsequent ideation phases. The framework bridges user research and feature ideation by keeping opportunity identification anchored to observable user work.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Pre-defined workflow diagram — a visual representation of the current user process (physical or digital)
- Friction points — moments of unexpected resistance or inefficiency in the workflow
- Pain points — sources of genuine user frustration or failure
- Major needs — capabilities the workflow reveals are missing or underserved
- Points of satisfaction — workflow moments users find effective (to preserve in redesigns)
- Annotation phase — independent participant mapping (10–15 minutes)
- Affinity mapping and dot voting — synthesis and prioritization of observations
**Guiding questions:**
- Where does the workflow require more steps, workarounds, or tool switches than users expect?
- Which pain points appear across multiple independent observers' annotations?
- What are users doing outside the product (in spreadsheets, emails, paper) that the product should support?
- Which points of satisfaction in the current workflow must be preserved in any redesign?
- What are the highest-voted pain points that, if resolved, would have the greatest user impact?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Accuracy and completeness of the workflow diagram as a research artifact
- Independence and quality of participant annotations
- Prioritization rigor in the affinity mapping phase
- Conversion rate of identified opportunities into actionable How-Might-We statements
- Coverage of all customer segments in workflow selection
**Typical use cases:**
- Enterprise software product discovery for complex operational workflows
- Process redesign for internal tools used by employees
- Identifying high-value feature opportunities in product roadmap planning
- Facilitating cross-functional product discovery workshops
**Strengths and limitations:** Workflow-to-Opportunities grounds feature ideation in observable user work rather than stakeholder opinion or feature requests, producing opportunities that are directly linked to documented user experience. It works well in workshop settings with diverse participants. Its limitation is that workflow diagrams must be prepared and validated before the session, requiring prior research investment, and the method may miss opportunities outside the documented workflow scope.
**Related frameworks:** Affinity Mapping, How-Might-We Statements, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Contextual Inquiry, Customer Journey Map, Opportunity Solution Tree

### Structured Innovation Workshop
**Category:** Creativity / Product Discovery
**Tags:** time-boxing, facilitated ideation, cross-functional, offsite, product workshop, design session
**How it works:** Structured Innovation Workshops, described by [CPO Club](https://cpoclub.com/product-development/design-frameworks/) as a time-boxed workshop approach to product ideation, impose structure on creativity by defining problem statements in advance, assigning roles, sequencing activities with explicit time boxes, and generating concrete deliverables rather than conversation. A well-designed innovation workshop moves through problem discovery (equalizing participant understanding of the customer), problem analysis (prioritizing and quantifying problems), and problem solving (generating and prioritizing solutions) phases, each with dedicated activities, timers, and facilitation guidance. The time-boxing principle is central: by constraining duration, the workshop prevents endless refinement and forces decisive output from divergent thinking.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Problem Discovery phase — aligning participant understanding of customer context
- Problem Analysis phase — quantifying urgency and identifying the most impactful problems
- Problem Solving phase — translating validated problems into prioritized development opportunities
- Time-boxing — explicit time limits on every activity to force decisive output
- Facilitation — a designated facilitator managing energy, participation, and transitions
- Diverse participant mix — PM, design, engineering, marketing, customer success
- Deliverables — concrete outputs (prioritized opportunity list, HMW statements, concept sketches)
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the specific deliverable this workshop must produce — and is it achievable in the time allotted?
- Are participant roles and the workshop agenda communicated before the session?
- Is the problem discovery phase grounded in customer research artifacts, not stakeholder opinion?
- Is the time-boxing discipline strict enough to force decisions without feeling rushed?
- How will workshop outputs feed directly into the next phase of the product development process?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Clarity and specificity of workshop objectives and deliverables
- Participant diversity relative to the problem being explored
- Adherence to time-boxing discipline throughout the session
- Quality and actionability of outputs produced
- Integration of workshop outputs into subsequent product decisions
**Typical use cases:**
- Product discovery kickoff sessions for new initiatives
- Quarterly product strategy and roadmap planning
- Cross-functional product team alignment on customer problems
- New product concept generation in established companies
**Strengths and limitations:** Structured Innovation Workshops compress months of fragmented ideation into focused, high-energy sessions that produce concrete outputs and build cross-functional alignment simultaneously. Time-boxing creates productive creative urgency. Their limitation is that workshop quality is highly facilitator-dependent, and without strong preparation (customer research, personas, workflow diagrams) the sessions can generate well-structured outputs that are not grounded in genuine customer insight.
**Related frameworks:** Design Sprint, How-Might-We Statements, Workflow-to-Opportunities, Affinity Mapping, Creative Problem Solving, Brainstorming

### Jobs Innovation Theory (Disruptive Innovation)
**Category:** Innovation / Strategy
**Tags:** Clayton Christensen, disruption, new-market foothold, low-end disruption, incumbent trap
**How it works:** Disruptive Innovation theory was developed by Clayton Christensen and introduced in *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997). It describes the mechanism by which new entrants displace incumbents: disruptors typically target either overserved customers (low-end disruption — offering "good enough" performance at lower cost) or non-consumers (new-market disruption — making a product accessible to people who previously could not use it at all). Incumbents rationally ignore disruptors initially because they are attacking unprofitable segments, but the disruptors improve over time and eventually capture the core market. Understanding disruption helps established companies identify where they are vulnerable and where genuine disruptive opportunities exist — as opposed to sustaining innovations (better products for existing customers), which incumbents typically win.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Sustaining innovations — improvements targeting existing customers on established performance metrics
- Low-end disruption — attacking the overserved fringe with simpler, cheaper solutions
- New-market disruption — creating consumption among non-consumers
- Performance trajectory — disruptor improvement rate exceeding customer need growth
- The innovator's dilemma — rational incumbent behavior that accelerates displacement
- Foothold market — the beachhead segment where disruption begins
**Guiding questions:**
- Are there customer segments in our market who are overserved by current solutions and would gladly trade performance for lower cost?
- Who currently cannot afford or access a solution in our space — and could a simpler version serve them?
- How quickly are potential disruptors improving, and are they on a trajectory to enter our core market?
- Which of our innovation investments are sustaining (high-margin, core) versus disruption-enabling?
- Are we making the mistake of dismissing low-end competitors because they are unprofitable today?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Accuracy of market segmentation separating overserved from appropriately served customers
- Quality of trajectory analysis comparing disruptor improvement to customer need growth
- Identification of genuine new-market non-consumption opportunities
- Organizational willingness to cannibalize existing business to capture disruption opportunity
- Historical accuracy of disruption predictions versus actual market outcomes
**Typical use cases:**
- Competitive strategy assessment for established firms
- Identifying market entry strategies for new ventures in incumbent-dominated markets
- Corporate innovation portfolio design (protecting against disruption while pursuing disruptive bets)
- Investment thesis evaluation for venture capital
**Strengths and limitations:** Disruptive Innovation theory is one of the most empirically validated and practically influential business frameworks of the past 30 years, explaining countless incumbent failures and guiding disruptive market entry strategies. Its limitation is that the theory has been applied so broadly (including to cases it does not actually fit) that its boundaries have been contested by Christensen himself. It is also backward-looking by nature — it is easier to identify disruption after the fact than to predict it prospectively.
**Related frameworks:** Blue Ocean Strategy, McKinsey Three Horizons, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Innovation Ambition Matrix, Lean Startup, Value Chain Analysis

### Lean UX
**Category:** UX Research / Product Management
**Tags:** Jeff Gothelf, Josh Seiden, hypothesis-driven, cross-functional, continuous learning, outcomes
**How it works:** Lean UX, developed by Jeff Gothelf and Josh Seiden and documented in *Lean UX: Designing Great Products with Agile Teams* (2013), applies Lean Startup principles to UX design practice, shifting the focus from deliverable-based UX (wireframes, specifications) to outcome-based design informed by continuous validated learning. The process begins with a problem statement and hypothesis articulation: "We believe [doing this] for [these people] will achieve [this outcome]. We will know we are right when [measurable signal] changes." Teams then build the minimum viable experience to test the hypothesis, measure outcomes, and refine. Lean UX integrates design, product, and engineering as a collaborative cross-functional trio rather than a handoff chain.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Problem statement — the business context and outcome the team is pursuing
- Assumption identification — the beliefs underlying the proposed design direction
- Hypothesis statement — the falsifiable prediction connecting design action to business outcome
- Minimum Viable Product/Feature — the smallest experiment that tests the hypothesis
- Collaborative design — design as a team sport involving PM and engineering from the start
- Outcomes over outputs — measuring user behavior change, not design delivery
- Continuous learning — hypothesis testing is ongoing, not a phase that ends
**Guiding questions:**
- Can we articulate our design intent as a falsifiable hypothesis?
- What is the smallest design we can build to test this hypothesis meaningfully?
- Are we measuring behavioral outcomes or just design deliverable completeness?
- Is the full product trio collaborating on design from the start, or is design a handoff?
- What did we learn from the last experiment that is changing our current design direction?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Quality and specificity of hypothesis statements
- Minimum viable fidelity of experiments relative to test objectives
- Integration of design, engineering, and product in collaborative sessions
- Outcome metrics selected for measurement versus output metrics
- Rate of hypothesis testing and learning cycles per sprint
**Typical use cases:**
- Cross-functional product teams working in agile sprint environments
- Replacing documentation-heavy UX processes with collaborative lean practices
- Feature development where user behavior outcomes are the success measure
- Startup teams without dedicated UX research resources
**Strengths and limitations:** Lean UX dramatically reduces UX documentation overhead and integrates design learning into the delivery cycle, preventing the "design handoff" failure mode where carefully researched designs are implemented incorrectly. It builds shared product understanding across the trio. Its limitation is that it requires genuine cross-functional collaboration culture — in organizations with siloed design processes, Lean UX can become an excuse to skip rigorous research rather than a method for embedding it.
**Related frameworks:** Design Thinking, Lean Startup, Agile/Scrum, Continuous Discovery, Hypothesis-Driven Development, Assumption Mapping
