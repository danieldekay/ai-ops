### ISO 31000:2018 Risk Management Framework
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** enterprise risk, principles-based, governance, ISO standard, international
**How it works:** ISO 31000:2018, published by the International Organization for Standardization, establishes internationally accepted principles and guidelines for integrating risk-based decision-making into governance, planning, management, reporting, policies, values, and culture ([NAVEX](https://www.navex.com/en-us/blog/article/risk-management-frameworks-for-organizations/)). The framework is non-prescriptive and technology-neutral, making it applicable to any organization regardless of type, size, or sector. It centers on a continuous, iterative risk management process anchored by leadership commitment and embedded throughout the organization. Risk practitioners use it to create, protect, and realize value by systematically identifying uncertainties that affect objectives, assessing their likelihood and consequence, and choosing proportionate responses. The 2018 revision placed new emphasis on human and cultural factors, iterative learning, and top-down commitment. It does not mandate specific controls but provides a reference architecture within which more specific frameworks operate.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Principles (value-creating, leadership, inclusive, iterative, best-available information)
- Framework (leadership commitment, integration, design, implementation, evaluation, improvement)
- Risk management process (communication, scope, assessment, treatment, monitoring, recording)
- Context establishment (internal, external, risk criteria)
- Risk identification (sources, events, causes, consequences)
- Risk analysis (likelihood, consequence, level of risk)
- Risk evaluation (comparing results against criteria)
- Risk treatment (modify, avoid, share, retain)
**Guiding questions:**
- Are risk management principles embedded in all organizational processes and decision-making?
- Has leadership formally committed resources and accountability for risk governance?
- Are risk criteria regularly reviewed against evolving business objectives?
- Does the organization learn from monitoring outcomes and adjust the framework accordingly?
- Are human and cultural factors explicitly considered in risk assessments?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Degree to which risk management is integrated into strategic and operational planning
- Clarity and adequacy of mandate, commitment, and resource allocation
- Quality of communication and consultation practices across stakeholders
- Frequency and rigor of framework evaluation and improvement cycles
- Alignment of risk criteria with organizational objectives and stakeholder values
**Typical use cases:**
- Enterprise-wide risk program design or redesign
- Setting the governance baseline before applying sector-specific frameworks
- Preparing organizations for ISO 31000 alignment assessments
- Harmonizing multiple departmental risk approaches under a single architecture
**Strengths and limitations:** ISO 31000's sector-agnostic design makes it one of the most broadly applicable risk frameworks available. Its principles-based nature allows organizations to tailor implementation to context, avoiding one-size-fits-all prescriptions. The framework's emphasis on leadership integration and iterative improvement aligns closely with modern enterprise risk thinking. However, its deliberate non-prescriptiveness means it provides limited operational guidance on how to actually execute risk assessments or select controls—organizations must supplement it with technical standards. Smaller organizations may also find the framework abstract without concrete implementation tools.
**Related frameworks:** COSO ERM, NIST RMF, ISO/IEC 27001, FAIR, King IV, AS/NZS 4360

### COSO ERM — Integrating with Strategy and Performance
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** enterprise risk, COSO, strategy, governance, internal control, principles-based
**How it works:** The Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission (COSO) updated its Enterprise Risk Management framework in 2017 to more tightly link risk management with strategy-setting and business performance ([NAVEX](https://www.navex.com/en-us/blog/article/risk-management-frameworks-for-organizations/)). The framework establishes five interrelated components and 20 underlying principles that guide how organizations identify and manage risk in pursuit of value creation. Unlike the original 2004 COSO ERM model, the 2017 revision explicitly addresses the relationship between risk appetite, strategic alternatives, and business performance—acknowledging that risk is inherent in strategy selection, not merely in execution. Organizations use it to align risk appetite with strategy, enhance risk-informed resource allocation, and improve board-level transparency about the entity's risk profile relative to its performance goals. The 2017 revision explicitly linked risk management to enterprise strategy, recognizing that organizations with poor risk integration consistently underperform peers.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Governance and culture (board oversight, culture, core values)
- Strategy and objective-setting (risk appetite, business context, strategy formation)
- Performance (risk identification, assessment, prioritization, risk responses)
- Review and revision (substantive changes, risk performance review)
- Information, communication, and reporting (data, technology, communication flows)
**Guiding questions:**
- Is the organization's risk appetite explicitly articulated and linked to strategic alternatives?
- Does board oversight include regular review of how risk profiles change with strategic shifts?
- Are risk responses selected considering cost, risk appetite, and performance impact?
- How does the organization detect substantive changes in its internal and external context?
- Is risk information communicated with sufficient clarity and frequency to decision-makers?
- Are culture and core values actively managed as risk-influencing factors?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Integration of risk appetite into strategy-setting decisions
- Quality and timeliness of risk information flowing to boards and senior management
- Completeness of risk identification across all five components
- Alignment of performance targets with the entity's risk profile
- Evidence of cultural reinforcement of risk accountability
**Typical use cases:**
- Board-level enterprise risk governance programs
- Strategic planning processes requiring formal risk appetite articulation
- SOX Section 302/404 internal control program design
- Annual enterprise risk assessments reported to audit committees
- Integration of risk into business unit performance scorecards
**Strengths and limitations:** COSO ERM's 2017 revision elevated risk management from a compliance function to a strategic discipline, making it highly relevant for boards and senior executives. Its 20 principles provide a structured yet flexible basis for tailoring implementation. The framework's wide adoption by publicly listed companies makes it a common language for audit committees and regulators. Its limitations include significant implementation complexity for smaller organizations, reliance on subjective risk appetite definitions, and limited prescriptive guidance on quantification methods. Organizations often combine it with ISO 31000 or FAIR for more technical rigor.
**Related frameworks:** ISO 31000, FAIR, Balanced Scorecard, SOX Internal Control, King IV, NIST RMF

### COSO Internal Control — Integrated Framework
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** internal control, financial reporting, SOX, COSO, audit, governance
**How it works:** The COSO Internal Control — Integrated Framework (updated 2013) provides a comprehensive model for designing, implementing, and evaluating internal control systems. Originally developed in response to the Treadway Commission's recommendations, it defines internal control as a process effected by people at every level of an organization to provide reasonable assurance regarding achievement of objectives across operations, reporting, and compliance ([ZenGRC](https://www.zengrc.com/blog/how-the-coso-framework-helps-you-comply-with-sox/)). The framework's five components and 17 principles, supported by 87 points of focus, allow management and boards to assess control design and operating effectiveness. It underpins Sarbanes-Oxley Section 404 attestation requirements in the United States and serves as the global reference architecture for internal control assessment. Auditors, controllers, and chief audit executives rely on it to structure control testing, remediation, and disclosure.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Control environment (tone at the top, integrity, competence, accountability)
- Risk assessment (organizational and fraud risk identification and analysis)
- Control activities (authorizations, reconciliations, segregation of duties)
- Information and communication (relevant, quality information; internal and external communication)
- Monitoring activities (ongoing evaluations, separate evaluations, deficiency reporting)
**Guiding questions:**
- Does the control environment reflect the organization's commitment to integrity and competence?
- Are fraud risks explicitly identified and addressed within the risk assessment process?
- Are control activities appropriately designed and operating effectively across all levels?
- Is information of sufficient quality and timeliness available for decision-making?
- Are control deficiencies promptly identified, communicated, and corrected?
- Do monitoring activities include both continuous and periodic separate evaluations?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Presence and operating effectiveness of controls addressing the 17 principles
- Completeness of fraud risk assessment and response
- Quality of management's documentation and evidence of control operation
- Timeliness of deficiency reporting to appropriate levels of management
- Alignment of controls with financial reporting reliability objectives
**Typical use cases:**
- SOX 302 and 404 compliance programs
- Internal audit risk-based audit planning
- Financial reporting reliability assessments
- Design of entity-level and process-level controls
- Regulatory examination preparation for financial institutions
**Strengths and limitations:** The framework's 17 principles and 87 points of focus provide unparalleled granularity for control design and testing, making it the gold standard for internal control assessment globally. Its three-category objective structure cleanly separates operations, reporting, and compliance concerns. However, the framework's depth can create compliance-focused, checklist-oriented implementations that miss the underlying spirit of control. Organizations sometimes treat the 87 points of focus as mandatory requirements rather than illustrative guidance. Integration with modern automated control environments requires significant supplementation.
**Related frameworks:** COSO ERM, SOX, COBIT 2019, ISO 31000, IIA Standards, PCAOB AS 2201

### FAIR — Factor Analysis of Information Risk
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** quantitative risk, cyber risk, financial quantification, information risk, FAIR Institute
**How it works:** FAIR (Factor Analysis of Information Risk) is an international standard and quantitative model for understanding, analyzing, and measuring cyber risk and operational risk in financial terms ([NAVEX](https://www.navex.com/en-us/blog/article/risk-management-frameworks-for-organizations/)). Developed by Jack Jones and standardized through the Open Group, FAIR decomposes risk into two primary components — probability of loss and magnitude of loss — and then breaks each component into specific contributing factors that can be measured and modeled. Rather than producing qualitative heat-map ratings, FAIR generates probability distributions of financial loss, enabling risk-informed resource allocation, insurance decisions, and cost-benefit analyses for security investments. Organizations use FAIR to answer the fundamental business question: "How much risk do we have, and what is it worth to reduce it?" FAIR analyses are increasingly used in board-level risk presentations and cyber insurance underwriting.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Loss event frequency (threat event frequency × vulnerability)
- Threat event frequency (contact frequency × probability of action)
- Vulnerability (threat capability vs. control strength)
- Loss magnitude (primary loss + secondary loss)
- Primary loss (productivity, response, replacement, competitive advantage, fines, reputation)
- Secondary loss (secondary stakeholder reactions and their financial impacts)
- Risk (probable frequency and probable magnitude of future loss)
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the probable frequency of a threat agent successfully executing an attack?
- How capable are the relevant threat agents relative to existing control strength?
- What are the primary and secondary financial losses associated with a loss event?
- How does reducing a specific control gap translate to a change in financial risk exposure?
- Can risk reduction investments be prioritized based on cost versus risk reduction value?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Availability and quality of data inputs for frequency and magnitude estimates
- Use of probability distributions rather than single-point estimates
- Ability to communicate results in financial terms to non-technical stakeholders
- Calibration of estimates against historical loss data
- Consistency of FAIR taxonomy application across analyses
**Typical use cases:**
- Cyber risk quantification for board and C-suite reporting
- Security investment prioritization using cost-benefit analysis
- Cyber insurance limit adequacy assessment
- Third-party risk financial exposure modeling
- Regulatory capital modeling for operational risk (Basel alignment)
**Strengths and limitations:** FAIR's financial quantification approach bridges the gap between technical security teams and business leadership, producing outputs that resonate with CFOs and boards. It forces analysts to make explicit, calibrated estimates rather than relying on subjective color ratings. The Open Group FAIR standard also provides a common taxonomy that enables comparison across organizations. However, FAIR requires significant data collection and analytical skill, making shallow implementations prone to garbage-in, garbage-out errors. Organizations often struggle to obtain quality frequency and magnitude data, and the model's complexity can create a false sense of precision.
**Related frameworks:** ISO 31000, NIST CSF, COSO ERM, OpenFAIR, Value-at-Risk (VaR), Operational Risk Basel Framework

### ISO/IEC 27001 Information Security Management System
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** information security, ISMS, ISO standard, certification, controls, risk treatment
**How it works:** ISO/IEC 27001 is the globally recognized standard for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an Information Security Management System (ISMS). It specifies requirements for assessing information security risks and implementing appropriate controls selected from Annex A (now aligned with ISO/IEC 27002:2022, which organizes controls into four themes: organizational, people, physical, and technological). The standard follows the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) management system cycle embedded in the ISO High-Level Structure, enabling integration with ISO 9001, ISO 22301, and other management systems. Certification by an accredited body signals to customers, regulators, and partners that an organization has systematically identified its information security risks and implemented a structured treatment program under formal management oversight. The standard was revised in 2022 to restructure controls into four themes and strengthen supply chain security requirements.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Context of the organization (internal/external issues, interested parties, scope)
- Leadership and commitment (policy, roles, responsibilities)
- Planning (risk assessment, risk treatment plan, Statement of Applicability)
- Support (resources, competence, awareness, communication, documented information)
- Operation (risk assessment execution, risk treatment implementation)
- Performance evaluation (monitoring, measurement, internal audit, management review)
- Improvement (nonconformities, corrective actions, continual improvement)
- Annex A controls (93 controls across organizational, people, physical, technological themes)
**Guiding questions:**
- Has the organization defined an ISMS scope that reflects its information risk boundaries?
- Are information security risks assessed against defined criteria for acceptable risk?
- Does the Statement of Applicability justify all included and excluded Annex A controls?
- Are control owners clearly defined and accountable for treatment effectiveness?
- Does management review include performance data sufficient to drive continual improvement?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness and adequacy of the risk assessment process
- Traceability from risks to selected controls in the Statement of Applicability
- Evidence of management review and decision-making based on performance data
- Internal audit coverage and independence
- Certification body conformity assessment results
**Typical use cases:**
- Achieving ISO/IEC 27001 certification to satisfy customer or regulatory requirements
- Building a structured information security program from the ground up
- Demonstrating security posture to enterprise customers during vendor due diligence
- Aligning with GDPR data security obligations
- Merging with SOC 2 or NIST CSF programs for complementary assurance
**Strengths and limitations:** ISO/IEC 27001 certification provides globally recognized, independently verified assurance of security management maturity, making it highly valued in international supply chains. The PDCA structure ensures controls are not static but subject to regular review and improvement. Integration with other ISO management systems reduces overhead for multi-standard organizations. However, certification scope can be narrowly defined to cover only a portion of the organization, potentially misleading stakeholders about overall posture. The standard's control-centric view can focus effort on documentation compliance rather than actual risk reduction outcomes.
**Related frameworks:** ISO/IEC 27002, NIST CSF, SOC 2, GDPR, ISO 31000, COBIT 2019

### NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** NIST, federal, cybersecurity, information system risk, authorization, FISMA
**How it works:** The NIST Risk Management Framework, described in NIST SP 800-37 Revision 2, provides a structured, disciplined, and repeatable process for integrating security and privacy risk management activities into the system development life cycle ([Splunk](https://www.splunk.com/en_us/blog/learn/risk-management-frameworks.html)). Originally developed for U.S. federal agencies to satisfy FISMA requirements, the RMF's seven-step lifecycle — Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, and Monitor — ensures that security controls are selected, implemented, assessed, and monitored continuously throughout a system's life. The Authorize step, which requires a senior authorizing official to formally accept residual risk, distinguishes NIST RMF from other frameworks by mandating explicit human accountability for risk decisions. The framework links directly to the NIST SP 800-53 control catalog for federal systems and NIST CSF for broader applicability.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Prepare (establish roles, risk strategy, organization-level risk assessment)
- Categorize (system impact level based on FIPS 199/NIST SP 800-60)
- Select (baseline controls from NIST SP 800-53, tailor as needed)
- Implement (apply controls, document implementation)
- Assess (test and evaluate control effectiveness)
- Authorize (authorization decision by senior official, plan of action and milestones)
- Monitor (ongoing assessment, reporting, change management, authorization maintenance)
**Guiding questions:**
- Has the system been categorized at the appropriate impact level based on data sensitivity and criticality?
- Are selected controls tailored and supplemented to address system-specific risks?
- Has control assessment been conducted by independent parties where required?
- Does the authorizing official have sufficient risk information to make an informed authorization decision?
- Are continuous monitoring activities triggering timely re-authorization when changes occur?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness and accuracy of system categorization
- Alignment of selected controls with system impact level and identified risks
- Independence and rigor of control assessment
- Quality and currency of the authorization package documentation
- Effectiveness of continuous monitoring in detecting and addressing changes
**Typical use cases:**
- Federal agency ATO (Authority to Operate) processes under FISMA
- Defense contractor system authorization (often combined with CMMC)
- Cloud service provider FedRAMP authorization
- State and local government cybersecurity programs aligned with federal standards
- Healthcare and financial sector adoption of NIST SP 800-53 control catalog
**Strengths and limitations:** NIST RMF's mandatory authorization step creates explicit senior-official accountability for residual risk, a governance feature absent in many other frameworks. Its direct linkage to NIST SP 800-53's extensive control catalog provides implementation specificity. The framework's broad adoption in the federal sector creates a large community of practice and training resources. However, the RMF's procedural rigidity and documentation burden can slow system delivery and create compliance-over-security cultures. The ATO process can become a point-in-time compliance event rather than a reflection of ongoing security posture without robust continuous monitoring.
**Related frameworks:** NIST SP 800-53, NIST CSF, FISMA, FedRAMP, CMMC, ISO/IEC 27001

### OCTAVE — Operationally Critical Threat, Asset, and Vulnerability Evaluation
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** operational risk, asset-based, self-directed, Carnegie Mellon, information security
**How it works:** OCTAVE, developed by the Software Engineering Institute at Carnegie Mellon University and first published in 1999, is a holistic, self-directed risk assessment methodology for identifying, analyzing, and managing information security risks ([Splunk](https://www.splunk.com/en_us/blog/learn/risk-management-frameworks.html)). Unlike externally driven assessment approaches, OCTAVE is designed to be facilitated internally by small, cross-functional teams that understand the organization's mission, operations, and risk tolerance. The methodology focuses on three primary questions: what critical assets does the organization rely on, what threats exist to those assets, and what vulnerabilities could be exploited. Three variants exist — OCTAVE (for large organizations), OCTAVE-S (streamlined for small organizations), and OCTAVE Allegro (focused on information assets in their full operational context). Results inform the development of protection strategies and risk mitigation plans tailored to the organization's specific operational context.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Phase 1: Build asset-based threat profiles (identify critical assets, threats, security requirements)
- Phase 2: Identify infrastructure vulnerabilities (technology components and their weaknesses)
- Phase 3: Develop security strategy and plans (risk analysis, protection strategy development)
- Critical asset identification and prioritization
- Threat profiling (threat actors, motives, outcomes)
- Vulnerability analysis (infrastructure, organizational practices)
- Risk characterization and prioritization
**Guiding questions:**
- Which organizational assets are most critical to the mission and what are their security requirements?
- Who are the relevant threat actors and what outcomes do they seek against each critical asset?
- What infrastructure vulnerabilities could enable identified threats to succeed?
- How does the organization's current security practice align with identified protection needs?
- Which risks warrant immediate mitigation versus acceptance or monitoring?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness of critical asset identification across operational areas
- Credibility and specificity of threat profiles for each asset
- Accuracy of vulnerability identification through both automated scanning and process review
- Feasibility of recommended protection strategies given organizational constraints
- Quality of risk prioritization relative to mission impact
**Typical use cases:**
- Organizational information security risk assessments where external consultants are not feasible
- Small and medium enterprise security program development
- Pre-assessment preparation before ISO 27001 or NIST RMF implementation
- Government agency self-assessments of operationally critical systems
- Educational institutions assessing research data and student information risks
**Strengths and limitations:** OCTAVE's self-directed design empowers internal teams and builds lasting risk management competence rather than creating consultant dependency. Its asset-centric approach ensures that risk assessment is grounded in operational reality rather than abstract threat taxonomies. The Allegro variant's streamlined process is accessible to organizations without deep security expertise. Limitations include the potential for internal teams to have blind spots regarding their own organization's vulnerabilities, difficulty scaling to large and complex environments, and a relatively limited treatment of quantitative risk analysis compared to FAIR.
**Related frameworks:** FAIR, NIST RMF, ISO/IEC 27001, ISO 31000, TARA, NIST SP 800-30

### TARA — Threat Assessment and Remediation Analysis
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** MITRE, systems security, threat assessment, cyber risk, remediation
**How it works:** TARA (Threat Assessment and Remediation Analysis) is a component of MITRE's systems security engineering practices used to recognize, evaluate, and select countermeasures against cyber threats and weaknesses ([Splunk](https://www.splunk.com/en_us/blog/learn/risk-management-frameworks.html)). The methodology uses three curated libraries — the Threat Agent Library (TAL), Methods and Objectives Library (MOL), and Common Exposure Library (CEL) — to systematically map threat agents to their methods, objectives, and applicable exposures before recommending countermeasures. TARA's six-step process moves from risk identification through baseline establishment, threat-agent objective and method identification, exposure determination, and strategic alignment of security measures. The framework is particularly suited to systems-engineering contexts where security must be designed into complex systems early in development rather than applied retrospectively. TARA's library-based approach enables rapid, repeatable threat analysis without building threat models from scratch for every system.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Threat Agent Library (TAL) — characterizes common threat agents by capability and intent
- Methods and Objectives Library (MOL) — describes threat agent attack methods and goals
- Common Exposure Library (CEL) — catalogs known vulnerabilities and exposures
- Risk identification and baselining
- Threat-agent-to-exposure mapping
- Countermeasure selection and cost-benefit evaluation
**Guiding questions:**
- Which threat agents are most relevant to the system based on capability and intent alignment?
- What attack methods and objectives do relevant threat agents employ?
- Which exposures in the system are accessible by identified threat agents using their methods?
- What is the residual risk after applying candidate countermeasures?
- How do countermeasure costs compare to their risk-reduction value?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness of threat agent coverage relative to system deployment environment
- Accuracy of exposure identification against the CEL
- Rigor of countermeasure cost-benefit analysis
- Integration of TARA outputs into system design decisions
- Traceability from threat agent to exposure to selected countermeasure
**Typical use cases:**
- Defense and intelligence system security engineering
- Critical infrastructure protection planning
- Systems-of-systems threat assessment in complex acquisition programs
- Early-stage security architecture analysis for high-assurance systems
- Countermeasure selection under budget constraints
**Strengths and limitations:** TARA's library-based approach provides systematic, repeatable threat assessment without requiring analysts to recreate threat catalogs from scratch. Its integration with MITRE's broader systems security engineering body of knowledge makes it well-suited to complex defense and critical infrastructure programs. The separation of threat agents, methods, and exposures into distinct libraries promotes analytical rigor. However, the libraries require regular updates to remain current with evolving threat landscapes, and the framework's complexity may exceed the needs of commercial organizations with less sophisticated threat environments.
**Related frameworks:** MITRE ATT&CK, STRIDE, OCTAVE, NIST SP 800-30, FAIR, ISO/IEC 27005

### King IV Report on Corporate Governance
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** corporate governance, South Africa, board oversight, integrated thinking, stakeholder
**How it works:** The King IV Report on Corporate Governance for South Africa, released in 2016, represents the fourth iteration of the King Committee's governance code and is widely regarded as one of the most progressive governance frameworks globally. King IV shifted from a compliance-based approach (King III) to an outcomes-based model organized around four governance outcomes — ethical culture, good performance, effective control, and legitimacy — achieved through 16 principles applied on an "apply and explain" basis. The framework explicitly integrates risk governance with strategy, stakeholder relationships, and integrated reporting, recognizing that sustainable value creation requires governing risk across financial, social, environmental, and ethical dimensions. Its principles apply across the full governance ecosystem, including boards, management, audit, risk, and remuneration committees. King IV introduced the concept of integrated thinking, connecting governance with sustainability, ethics, and value creation across all stakeholder relationships.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Governance outcomes (ethical culture, performance, control, legitimacy)
- Leadership, ethics, and corporate citizenship
- Strategy, performance, and reporting
- Governing structures and delegation (board, CEO, board committees)
- Governance functional areas (risk governance, technology and information governance, compliance, remuneration, assurance)
- Stakeholder relationships
- Institutional investors (applied separately for investors)
**Guiding questions:**
- Does the board govern risk in a way that supports achieving the four governance outcomes?
- Is the organization's risk appetite explicitly linked to strategy and performance targets?
- How does the board ensure that its risk governance extends to social, environmental, and ethical risks?
- Is integrated thinking embedded in the organization's planning and reporting processes?
- Does the audit and risk committee have sufficient independence and competence to discharge its mandate?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Quality of "apply and explain" disclosures relative to each of the 16 principles
- Alignment of risk governance with strategy formulation processes
- Independence and composition adequacy of board committees
- Integration of financial and non-financial risk in reporting
- Evidence of ethical culture embedded through governance practices
**Typical use cases:**
- Board governance design and evaluation in South African listed companies
- Governance maturity assessments in African and emerging market organizations
- Integrated reporting alignment under the International <IR> Framework
- Board committee terms of reference development
- Stakeholder engagement program design
**Strengths and limitations:** King IV's outcomes-based, "apply and explain" model is more substantive than checkbox compliance regimes, encouraging genuine governance reflection. Its integration of risk with ethics, strategy, and stakeholder relationships is more holistic than many Western governance codes. The framework's adoption of integrated thinking has influenced the global integrated reporting movement. Limitations include geographic concentration in South Africa, making international benchmarking difficult, and the "apply and explain" flexibility that allows superficial disclosures without meaningful governance substance.
**Related frameworks:** COSO ERM, ISO 31000, IIRC Integrated Reporting Framework, UK Corporate Governance Code, G20/OECD Principles of Corporate Governance

### Operational Risk Framework — Basel II/III Advanced Measurement Approach
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** Basel, banking, operational risk, AMA, regulatory capital, financial institutions
**How it works:** The Basel II/III operational risk framework, developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision, requires banks to identify, assess, monitor, and mitigate operational risk — defined as the risk of loss from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, systems, or external events. Under the Advanced Measurement Approach (AMA, now superseded by the Standardized Approach in Basel III final rules), banks could develop internal loss distribution models. The current Basel III Standardized Approach (SA) uses a Business Indicator Component multiplied by an Internal Loss Multiplier derived from historical loss data across seven event type categories (including internal fraud, external fraud, employment practices, clients and products, damage to physical assets, business disruption, and execution delivery process management) to calculate minimum regulatory capital. Beyond capital, the framework mandates robust operational risk management programs including risk and control self-assessments (RCSA), key risk indicators (KRIs), and scenario analysis.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Seven Basel II operational risk event types (internal fraud, external fraud, EPWS, CPBP, DPFA, BDSF, EDPM)
- Risk and Control Self-Assessment (RCSA)
- Key Risk Indicators (KRIs) and monitoring thresholds
- Internal loss data collection and categorization
- External loss data and scenario analysis
- Business environment and internal control factors (BEICF)
- Capital calculation (Standardized Approach under Basel III)
**Guiding questions:**
- Are operational loss events captured systematically and categorized consistently against the seven event types?
- Do RCSAs reflect actual operational risk exposures rather than theoretical assessments?
- Are KRIs calibrated to provide early warning of increasing risk levels?
- Does scenario analysis include tail-risk scenarios beyond historical experience?
- Is operational risk governance embedded in the three lines of defense model?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness and accuracy of internal loss data collection
- Credibility and coverage of scenario analysis inputs
- Effectiveness of KRI thresholds in triggering management action
- Capital adequacy calculation accuracy under the Standardized Approach
- Independent validation of operational risk models and data
**Typical use cases:**
- Bank regulatory capital calculation for operational risk
- Enterprise-wide operational risk program design for financial institutions
- Regulatory examination preparation for banking supervisors
- Merger integration risk assessment in banking acquisitions
- Insurance underwriting of bank operational risk exposures
**Strengths and limitations:** The Basel operational risk framework creates a common language and minimum standard for banks globally, enabling regulatory comparability. Its seven event-type taxonomy provides a structured classification system applicable beyond banking. However, the framework's regulatory-capital focus can distort risk management priorities toward capital optimization rather than loss prevention. Historical loss data requirements disadvantage banks with shorter operating histories, and low-frequency, high-severity risks are inherently difficult to model quantitatively.
**Related frameworks:** COSO ERM, ISO 31000, FAIR, Three Lines of Defense Model, IIA Standards, RCSA methodologies

### Three Lines of Defense (Three Lines Model)
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** governance, accountability, internal audit, risk ownership, IIA model
**How it works:** The Three Lines of Defense model — updated by the Institute of Internal Auditors (IIA) in 2020 as the "Three Lines Model" — clarifies how governance bodies, management, and assurance functions interact to achieve organizational objectives and manage risk effectively. The first line comprises operational management and process owners who own and manage risks day-to-day. The second line consists of risk, compliance, legal, and control functions that provide oversight, frameworks, and monitoring of first-line activities. The third line is internal audit, which provides independent, objective assurance to governing bodies about the effectiveness of governance, risk management, and control. The 2020 update refined the model to emphasize collaboration and value creation rather than purely defensive roles, and clarified that external audit and regulatory bodies sit outside the three lines while remaining relevant stakeholders.
**Dimensions / components:**
- First line: operational management, process owners, control activities
- Second line: risk and compliance functions, financial control, security, quality, legal
- Third line: internal audit (independent assurance to governing body)
- Governing body: board, audit committee (ultimate accountability)
- Senior management: operational responsibility, delegation to three lines
- External assurance: regulators, external auditors (outside the three lines)
**Guiding questions:**
- Are first-line managers genuinely accountable for identifying and managing risks within their processes?
- Do second-line functions add value through advisory and oversight roles rather than creating duplicate controls?
- Does internal audit maintain organizational independence from the activities it reviews?
- Is the governing body receiving sufficient, credible assurance from all three lines?
- How do the three lines coordinate to avoid gaps and overlaps in risk and control coverage?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Clarity of role delineation and accountability at each line
- Independence of internal audit from management influence
- Quality of assurance information reaching the governing body
- Absence of assurance gaps or excessive duplication across lines
- Evidence of effective coordination and information-sharing between lines
**Typical use cases:**
- Designing enterprise risk governance structures
- Clarifying accountability in regulated financial institutions and banks
- Internal audit charter development and positioning
- Second-line function design (risk, compliance, financial control)
- Board and audit committee governance structuring
**Strengths and limitations:** The Three Lines Model provides an intuitive, widely understood architecture for governance accountability that resonates with boards, auditors, and regulators. Its adoption by the IIA has made it a global reference standard. The 2020 update's emphasis on collaboration and value creation addresses earlier criticisms that the model encouraged siloed defensive thinking. Limitations include the tendency for organizations to apply the model mechanically without genuine accountability differentiation, and the difficulty of maintaining second-line independence when compliance functions are embedded within business units.
**Related frameworks:** COSO Internal Control, IIA Standards for Internal Audit, Basel Operational Risk, COSO ERM, King IV, ISO 31000

### Bow-Tie Risk Analysis
**Category:** Risk Management
**Tags:** barrier-based, hazard analysis, causal chain, process safety, risk visualization
**How it works:** Bow-Tie risk analysis is a visual, barrier-based risk assessment methodology that maps the causal pathways from hazard sources through a critical event (the "knot") to potential consequences, while overlaying the preventive and mitigating barriers that interrupt those pathways. The left side of the diagram represents threat pathways and preventive barriers (controls that stop a hazard becoming an event), while the right side represents consequence pathways and mitigating barriers (controls that reduce harm after an event occurs). Originally developed in the chemical and process safety industries, Bow-Tie has expanded into aviation, healthcare, financial services, and cybersecurity due to its intuitive visualization of risk logic. Each barrier is assigned an owner, enabling accountability assignment for control maintenance. Degradation factors — conditions that weaken barriers — are also mapped, creating a dynamic picture of risk management effectiveness.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Hazard sources (initiating conditions)
- Threat pathways (causes leading to the critical event)
- Critical event (the "top event" or loss of control)
- Preventive barriers (left-side controls)
- Consequence pathways (escalation paths from the critical event)
- Mitigating barriers (right-side controls)
- Degradation factors and recovery measures
**Guiding questions:**
- What hazards exist and which threats could cause loss of control?
- Are preventive barriers adequate, independent, and maintained to block each threat pathway?
- If the critical event occurs, do mitigating barriers limit consequences to acceptable levels?
- Who is accountable for maintaining each barrier, and is that accountability enforced?
- What degradation factors could weaken barriers, and are they monitored?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Completeness of threat and consequence pathway identification
- Independence and reliability of identified barriers
- Clarity of barrier ownership and maintenance accountability
- Identification and management of degradation factors
- Integration of Bow-Tie results into operational risk registers and safety cases
**Typical use cases:**
- Process safety risk assessment in oil, gas, and chemical facilities
- Aviation safety management system hazard analysis
- Healthcare clinical risk management for high-consequence procedures
- Cybersecurity scenario analysis for critical infrastructure
- Major project risk bow-tie workshops during design phases
**Strengths and limitations:** Bow-Tie's visual format makes complex risk logic accessible to operational personnel who are not risk specialists, facilitating broad organizational engagement. The explicit mapping of barriers and their owners creates accountability architecture that complements other frameworks. It excels at communicating systemic risk to regulators and safety authorities. However, Bow-Tie can become unwieldy for complex systems with many interacting threats, and the visual simplicity can mask important probabilistic nuances. It is most effective when combined with quantitative reliability data rather than used as a purely qualitative tool.
**Related frameworks:** FMEA, HAZOP, Swiss Cheese Model, ISO 31000, Fault Tree Analysis, Event Tree Analysis

### NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 (CSF 2.0)
**Category:** Cybersecurity Risk
**Tags:** NIST, cybersecurity, risk management, critical infrastructure, outcomes-based, voluntary
**How it works:** The NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0, released in February 2024, is a voluntary framework that helps organizations of all sizes and sectors manage and reduce cybersecurity risks by describing desired outcomes organized around six core functions ([NAVEX](https://www.navex.com/en-us/blog/article/risk-management-frameworks-for-organizations/)). The 2.0 update added a sixth function — Govern — to the original five (Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover), reflecting the recognition that cybersecurity risk management must be embedded in enterprise risk governance. CSF 2.0 is explicitly designed to be applicable beyond critical infrastructure to any organization and emphasizes supply chain cybersecurity risk management within the Govern function. The framework is implemented through Profiles (current and target states) and Tiers (maturity levels from Partial to Adaptive). Quick Start Guides, Community Profiles, and online resources support implementation across sectors and organization sizes.
**Dimensions / components:**
- Govern (GV): cybersecurity risk governance, strategy, supply chain risk
- Identify (ID): asset management, risk assessment, improvement
- Protect (PR): identity management, awareness training, data security, platform security
- Detect (DE): continuous monitoring, adverse event detection
- Respond (RS): incident management, analysis, mitigation, communication
- Recover (RC): incident recovery, communication during recovery
**Guiding questions:**
- Is cybersecurity risk management governed from the top of the organization with defined roles and risk tolerance?
- Does the organization have a comprehensive inventory of assets and associated risk assessments?
- Are protective controls commensurate with the sensitivity and criticality of protected assets?
- Can the organization detect adverse events in a timeframe consistent with its risk tolerance?
- Are response and recovery plans tested and updated based on lessons learned?
- Is supply chain cybersecurity risk managed within the Govern function?
**Evaluation criteria:**
- Alignment of current Tier with organizational risk tolerance and sector expectations
- Gap between current and target Profile across all six functions
- Coverage and accuracy of asset inventories supporting risk identification
- Effectiveness and timeliness of detection and response capabilities
- Governance integration: is CSF embedded in organizational risk governance?
**Typical use cases:**
- Enterprise cybersecurity program development and maturity assessment
- Board-level cybersecurity risk reporting using Tier and Profile language
- Critical infrastructure sector-specific profile development
- Third-party cybersecurity risk requirement specification
- Regulatory gap assessment for HIPAA, NERC CIP, or state cybersecurity laws
**Strengths and limitations:** CSF 2.0's addition of the Govern function addresses a historic gap by anchoring cybersecurity in enterprise governance rather than treating it as a purely technical function. The framework's broad voluntary adoption creates a common language across sectors and facilitates regulatory alignment. Its outcome-based, non-prescriptive design enables flexible implementation across diverse environments. Limitations include the lack of prescriptive guidance on how to achieve specific outcomes, making implementation quality highly variable. Organizations may use CSF for external communication without genuine internal adoption, and Tier assessment can be subjective without supporting metrics.
**Related frameworks:** NIST SP 800-53, ISO/IEC 27001, NIST RMF, CIS Controls, COBIT 2019, C2M2
