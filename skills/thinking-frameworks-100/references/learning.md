# Learning & Knowledge Frameworks

Frameworks for how we acquire, retain, and structure knowledge.

| #   | Framework                                   | Description                                                                                                                                                        | Origin                                                     |
| --- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| 119 | **Bloom's Taxonomy**                        | Six hierarchical levels of cognitive learning: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create. Guides instructional design and assessment.            | Benjamin Bloom (1956); revised Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) |
| 120 | **Feynman Technique**                       | To truly learn something: (1) choose a concept, (2) teach it to a child, (3) identify gaps, (4) simplify. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it. | Richard Feynman                                            |
| 121 | **Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle**      | Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualization → Active Experimentation. Effective learning requires all four stages.                   | David Kolb                                                 |
| 122 | **Zettelkasten Method**                     | A knowledge management system using atomic, interconnected notes. Each note captures one idea and links to others, forming an emergent network of knowledge.       | Niklas Luhmann                                             |
| 123 | **Spaced Repetition**                       | Review information at increasing intervals to exploit the spacing effect and transfer knowledge to long-term memory. Far more effective than massed study.         | Sebastian Leitner; Ebbinghaus                              |
| 124 | **Double-Loop Learning**                    | Single-loop: fix the problem. Double-loop: question the assumptions and mental models that created the problem. Leads to transformational learning.                | Chris Argyris                                              |
| 125 | **GROW Model**                              | A coaching framework: Goal (what do you want?) → Reality (where are you now?) → Options (what could you do?) → Will (what will you do?).                           | John Whitmore                                              |
| 126 | **Deliberate Practice**                     | Focused, effortful practice with clear goals, immediate feedback, and working at the edge of one's ability. The engine of expertise development.                   | Anders Ericsson                                            |
| 127 | **Learning Pyramid (Cone of Experience)**   | Retention rates vary by mode: lecture (5%), reading (10%), audiovisual (20%), demonstration (30%), discussion (50%), practice (75%), teaching others (90%).        | Edgar Dale (debated)                                       |
| 128 | **Fink's Taxonomy of Significant Learning** | Six interactive categories: Foundational Knowledge, Application, Integration, Human Dimension, Caring, Learning How to Learn. Goes beyond cognition to motivation. | L. Dee Fink                                                |
| 129 | **Shu-Ha-Ri**                               | Three stages of mastery: Shu (follow the form), Ha (break the form), Ri (transcend the form). Applies to any skill development.                                    | Japanese martial arts tradition                            |

## Key Sources

- _Taxonomy of Educational Objectives_ — Benjamin Bloom
- _Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise_ — Anders Ericsson
- _How to Take Smart Notes_ — Sönke Ahrens (Zettelkasten)
- _Coaching for Performance_ — John Whitmore
