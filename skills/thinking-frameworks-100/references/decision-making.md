# Decision-Making Frameworks

Structured approaches to making better choices under conditions of uncertainty, complexity, or time pressure.

| #   | Framework                              | Description                                                                                                                                                           | Origin                          |
| --- | -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| 13  | **Cynefin Framework**                  | Categorizes situations into five domains — Clear, Complicated, Complex, Chaotic, and Confused — each requiring a different decision approach.                         | Dave Snowden (1999)             |
| 14  | **OODA Loop**                          | Observe → Orient → Decide → Act. A rapid decision cycle designed for competitive, dynamic environments.                                                               | Col. John Boyd                  |
| 15  | **Eisenhower Matrix**                  | Prioritize tasks on two axes: Urgent vs. Important. Four quadrants: Do, Schedule, Delegate, Eliminate.                                                                | Dwight D. Eisenhower            |
| 16  | **Decision Matrix (Weighted Scoring)** | List options, define criteria, assign weights, and score each option against each criterion. The highest aggregate score wins.                                        | Operations research             |
| 17  | **Regret Minimization Framework**      | Project yourself to age 80 and ask: "Which decision will I regret _not_ making?" Optimizes for long-term fulfillment.                                                 | Jeff Bezos                      |
| 18  | **Pre-Mortem Analysis**                | Before executing a plan, imagine it has already failed. Ask "Why did it fail?" to surface hidden risks proactively.                                                   | Gary Klein                      |
| 19  | **Reversibility Test**                 | Distinguish between reversible and irreversible decisions. Take quick action on reversible ones; invest more deliberation in irreversible ones.                       | Jeff Bezos (Type 1 vs Type 2)   |
| 20  | **Vroom-Yetton-Jago Model**            | A decision tree that helps leaders determine the right level of team participation (autocratic → consultative → group) for each decision.                             | Vroom, Yetton, Jago             |
| 21  | **Kepner-Tregoe Method**               | Situation Appraisal → Problem Analysis → Decision Analysis → Potential Problem Analysis. A systematic approach to structured decision-making.                         | Charles Kepner, Benjamin Tregoe |
| 22  | **Cost-Benefit Analysis**              | Quantify and compare the expected costs and benefits of each option. Choose the option with the highest net benefit.                                                  | Jules Dupuit (19th c.)          |
| 23  | **Decision Tree Analysis**             | Map out decisions, chance events, and outcomes as a branching tree. Assign probabilities and values to calculate expected outcomes.                                   | Operations research             |
| 24  | **Scenario Planning**                  | Develop multiple plausible future scenarios (not predictions) and prepare strategies that are robust across several possible futures.                                 | Pierre Wack / Royal Dutch Shell |
| 25  | **10/10/10 Rule**                      | How will I feel about this decision 10 minutes from now? 10 months? 10 years? Balances short-term emotion with long-term perspective.                                 | Suzy Welch                      |
| 26  | **Satisficing vs. Maximizing**         | Satisficing = choosing the first option that meets your threshold ("good enough"). Maximizing = exhaustively searching for the _best_ option. Know which mode to use. | Herbert Simon                   |
| 27  | **Bounded Rationality**                | Humans don't have unlimited time, information, or cognitive capacity. Rational decisions are "bounded" by these limits, leading to heuristic shortcuts.               | Herbert Simon                   |
| 28  | **Two-Track Analysis**                 | Analyze every decision on two tracks: (1) rational/analytical factors and (2) psychological/behavioral factors that might distort judgment.                           | Charlie Munger                  |

## Key Sources

- _Thinking, Fast and Slow_ — Daniel Kahneman
- _Sources of Power_ — Gary Klein
- _The Fifth Discipline_ — Peter Senge
- Snowden & Boone (2007), "A Leader's Framework for Decision Making", _Harvard Business Review_
