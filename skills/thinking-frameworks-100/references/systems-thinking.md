# Systems Thinking

Frameworks for understanding interconnections, feedback loops, and emergent behavior in complex systems.

| #   | Framework                                        | Description                                                                                                                                                                     | Origin                              |
| --- | ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| 61  | **Systems Thinking (General)**                   | Study the whole rather than the parts. Understand how components interact, produce feedback, and create emergent behavior that individual analysis misses.                      | Ludwig von Bertalanffy              |
| 62  | **Feedback Loops**                               | Reinforcing loops amplify change (positive feedback), while balancing loops resist change (negative feedback). Most real-world dynamics are driven by interplay of both.        | Systems dynamics                    |
| 63  | **Stock and Flow Diagrams**                      | Model systems as stocks (accumulated quantities) and flows (rates of change). Understanding stocks and flows prevents common misperceptions of system behavior.                 | Jay Forrester                       |
| 64  | **Iceberg Model**                                | Events are the visible tip. Below the surface: patterns of behavior → underlying structures → mental models. Address the deeper levels for lasting change.                      | Systems thinking tradition          |
| 65  | **Causal Loop Diagrams**                         | Map variables and their causal connections with arrows showing polarity (+/−). Reveals hidden reinforcing and balancing loops driving system behavior.                          | Peter Senge / System dynamics       |
| 66  | **Leverage Points**                              | Not all interventions are equal. Donella Meadows identified 12 places to intervene in a system, from least to most powerful (e.g., changing parameters vs. changing paradigms). | Donella Meadows                     |
| 67  | **The Fifth Discipline**                         | Organizations that learn fastest win. Five disciplines: Personal Mastery, Mental Models, Shared Vision, Team Learning, and Systems Thinking (the integrative "fifth").          | Peter Senge                         |
| 68  | **Complexity Theory / Complex Adaptive Systems** | In complex systems, agents interact locally following simple rules, producing emergent, unpredictable macro-behavior. Cannot be controlled, only influenced.                    | Santa Fe Institute; Stuart Kauffman |
| 69  | **Network Effects**                              | The value of a product or service increases as more people use it. Creates winner-take-all dynamics and lock-in.                                                                | Robert Metcalfe; economics          |
| 70  | **Emergence**                                    | Complex patterns and behaviors arise from simple interactions among components. The whole is more than the sum of its parts.                                                    | Complexity science                  |
| 71  | **Antifragility**                                | Some systems don't just withstand stress — they improve from it. Antifragile > Resilient > Robust > Fragile. Design systems that benefit from volatility.                       | Nassim Taleb                        |

## Key Sources

- _Thinking in Systems_ — Donella Meadows
- _Antifragile_ — Nassim Taleb
