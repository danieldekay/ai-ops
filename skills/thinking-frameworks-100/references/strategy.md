# Strategic Thinking & Business Strategy

Frameworks for analyzing competitive positions, market dynamics, and strategic options.

| #   | Framework                            | Description                                                                                                                                                              | Origin                                  |
| --- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| 41  | **SWOT Analysis**                    | Assess Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. A simple 2×2 matrix for strategic position assessment.                                                         | Albert Humphrey (1960s)                 |
| 42  | **Porter's Five Forces**             | Analyze industry attractiveness through: (1) competitive rivalry, (2) threat of new entrants, (3) threat of substitutes, (4) supplier power, (5) buyer power.            | Michael Porter                          |
| 43  | **BCG Growth-Share Matrix**          | Classify business units as Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, or Dogs based on market growth rate and relative market share.                                              | Boston Consulting Group                 |
| 44  | **Ansoff Growth Matrix**             | Four growth strategies: Market Penetration, Product Development, Market Development, Diversification — ordered by increasing risk.                                       | Igor Ansoff                             |
| 45  | **Blue Ocean Strategy**              | Instead of competing in "red oceans" (existing markets), create "blue oceans" — uncontested market space where competition is irrelevant.                                | W. Chan Kim, Renée Mauborgne            |
| 46  | **McKinsey 7S Framework**            | Align seven organizational elements: Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared Values, Style, Staff, Skills.                                                                  | Tom Peters, Robert Waterman (McKinsey)  |
| 47  | **Value Chain Analysis**             | Decompose a firm's activities into primary (production, marketing, delivery) and support (HR, tech, procurement) to identify where value is created.                     | Michael Porter                          |
| 48  | **Business Model Canvas**            | A one-page visual template with 9 building blocks: Value Proposition, Customer Segments, Channels, Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Activities, Partners, Cost Structure. | Alexander Osterwalder                   |
| 49  | **GE-McKinsey Nine-Box Matrix**      | Evaluate business units on Industry Attractiveness × Competitive Strength (High/Medium/Low). Guides portfolio investment.                                                | GE & McKinsey                           |
| 50  | **Three Horizons Model**             | Plan growth across three time horizons: H1 (core business today), H2 (emerging opportunities), H3 (exploratory/visionary ventures).                                      | McKinsey                                |
| 51  | **PESTLE Analysis**                  | Scan the macro-environment: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, and Environmental factors.                                                                | Francis Aguilar (PEST, 1967)            |
| 52  | **Balanced Scorecard**               | Measure performance across four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Processes, and Learning & Growth. Links strategy to operations.                              | Kaplan & Norton                         |
| 53  | **OKRs (Objectives & Key Results)**  | Set ambitious Objectives and measure progress with 3-5 measurable Key Results per objective. Quarterly cycles create focus and alignment.                                | Andy Grove (Intel); Larry Page (Google) |
| 54  | **Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)**           | Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to accomplish specific jobs. Understand the functional, emotional, and social jobs.                                      | Clayton Christensen                     |
| 55  | **Wardley Mapping**                  | Map your value chain on an evolution axis (Genesis → Custom → Product → Commodity) to visualize strategic landscape and anticipate movement.                             | Simon Wardley                           |
| 56  | **Hoshin Kanri (Policy Deployment)** | Cascades breakthrough objectives from top leadership down to execution teams, ensuring alignment at every level.                                                         | Japanese management (Toyota)            |
| 57  | **TOWS Matrix**                      | Extension of SWOT that explicitly generates strategies: SO (leverage strengths for opportunities), WO, ST, WT. More action-oriented.                                     | Heinz Weihrich                          |
| 58  | **Strategy Canvas**                  | Visual diagram plotting your offering vs. competitors across key factors. Identifies where you can differentiate. Central to Blue Ocean Strategy.                        | Kim & Mauborgne                         |
| 59  | **Core Competencies**                | Identify the unique combination of skills, technologies, and knowledge that gives competitive advantage and is hard to replicate.                                        | Prahalad & Hamel                        |
| 60  | **Greiner Growth Model**             | Organizations evolve through 5 phases of growth, each ending in a crisis: creativity → direction → delegation → coordination → collaboration.                            | Larry Greiner                           |

## Key Sources

- _Competitive Strategy_ / _Competitive Advantage_ — Michael Porter
- _Blue Ocean Strategy_ — W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne
- _Business Model Generation_ — Alexander Osterwalder
- _Wardley Maps_ — Simon Wardley (free book)
- _Measure What Matters_ — John Doerr (OKRs)
