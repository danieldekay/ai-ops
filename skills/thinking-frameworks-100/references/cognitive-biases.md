# Cognitive Biases & Psychological Models

Understanding the systematic errors in human thinking — and how to counteract them.

| #   | Framework                        | Description                                                                                                                                               | Origin                               |
| --- | -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| 105 | **Confirmation Bias**            | We naturally seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms our existing beliefs. Counter: actively seek disconfirming evidence.                 | Peter Wason                          |
| 106 | **Anchoring Effect**             | The first piece of information we encounter disproportionately influences subsequent judgments. Be wary of initial anchors in negotiations and estimates. | Tversky & Kahneman                   |
| 107 | **Availability Heuristic**       | We overweight information that is easily recalled (recent, vivid, emotional). This skews risk assessment and probability estimates.                       | Tversky & Kahneman                   |
| 108 | **Sunk Cost Fallacy**            | Continuing an activity because of previously invested resources (time, money) rather than future value. Rational decisions ignore sunk costs.             | Behavioral economics                 |
| 109 | **Loss Aversion**                | Losses feel roughly 2× as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable. Drives risk aversion and status quo bias.                                         | Kahneman & Tversky (Prospect Theory) |
| 110 | **Dunning-Kruger Effect**        | Those with low ability in a domain overestimate their competence; experts often underestimate theirs.                                                     | David Dunning, Justin Kruger         |
| 111 | **System 1 / System 2 Thinking** | System 1 is fast, intuitive, and error-prone. System 2 is slow, deliberate, and effortful. Know when to engage each — and when System 1 is fooling you.   | Daniel Kahneman                      |
| 112 | **Framing Effect**               | How information is presented (positively vs. negatively framed) dramatically changes decisions, even when the underlying facts are identical.             | Tversky & Kahneman                   |
| 113 | **Groupthink**                   | Desire for conformity in a group leads to irrational decision-making. Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, self-censorship, pressure on dissenters.     | Irving Janis                         |
| 114 | **Lollapalooza Effect**          | When multiple cognitive biases or tendencies operate in the same direction simultaneously, producing extreme, outsized outcomes.                          | Charlie Munger                       |
| 115 | **Hyperbolic Discounting**       | We disproportionately prefer smaller rewards now over larger rewards later. The closer the immediate reward, the sharper the discounting.                 | Behavioral economics                 |
| 116 | **Social Proof**                 | In uncertain situations, we look at what others are doing and follow suit. Powerful driver of behavior — and manipulation.                                | Robert Cialdini                      |
| 117 | **Recency Bias**                 | We give greater weight to recent events and information, underweighting historical base rates.                                                            | Behavioral finance                   |
| 118 | **Hindsight Bias**               | After an event occurs, we believe we "knew it all along." Distorts our ability to learn from outcomes.                                                    | Baruch Fischhoff                     |

## Key Sources

- _Thinking, Fast and Slow_ — Daniel Kahneman
- _Influence_ — Robert Cialdini
- _Poor Charlie's Almanack_ — Charles Munger (25 standard causes of human misjudgment)
- _Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases_ — Tversky & Kahneman
