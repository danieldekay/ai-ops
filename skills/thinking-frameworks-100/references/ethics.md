# Ethics & Philosophical Frameworks

Frameworks for moral reasoning, values alignment, and principled decision-making.

| #   | Framework                      | Description                                                                                                                                                                      | Origin                           |
| --- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| 174 | **Utilitarianism**             | The right action is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Evaluate actions by their consequences and aggregate well-being.                            | Jeremy Bentham, John Stuart Mill |
| 175 | **Veil of Ignorance**          | Design a just society as if you didn't know your position within it (rich/poor, healthy/sick, majority/minority). Ensures decisions are fair and unbiased.                       | John Rawls                       |
| 176 | **Categorical Imperative**     | Act only according to rules that you could will to be universal laws. "What if everyone did this?" — if the answer is bad, don't do it.                                          | Immanuel Kant                    |
| 177 | **Effective Altruism**         | Use evidence and reason to determine the most effective ways to do good. Prioritize causes that are Important, Neglected, and Tractable (INT framework).                         | William MacAskill, Peter Singer  |
| 178 | **Stoic Dichotomy of Control** | Focus only on what you can control (your actions, reactions, judgments). Accept what you cannot control (others' behavior, external events). The foundation of Stoic resilience. | Epictetus                        |
| 179 | **Ikigai**                     | The intersection of four circles: What you love, What you're good at, What the world needs, What you can be paid for. Finding all four = purpose.                                | Japanese philosophy              |
| 180 | **Trolley Problem**            | A thought experiment that forces a choice between killing one person to save five. Reveals differences between consequentialist and deontological moral intuitions.              | Philippa Foot                    |

## Key Sources
